The S&P 500 fell just 0.5% yesterday. If that's all you read, you missed the story completely.

A ceasefire collapsed. A naval blockade went live in the Strait of Hormuz. A blue-chip earnings guide missed by a single percentage point. And the index's reaction was a shrug. That's not resilience. That's a tape that hasn't caught up to a stagflation setup that just walked through the door.

Start with bonds. The 10-year yield rose 2.4 basis points to 4.569% while equities sold off. In a normal risk-off session, yields fall as capital rushes to safety. They didn't. That's the bond market telling you this isn't a growth scare, it's an inflation problem, and oil is the transmission mechanism.

Then gold. It held $4,001 through the entire session, calm rather than spiking. No panic bid yet. That's not boring, that's a market with room left to run if the Hormuz standoff escalates further, and right now it's escalating by the day.

And oil. WTI rose 1.2% to $79.86 on the day a naval blockade actually went live and Iran declared the strait a red line. For an event that threatens 20% of the world's seaborne crude, that is a modest move. The market is still pricing a resolution. It may not get one this weekend.

The corporate proof point that makes this concrete: Netflix guided Q3 revenue to $12.86 billion against a $13.00 billion consensus, a miss of roughly 1%, and the stock dropped 9% after hours. The market isn't punishing the miss. It's punishing the multiple. That's a valuation story, and it's spreading across the ad-tech and streaming complex this morning.

The second-order story nobody's covering: tanker war-risk insurance through the Strait of Hormuz. Every day the blockade holds without resolution, the premium shippers pay to move crude through that corridor compounds quietly, off-exchange, long before it ever shows up in a futures print. That cost gets passed through eventually. It always does.

So which market has this right? Equities, treating a live naval blockade and a collapsed ceasefire as a half-point pullback? Or bonds, gold, and crude, all quietly pricing a sustained, supply-driven inflation problem?

My money's on the dissenters. A 0.5% equity drawdown is not proportionate to what's actually live in the Gulf right now.
