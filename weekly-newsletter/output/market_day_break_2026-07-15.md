*Daily Edition · Market intelligence at the open*

## The Brief

June CPI printed -0.4% MoM and +3.5% YoY: the market rallied, then Fed Chair Kevin Warsh took the mic and erased it. The Nasdaq closed down 1.55%, the 10-year yield rose 4bps to 4.609%, and gold slipped. That is not what relief looks like. The easy money is gone. Today you're navigating a Warsh-capped rate backdrop, an [AI](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/AI) bid with fresh fuel from [ASML](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/ASML), and a Strait of Hormuz oil shock with 48 hours before Iran retaliates or blinks.

The **S&P 500 fell 0.79% to 7,515.34**, the **Dow slipped 0.26% to 52,498.64**, and the **Nasdaq led the losses, down 1.55% to 25,873.18**. That is not a market shrugging off a hot CPI beat, that's the AI-heavy index eating the biggest hit of the four majors. WTI crude surged to **$80.16, up 1.0%**, the 10-year yield rose 4 basis points to **4.609%**, and gold slipped 0.6% to **$4,037.70** even as the dollar index fell to 101.02, a currency apparently more convinced inflation is cooling than the bond market is. The Russell 2000 dropped a comparatively mild 0.83%, for once small caps weren't the ones doing the bleeding.

The June CPI print of **-0.4% MoM and +3.5% YoY** beat the 3.8% consensus forecast and ignited the initial rally. But Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's congressional testimony poured cold water on rate-cut hopes, reiterating zero tolerance for persistent inflation while markets still price a 70-73% chance of a September hike. Think of it this way: the CPI handed traders a key, but Warsh changed the lock. The **Strait of Hormuz standoff** layered a separate crude shock on top, with the reinstated U.S. naval blockade on Iranian ports driving energy prices higher and lifting maritime insurance costs globally. Those two forces, a cooler CPI and a hot geopolitical oil bid, are pulling the market in opposite directions simultaneously.

## What it means for you

For ETF investors, the tension is the story: [ASML](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/ASML)'s 4% surge on a raised sales forecast and Goldman/JPMorgan getting flagged as [AI](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/AI) winners argue for continued semiconductor and tech exposure ([SOXX](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/SOXX), [QQQ](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/QQQ), [XLK](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/XLK)), yet the Nasdaq itself closed down 1.55%, the session's worst performer. The [AI](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/AI) trade is winning on single names while the index it lives in gets sold. The crude move above $80 makes the **energy trade worth revisiting** ([XLE](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/XLE), [USO](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/USO)), particularly if the Hormuz standoff escalates further in the next 48-hour window Reuters flagged for potential Iranian retaliation. [TLT](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/TLT) holders face a difficult setup: cooler inflation should lift bonds, but Warsh's hawkish floor and the September hike probability keeps yields sticky at these levels. The Russell ([IWM](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/IWM))'s 0.83% decline was actually the mildest of the four majors: small caps didn't lead the bleeding this time, the Nasdaq did.

Going into today, S&P futures are up a thin 0.1% to 7,599, Nasdaq futures lead at +0.4%, and KOSPI's 7% surge is the standout APAC signal, driven by the global tech and [AI](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/AI) relief trade, though a single-session pop that size in Seoul usually ends in profit-taking, not a new regime. The swing factor this morning is **Durable Goods Orders at 8:30 AM ET**: a strong print validates economic resilience and supports equities, but a weak number combined with sticky yields risks a reversal in the tech-led bounce. Watch whether the 10-year holds below 4.65% through the morning session.
