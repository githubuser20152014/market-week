America conducted military strikes against Iranian missile sites near the Strait of Hormuz yesterday. Oil fell 3.4%. This is what happens when markets price the optimistic scenario: traders saw one LNG tanker transit the Strait for the first time since February and treated it like a ceasefire, ignoring the retaliation vow that arrived overnight.

Here's what the cross-asset moves are telling you:

Gold held $4,524 despite live US airstrikes and a risk-on equity rally. That's not a flight-to-safety bid - that's buyers defending a floor. If gold refuses to move on military action, the market isn't pricing systemic risk yet.

Bonds didn't budge. The 10Y slipped to 4.558% on a mild risk-on tone. Fed Chair Warsh has a January 2027 hike baked in, and that's a ceiling on any duration rally. No safe-haven rotation in sight.

Crude is the only asset that moved decisively: WTI down 3.36% to $93.35. The supply premium is deflating. That matters because energy stocks still price that premium in. XLE is heading for a structural headwind as Hormuz reopening probabilities rise.

The real signal came from small caps: Russell 2000 up 0.91% while geopolitical risk is active. That's a vote of confidence in domestic credit and growth, not a flight to safety. Domestic growth rotation is the trade, assuming Iran doesn't follow through on retaliation.

Which market got it right? The one pricing peace, or the one that saw the retaliation vow? We find out at market open when Iran headline risk actually prices into the session.
