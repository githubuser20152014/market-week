Market IQ — Economic Concepts, Plain & Simple

No economics degree required. Each card explains one concept — what it is, why it matters, how often it's published, and what the recent trend means for your money.

Macro Indicators
GDP

Gross Domestic Product — the total value of all goods and services produced in a country. Think of it as the economy's report card: when it's growing, jobs and incomes tend to rise.

Published: Quarterly ↓ Slowing
Inflation
CPI

Consumer Price Index — measures how much everyday prices (food, rent, gas) have changed. When CPI rises fast, your money buys less. The Fed watches this very closely.

Published: Monthly ↑ Elevated
Central Banks
Fed Funds Rate

The interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans. When the Fed raises it, borrowing gets more expensive for everyone — mortgages, car loans, credit cards.

Set: 8× per year → Hold
Employment
Non-Farm Payrolls

How many jobs were added (or lost) last month, excluding farms. It's the single most market-moving data release of the month — even a small miss can move stocks sharply.

Published: Monthly ↑ Strong
Inflation
PCE

Personal Consumption Expenditures — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge. It's broader than CPI and adjusts for changing consumer habits. When it's hot, rate cuts get delayed.

Published: Monthly ↑ Above Target
Markets
Yield Curve

The difference in interest rates between short and long-term government bonds. When it "inverts" (short rates higher than long), it has historically preceded recessions.

Real-time → Normalizing
