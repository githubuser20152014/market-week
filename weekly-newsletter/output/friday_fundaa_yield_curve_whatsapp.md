There's a chart that has predicted every US recession since the 1970s. Economists watch it obsessively. Most people have never heard of it.

It's called the *yield curve*.

Quick version: when you buy a US government bond, you earn interest. Short-term bonds (2 years) pay one rate. Long-term bonds (10 years) pay a higher rate — more time, more risk, more reward. Plot them on a chart and you get a line that normally slopes upward.

Sometimes the line flips. Short-term rates go *higher* than long-term rates. That's called an inversion — and it means bond investors are so worried about the future they're piling into long bonds for safety, pushing long yields down while short rates stay high.

The 2-year/10-year spread inverted in 2022. Stayed inverted for two years — the longest on record.

The twist: the recession usually doesn't arrive *during* the inversion. It arrives *after* it un-inverts — when the Fed is already cutting rates because the slowdown has started.

The curve has mostly un-inverted over the past few months 👀

Not a guarantee of anything. But the chart with the best track record is now in the phase that historically comes before downturns. Worth knowing.
