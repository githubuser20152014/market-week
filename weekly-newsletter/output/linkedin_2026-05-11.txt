The Dow added 0.02% on Friday. Two basis points of conviction, wrapped in 24 hours of geopolitical collapse. The financial media called it a "broad equity rally." The honest read: one index rallied on tech, one index barely breathed, and the story the market is telling you about "risk-on" is narrower than the headline suggests.

Here's why you shouldn't trust the headline:

BONDS didn't buy it. The 10-year Treasury yield fell only 2.8 basis points to 4.364% on a day when Iran peace talks collapsed, Trump rejected the latest offer, and Netanyahu declared the war "not over." In a genuine risk-on session, bonds sell off as money rotates into equities. That didn't happen. The bond market is pricing something the equity headline isn't: energy-driven inflation keeping the Fed sidelined, and a geopolitical premium with no near-term resolution.

GOLD didn't buy it either. Gold dropped 0.89% to $4,678 - its second consecutive down day from last week's $4,727 peak. A war escalation day where gold falls is not a contradiction. It is a signal: the market is rotating from macro-panic gold into the energy disruption itself. The trade is not "buy safety." The trade is "own the supply shock directly."

CRUDE told the real story. WTI surged 2.33% to $97.64, with futures holding steady overnight at $97.66. This is the market's honest vote. Not risk-on. Energy disruption as a structural, investable theme.

The corporate proof point: Saudi Aramco posted a 26% Q1 profit surge. Their pipeline runs around the Strait of Hormuz, so every tanker rerouted away from the Strait is direct revenue. The IEA called the Hormuz disruption "the biggest supply shock in history." Aramco called it a very good quarter. When the supply shock is a revenue event for the infrastructure positioned around it, that is not a temporary price spike - that is a structural trade.

The second-order story nobody is covering: Spirit Airlines ceased operations Friday, citing jet fuel costs as the final blow. This is the first major corporate casualty of the energy shock economy, and it will not be the last. The low-cost carrier model was built on $60 oil. At $97 WTI, the margin math doesn't work. Spirit is the leading indicator, not an isolated incident. Every fuel-intensive, margin-thin business is looking at the same math right now.

Which market got it right? The Dow's 0.02% tells you the old economy isn't celebrating. Nasdaq's 1.71% tells you tech has its own bid, mostly disconnected from energy costs. WTI at $97.64 tells you the real story: the Hormuz closure is structural, Aramco's profit surge is structural, and Spirit's exit is structural. The rotation from gold to energy is not noise. It is the market repricing who wins and who loses in a world where the Strait of Hormuz is a parking lot.
