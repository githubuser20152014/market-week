$79.72 Oil, a Naval Blockade, and a Market That Isn't Bothered Yet.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

Iran just declared a red line on the Strait of Hormuz. The U.S. Navy has been running a blockade for five straight nights. And WTI crude closed at $79.72, up a whopping 0.15%, like a war for the world's most important shipping lane is a Tuesday afternoon inconvenience.

Meanwhile the S&P 500 parked half a percent from an all-time high on cooling inflation data, because nothing says confidence like ignoring a war. KOSPI didn't get that memo, down 6.4% overnight after the Bank of Korea yanked rates higher, and Nasdaq futures are following its lead, off 0.8% pre-market.

Somewhere between $79.72 oil and a 6.4% crash in Seoul, one of these markets is about to be very wrong.

Here's my read:

The engine behind yesterday's rally was cooler-than-expected PPI data, riding on the back of Tuesday's CPI surprise of 3.5% year-on-year against a 3.8% forecast, plus a clean earnings beat from the big banks. Think of the bond market as a smoke detector: two soft inflation prints in a row and it stopped beeping, so equities took that as the all-clear to push toward record highs. The S&P 500 gained 0.38% to 7,572, the Nasdaq led at +0.62% to 26,269, and the 10-year yield dropped 4 basis points to 4.545%, a rounding error dressed up as a rally.

Fed Chair Warsh didn't get the same signal. He kept his hawkish posture intact, insisted the Fed answers to no one while confirming he meets often with the Trump administration, and traders still price 70-73% odds of a September hike. The rally has a chaperone standing in the corner. Add in TSMC's $100 billion Arizona investment after a 77% profit surge, and you've got a market that wants to celebrate three things at once while pretending the Strait of Hormuz isn't the fourth thing sitting right there.

The One Trade: Long $XLE

Iran's Hormuz red line, now backed by five nights of U.S. airstrikes and an active naval blockade, is a supply disruption that WTI at $79.72 has not fully priced. Energy is the one sector today where the geopolitical catalyst and the technical setup actually line up.

Confirms: WTI crude holds above $79.50 through 10 AM ET and XLE opens above its 5-day moving average near $95.50, confirming buyers aren't fading the Hormuz headline.

Kill switch: Iran signals a diplomatic off-ramp, or U.S. officials walk back strike threats before the open, sending WTI below $78.50 and collapsing the geopolitical premium in energy names fast.

What else I'm watching

  • Semiconductors: hold domestic SOXX on TSMC's Arizona commitment, but stay away from Korea-heavy EM tech until the won stabilizes. A 6.4% overnight crash isn't a buy-the-dip signal, it's a warning label.
  • Financials: XLF is supported by strong bank earnings out of BlackRock and Morgan Stanley. United's $6 billion fuel-cost warning is the margin story nobody's pricing into the broader industrial complex yet.
  • Duration: TLT can extend its bounce if today holds without a hawkish Fed catalyst, but don't you dare treat this as conviction. Yields back above 4.58% and the trade flips overnight.
  • Nasdaq/QQQ: the most vulnerable major index at the open. Warsh's hawkish independence signal plus Iran's energy threat plus zero relief catalysts on today's calendar is not a combination tech wants to see.
  • Gold: flat at $4,044, refusing to pick a side. That's not boring, that's a market that can't decide whether inflation is dead or the Middle East is about to make it very much alive again.
