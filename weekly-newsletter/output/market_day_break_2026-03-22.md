## The Brief

The dominant mood is geopolitical risk repricing — the U.S.-Iran war is now the single controlling variable across every asset class, and markets are recalibrating in real time. The most striking cross-asset signal is the violent reversal in crude oil: WTI closed above $100 yesterday but is cratering nearly 6% overnight on reports the White House may release sanctioned Iranian crude to cap prices, while gold is also selling off sharply — suggesting this isn't a clean safe-haven bid but a chaotic, policy-headline-driven environment where nothing is anchored. Equity futures are modestly lower but nowhere near the damage in APAC, meaning the U.S. open will be fragile and headline-dependent.

U.S. equities closed in the red across the board Tuesday, with the Dow leading losses at -0.44% (46,021), followed by the Russell 2000 (-0.31%, 2,470), the S&P 500 (-0.27%, 6,606), and the Nasdaq (-0.21%, 22,105). Crude oil surged 1.59% to close at $100.22 — its psychological breakout level — while the 10-year Treasury yield ticked up 4 basis points to 4.32%, the USD Index firmed modestly to 99.45, and gold pulled back 0.64% to $4,830 after its recent historic run.

The story is the Iran war and its commodity shock transmission. Oil crossing $100 is the equivalent of a tax hike on every consumer and business simultaneously — it compresses margins, stokes inflation expectations, and puts the Fed in an impossible position. The 10-year yield rising alongside oil is the bond market pricing that stagflationary cocktail: growth gets hit, but inflation stays sticky, so the Fed can't cut to the rescue. Small caps ([IWM](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/IWM)) got hit hardest because they carry more floating-rate debt and have less pricing power to absorb an energy cost surge — exactly the dynamic you'd expect when crude spikes.

For investors, the regime has shifted. Energy ([XLE](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/XLE)) was the clear winner yesterday and remains structurally bid as long as the Iran conflict persists, but the overnight reversal in crude warns that this trade is now hostage to Trump's policy decisions on Iranian supply releases — it could gap violently in either direction. Defensives like utilities ([XLU](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/XLU)) and consumer staples ([XLP](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/XLP)) deserve a closer look as stagflation hedges, while long-duration bonds ([TLT](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/TLT)) are a trap here — yields rising with oil means duration gets punished on both the inflation and supply side. Gold ([GLD](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/GLD)) pulling back despite a live geopolitical war is a warning sign: either positioning was too crowded or the dollar is reasserting, and you need to watch that relationship carefully before adding.

Going into today's open, S&P futures are off ~0.18% and Dow futures -0.40%, but the real story is what happened overnight: APAC was a bloodbath — Nikkei -3.38%, KOSPI -2.73%, Nifty -3.26%, Hang Seng -2.02% — all pricing the Iran war escalation risk that the U.S. session hasn't fully absorbed yet. European markets are another 1.5-2% lower in early trade. WTI is now at $94.32 in the pre-market — a nearly $6 collapse from yesterday's close — which should mechanically relieve some inflation fear but also signals policy chaos. The swing factor today is any headline on Iranian crude sanctions waivers or military escalation; New Home Sales at 10 AM ET is a distant secondary concern. Watch the $95 level in crude and 4.35% on the 10-year as the lines in the sand.

- Avoid adding [TLT](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/TLT) or any long-duration fixed income here — with the 10-year at 4.32% and oil still elevated, the stagflation dynamic punishes bonds from both ends; short-duration alternatives ([SGOV](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/SGOV), [BIL](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/BIL)) are the right place to park defensive capital until the rate path clarifies.

- Watch [GLD](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/GLD) closely before adding: gold selling off in an active war with a weakening dollar is anomalous and may signal crowded positioning being unwound — wait for stabilization above the $4,800 spot level before treating this dip as a buy; a close below $4,750 would be a technical red flag requiring reassessment of the entire safe-haven thesis.
- If APAC weakness (-2% to -3%) bleeds into the U.S. open and the S&P tests the 6,550-6,580 zone, consider a tactical hedge via [SQQQ](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/SQQQ) or buying puts on [QQQ](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/QQQ) rather than selling outright — the geopolitical situation can flip on a single headline and you don't want to be structurally short into a ceasefire rumor.
