The Soft Landing Had a Great Run. 3.8% CPI Just Filed the Death Certificate.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

April CPI printed 3.8% YoY and 0.6% MoM. Hot. The energy component alone was up 17.9%, because the Strait of Hormuz is closed and someone decided a toll booth covering 20% of global oil supply was optional. The Fed's 2026 rate cut schedule has been relocated to 2027. Pack light.

Here is the tell: Gold closed at $4,704 yesterday. The dollar also firmed. Yields also rose to 4.463%. Gold is supposed to fall when the dollar strengthens and yields climb. It did not. When gold breaks its inverse relationship with both at the same time, institutional money is sending a message. The message is: this is not a one-day CPI blip. This is a regime.

Pre-market, Nasdaq futures are up 0.93% on the Trump-Xi Beijing summit and Jensen Huang's appearance as what can only be described as the world's most valuable diplomat. The market wants to believe the summit buys a tech reprieve. The CPI print does not care about the summit. The CPI print reports to the Strait of Hormuz.

Here's my read:

The stagflation fingerprint is complete. Nasdaq -0.71% to 26,088, because rate-sensitive growth stocks and a 4.463% 10-year yield are a bad combination. Russell 2000 -0.97% to 2,842: small-caps are the canaries of domestic credit, and right now they are canaries with a cough. The Dow gained 0.11% to 49,760, because energy and industrials love $101 crude and don't apologize for it. Gold at $4,704 with the dollar at 98.49 and yields at 4.463% is the clearest possible signal that institutional buyers are not waiting for the next CPI print. They are positioned. WTI at $101.59 pulled back from intraday Brent highs near $106, but the structural bid stays intact as long as the Strait does not reopen. The Fed is parked until 2027. This is higher-for-longer, but the reason is a burning toll booth, not a hot jobs market, and that distinction matters enormously for where you want to be.

The One Trade: Long $GLD

Gold closed at $4,704 while both the dollar and yields rose on the CPI beat, breaking the normal inverse relationship. That is not a hedge trade. That is institutional demand for a stagflation and geopolitical regime position that has nothing to do with the dollar-yield seesaw. When gold decouples from both, the floor is structural, not tactical.

Confirms: GLD holds above $4,690 through the first hour of trading and does not sell off into the Nasdaq futures-led open. If gold is bid even as tech bounces, the stagflation/geopolitical floor is real.

Kill switch: GLD breaks below $4,660 on a concrete Strait of Hormuz ceasefire or a major de-escalation signal from the Trump-Xi summit. That removes both the geopolitical and inflation legs simultaneously. Get out fast.

What else I'm watching

  • $XLE - don't you dare sell it. The Hormuz closure shows no sign of resolution. WTI above $100 is the structural floor while Brent threatens $106-$150. Energy is the only sector with a direct fundamental tailwind from the shock driving everything else lower. Add on dips.
  • $QQQ - trim into the bounce. Nasdaq futures up 0.93% on the Beijing summit headline is a sentiment trade, not a yield trade. The 10-year at 4.463% and CPI at 3.8% are the underlying reality. If the summit produces no concrete chip deal by midday, the gap up fades fast.
  • $TLT - stay away from it. Yields rising on energy-driven inflation is not the same as yields rising on growth fears. TLT's duration is a liability, not a cushion, until the Strait reopens and energy CPI rolls over. The Fed is on hold into 2027.
  • $SMH - the trade nobody is talking about. KOSPI surged 2.63% overnight on semiconductor optimism. Watch whether SMH holds pre-market gains through the first hour. If it reverses hard before noon, the Beijing summit optimism is noise and you want to be flat or short big-cap tech into the close.
  • Durable Goods at 8:30 AM ET. A weak print confirms capex is buckling under energy cost pressure and validates the stagflation trade. A strong print muddies the picture.
