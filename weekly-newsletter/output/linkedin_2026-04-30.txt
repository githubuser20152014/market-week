The S&P 500 closed essentially flat yesterday. Here's why that's the most misleading number in markets.

The index closed at 7,135. Down 0.04%. The kind of number that makes you think nothing happened. Everything happened.

---

The bond market's message: The 10-year Treasury yield climbed 6.4 basis points to 4.42%. Bonds should have rallied during an equity selloff. They didn't. That means institutional money isn't rotating into Treasuries as a safe haven - it's pricing elevated inflation as a structural outcome, not a temporary shock. The Fed held at 3.5%-3.75% yesterday with its highest dissent vote since 1992. Half the committee wants to hike. The other half is terrified of slowing growth. Bonds read that as one thing: the Fed is stuck.

Gold's message: Gold closed up 2% to $4,637 while the dollar softened and yields climbed. That combination almost never holds in normal markets. When yields rise and gold also rises, the inflation premium is overwhelming the rate premium. Buyers aren't trading gold as a currency hedge. They're buying insurance against something the Fed won't name.

Crude's message: WTI at $106.88, more than double its pre-conflict baseline. Brent hit $126 intraday. The World Bank now says average energy prices could climb 25% this year. The UAE's OPEC exit takes effect today, removing the last institutional lever that could add supply. The Strait of Hormuz is still closed. There is no diplomatic resolution on the calendar. This is not a spike. It is the new baseline.

---

The corporate proof point: Qualcomm surged 16% on China order commentary and a new hyperscaler customer. A real chip demand signal landing in the middle of an anxiety-driven session. If semiconductor demand is turning independent of macro, that's a layer of fundamental support that doesn't care about oil prices or Fed minutes.

The angle nobody is covering: Kevin Warsh is the next Fed Chair. Warsh is a known inflation hawk. The market has priced a pause. The forward rate path hasn't priced a Warsh Fed. That repricing hasn't started yet.

---

Three markets gave the same verdict yesterday: bonds said no rescue, gold said inflation wins, crude said the supply shock is structural. The equity market said "fine, basically flat."

The 8:30 AM GDP and PCE prints land this morning. A below-consensus GDP paired with a hot PCE is the textbook confirmation. One of those markets is lying. I know which one I trust.
