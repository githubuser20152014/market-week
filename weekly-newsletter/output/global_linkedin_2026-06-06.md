The S&P 500 lost 2.6% this week. The Nasdaq lost 4.6%. Iran's Strait of Hormuz toll demands are the headline reason. Here is the part the headline misses.

This was not a geopolitical spike. It was the first week of a new macro regime.

---

**The dissenting markets told you first.**

Bonds: The 10-year Treasury closed at 4.54%, up 8 bps on the week. The bond market is not pricing a recession. It is pricing oil-driven structural inflation that prevents the Fed from cutting even as growth slows. That is a different problem than 2022. It has different solutions.

Gold: Gold fell 4.1% to $4,337 while oil surged toward $98 intraweek. That is not a sign inflation risk is easing. It is forced liquidation - leveraged longs selling real assets to cover equity margin calls as yields rose and the dollar snapped back to 100. The inflation signal is intact. The position was crowded.

Crude: WTI closed at $90.54, up 2.3%, with US crude inventories falling for a seventh consecutive week. Energy executives are publicly warning of $150 per barrel if the Hormuz blockade persists. The market is pricing a war premium. It is not pricing the scenario where that premium becomes permanent.

---

**The corporate proof point nobody connected.**

Dell surged 33% on Monday on AI infrastructure spending euphoria. By Friday, the Nasdaq had given back 4.6%. The same capex buildout powering the AI thesis is now being repriced against a structural energy cost increase from a blockade on 20% of global seaborne oil. The irony is not subtle.

---

**The second-order story the coverage missed.**

MSCI Emerging Markets fell 6.9% in five sessions - the sharpest single-week drawdown in the dataset. The story nobody is covering is the triple compression: dollar strength at 100+, oil threatening import bills for energy-dependent EM economies, and 12.5% tariffs targeting 60 economies including China and India. EM sovereign borrowers with dollar-denominated debt are now paying more to service that debt, facing higher energy import costs, and watching their export markets get tariffed simultaneously. That is not a risk-off rotation. That is a structural repricing that has further to run.

---

**Which market got it right?**

Bonds. The 10-year at 4.54% has been pricing a world where the Fed cannot cut because oil and tariffs keep structural inflation elevated. Equities spent four sessions catching up to that reality. The ECB hiking 25 bps into 0.1% GDP growth on June 11 confirms the regime label: stagflation. The Nasdaq's 4.6% decline is not the end of the adjustment.
