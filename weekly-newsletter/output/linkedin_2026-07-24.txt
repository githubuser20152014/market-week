The S&P fell 1.21% yesterday. Futures are up 0.27% this morning, betting it was a one-day AI panic that's already over.

They're betting on the wrong worry.

---

Gold closed at $4,062, up 0.38%, on a day the Nasdaq lost 2.15% and the 10-year yield climbed to 4.703%. Gold is not supposed to hold a bid while yields rise; higher yields make a non-yielding asset less attractive, full stop. Instead gold shrugged off the competition, which means whatever it's pricing outranks the bond market's own math.

Speaking of bonds: a 4.6 basis point jump on the 10-year the same day equities sold off is not the "flight to safety" script. It's the market repricing for inflation, not growth risk, and those two scenarios call for opposite portfolios.

Crude did its own strange thing. Brent briefly touched $102 on a twelfth straight night of Red Sea tanker attacks, and WTI still closed down 3.46% to $89. A live shipping-chokepoint crisis usually holds a fear premium longer than a few hours. This one didn't. Either oil traders think the conflict resolves fast, or they've stopped pricing tail risk altogether.

---

The corporate proof point: Alphabet and Tesla both confirmed the market's actual fear this earnings season, which isn't slowing growth, it's spending discipline. AI infrastructure capex is accelerating while near-term returns stay theoretical, and Alphabet's guidance just reset the valuation math for every cloud hyperscaler sitting behind it.

---

Here's the angle nobody's covering: on the same day cloud stocks got hammered for spending without proof of return, Oracle signed a 10-year, up to $7 billion software contract with the Pentagon. Government AI and infrastructure spending doesn't answer to quarterly ROI scrutiny the way commercial cloud does. While Wall Street interrogates Alphabet's capex, a chunk of that same spending thesis just got a decade of guaranteed government revenue instead. Two very different AI trades are happening in the same sector right now, and most coverage is treating them as one story.

---

So which market got yesterday right? Gold and bonds are both pricing something uncomfortable. Oil is pricing nothing. Futures this morning are pricing a bounce.

One of them is going to look very wrong by lunch. Today's 10:00 AM New Home Sales print is the first real data point that starts settling which one.
