# LinkedIn Post — Friday Fundaa: Oil as Feedstock

**Oil hit $100 a barrel. You felt it at the pump. That's actually the cheapest part.**

You pulled into a gas station this week and saw $3.59. Six weeks ago it was $2.49. The connection to the headline clicked: so that's what $100 oil feels like.

You're right. And that dollar-ten per gallon is real.

But the pump accounts for about 45% of what a barrel of crude actually becomes. The other 55% doesn't go into your tank. It goes into almost everything else you touch.

**Oil isn't just a fuel. It's a raw material.**

Roughly 6,000 everyday products are made from petroleum derivatives. Not products that use oil as energy to manufacture — products where oil is the physical ingredient.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

- **Plastics.** Every bottle, bag, blister pack, and food container starts as naphtha — a fraction of crude. Your grocery store is mostly petroleum with a thin layer of food on top.
- **Clothing.** About 60% of all clothing made globally is synthetic. Polyester, nylon, acrylic, spandex — all petroleum derivatives. The fleece you wear in the morning is a refined barrel.
- **Fertilizer and food.** Petroleum-derived pesticides and herbicides blanket virtually all commercial farming. When oil gets expensive, farm input costs rise. That increase reaches the grocery shelf 6 to 18 months later.
- **Medicine.** Aspirin, ibuprofen, many antihistamines — synthesized from petrochemical precursors. The pill you took this morning traces back to a barrel.

**The number that matters: $80 vs. $100.**

When oil moves from $80 to $100 a barrel, ethylene feedstock costs — the building block of most plastics — rise from roughly $600 to $800 or $900 per ton. That's a 25 to 35% input cost increase hitting every plastic product in every supply chain at once. It doesn't show up at the pump. It shows up 3 to 9 months later in the price of your shampoo, your garbage bags, your kid's lunchbox, your medication packaging.

**The pump price and the shelf price run on different clocks.**

When you fill up today, you're paying for today's crude. When you stand at the checkout in October wondering why prices haven't come down even though oil fell, you're paying for crude from April. Supply chains don't reprice overnight. Manufacturers lock in input costs months ahead. Retailers work through existing inventory before new orders get placed at lower prices.

The lag runs in both directions. Oil spikes today, your bill spikes next fall. Oil drops today, your bill eases sometime next year. By the time you feel the relief, the headlines have already moved on.

So the real question when oil hits $100 isn't what it costs to fill the tank. It's: what does the grocery bill look like in October? What's happening to every supply chain that runs on plastic, synthetic fiber, or fertilizer?

One barrel. Several thousand products. And a delay built in, so by the time you feel it, the number that caused it is already old news.
