### Trade Truce Sparks Global Risk-On Rotation

The dominant story this week was a broad, synchronized global equity rally accompanied by a sharp collapse in volatility. The VIX fell **13.25%** to close at **16.7**, after touching **19.44** intraday earlier in the week. That compression tells you fear was priced in at the open and systematically sold as the week progressed. Without specific news context provided, the price action itself tells a coherent story: cyclically-sensitive assets, small caps, and internationally-exposed equities all outperformed defensives and large-cap US tech, a rotation that historically follows either a geopolitical de-escalation or a dovish central bank pivot.

The most counterintuitive signal: **US mega-cap tech lagged badly**. The Nasdaq gained just **0.21%** while the Russell 2000 surged **2.72%** and the DAX added **4.43%**. When small caps and foreign markets outpace the S&P's tech-heavy composition by that margin, you are watching a regime shift, not noise. Capital is moving away from the crowded US large-cap growth trade and toward unloved cyclicals and international equities. **Oil's 8.87% weekly plunge** added fuel to that rotation: lower energy costs compress input costs for manufacturers and consumers globally, which is a de facto stimulus for the real economy without a single central bank move.

### What it means for you

This week's defining move was a simultaneous compression of volatility and a sharp rotation into cyclical, small-cap, and international equities. **The DAX gained 4.43% and the Russell 2000 surged 2.72%**, both dramatically outpacing US large-cap tech's 0.21% Nasdaq gain. **Oil dropped 8.87% to $96.60**, which acts as an economic stimulus for import-dependent economies and consumer-facing businesses globally. For a patient, globally diversified investor, the takeaway is clear: the crowded trade in US mega-cap growth is losing relative momentum, and the case for holding **EWG**, **FEZ**, and **IWM** alongside core **SPY** is now supported by both price action and macro tailwinds. Gold's mild pullback to $4,521 is not a breakdown; hold **GLD** as the portfolio hedge remains valid. The flat dollar this week meant international equity gains translated cleanly into USD returns, a window that narrows if the macro story shifts.

### Equity Markets - Europe and Small Caps Steal the Show

Europe was the standout performer of the week by a wide margin. The **DAX surged 4.43%**, the **Euro Stoxx 50 added 3.83%**, the CAC 40 gained **3.24%**, and even the more defensive FTSE 100 rose **2.66%**. These are not incremental moves. The DAX closed at **24,888**, approaching its weekly high of **24,944**. European equities are pricing in a combination of ECB rate-cut tailwinds, a potential improvement in external demand, and relief from elevated energy cost pressure as oil drops. The breadth across markets from Germany to the UK confirms this is not a single-country story.

In the US, the rotation beneath the surface is the signal. The **Russell 2000 gained 2.72%** against the Nasdaq's **0.21%**, a gap of more than 250 basis points in a single week. Small caps are rate-sensitive and domestically focused: their outperformance aligns with the **10-year Treasury yield easing 4 bps to 4.56%**. The S&P 500's **0.79% gain** is misleading as headline performance. Strip out the mega-cap drag and US equities had a strong week too. In Asia-Pacific, the **Nikkei jumped 3.33%** to close at **63,339**, while the Hang Seng slipped **0.9%**. The China-adjacent trade remains hesitant. Emerging markets broadly were flat, with **EEM up just 0.34%**.

### Currency Markets - Sterling Leads, Yen Slips Quietly

The USD Index was essentially unchanged, closing at **99.32** with a weekly move of just **+0.04%**. That flatness masked divergence underneath. **GBP/USD rose 0.92% to 1.3433**, the strongest G10 currency performer of the week. The pound's gain likely reflects improved UK economic sentiment and relative rate expectations. The Swiss franc added **0.33%** and the Australian dollar **0.27%**, both modest risk-on signals. The euro slipped **0.1%** against the dollar despite European equity strength, suggesting EUR/USD remains capped by ECB rate-cut expectations even as German stocks rally.

The Japanese yen weakened **0.17%**, a small but directionally consistent move. With the Nikkei surging over 3%, a softer yen is part of the same trade: Japanese exporters benefit from yen weakness, which reinforces equity upside without creating currency headwinds for JPY-denominated assets held by local investors. For globally diversified ETF holders, the flat dollar means international equity gains this week translated almost fully into USD returns, a clean setup that does not always hold.

### Commodities & Metals - Oil Crashes, Gold Softens, Silver Holds

**WTI crude oil dropped 8.87% to $96.60**, after trading as high as $109.47 intraday at the week's peak. That is a $14.74 intraweek range, signaling genuine uncertainty followed by decisive selling. An oil move of this magnitude in a single week is either supply-driven, demand-driven, or geopolitical de-escalation removing a war-premium. Without confirmed news context, the scale of the reversal from the weekly high strongly implies a geopolitical risk premium being unwound. Lower oil is unambiguously positive for consumer purchasing power, airline margins, and import-heavy economies in Europe and Asia.

Gold fell **0.92% to $4,521**, a mild pullback from elevated levels that is consistent with reduced safe-haven demand as risk appetite improved. The key insight: gold is not breaking down. It is consolidating near highs while equities rally, which historically suggests investors are not abandoning the hedge, just trimming at the margin. Silver held up better, gaining **0.72%** and closing at **$75.89**, supported by its industrial demand component. Natural gas fell **2.68%** to **$2.91**, continuing its soft trajectory.
