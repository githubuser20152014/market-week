Wall Street closed higher yesterday. Crude crossed $101 and the Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed through 2027.

Those two sentences shouldn't coexist. And yet.

---

The dissenting signals were loud if you knew where to look.

Crude jumped 3% to $101.03. Not a spike on a headline - a sustained move on confirmed disruption. Saudi Aramco's CEO publicly stated normalization won't come before 2027. The IEA labeled it the biggest supply shock in history. The market added 0.19% to the S&P 500.

Gold fell 0.23% to $4,707. On the same day. The asset specifically designed to absorb geopolitical chaos slipped while oil ripped 3%. The dollar ticked up 0.32% - apparently enough to override a closed chokepoint. Bond yields climbed 4.6bps to 4.41%, the bond market quietly pricing in the inflation math that equities chose to ignore.

Asia didn't ignore it. KOSPI dropped 2.3% overnight. India fell 1.5%. Nasdaq futures are down 0.7% pre-market. The overnight session reached a different conclusion than New York.

---

The corporate proof point arrived quietly. Spirit Airlines just became the first confirmed casualty of the fuel shock - folding under costs that were already existential before crude crossed $100. This isn't an airline story. It's what $101 oil looks like when it reaches the income statement. Ultra-low-cost carriers were the first to show it. They won't be the last.

Meanwhile, Big Oil's Q1 earnings revealed something quieter: trading desks are now the most profitable division at BP and Shell. They're not just producing oil anymore. They're trading it. In a world where the Strait of Hormuz is a geopolitical instrument, that's not a coincidence.

---

The second-order angle nobody is running: the transport sector is entering a structurally different cost environment. Airlines are the visible casualty. Logistics, shipping, and any business with high fuel exposure is repricing quietly. The damage doesn't arrive all at once - it accumulates in margin guidance over the next two quarters.

---

So which market got it right yesterday?

Equities voted for stability. Asia, bonds, gold, and crude voted for risk.

Four against one. CPI at 8:30 AM ET gets the deciding vote this morning. A hot print validates the risk side. A soft print buys time - but not forever, not with oil above $100.
