The S&P closed up 0.42% yesterday. Don't get comfortable.

While equities partied, three other markets were sending a very different memo, and none of them believe the rally.

Bonds: The 10-year yield ticked up 3 basis points to 4.569% after hawkish Fed minutes killed the July rate cut and put a September hike back in play. If bonds thought the equity move was justified, yields would be falling on flight-to-safety demand, not rising on rate-hike odds.

Gold: Gold slipped just half a percent to $4,083 despite 90 U.S. airstrikes on Iran and an active retaliation cycle across the Gulf. A half-percent dip during a live shooting war isn't gold agreeing with the rally. It's gold refusing to sell into chaos.

Crude: WTI jumped 2% to $72.84 as tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz ground to a near-standstill. Twenty percent of the world's seaborne oil runs through that chokepoint. This isn't a supply story yet, it's a shipping-insurance story, and premiums move faster than barrels do.

Then there's TSMC, which posted a 68% revenue surge, the most bullish AI demand signal in months. On any other day that's the headline. Today it's a rounding error, because SK Hynix's Nasdaq debut turned into an 8.9% single-day collapse in Seoul, the worst in years, and it's dragging tech sentiment down with it.

Here's the angle nobody's covering: the same Gulf states caught in the Hormuz standoff also supply a huge share of the world's ammonia and urea, the raw inputs for nitrogen fertilizer. A prolonged shipping freeze doesn't just raise oil prices. It raises the cost of growing food, months before anyone notices it at the register.

So which market got it right yesterday? Equities said everything is fine. Bonds, gold, and the shipping lanes said it isn't. History says bet on the ones that don't get invited to the rally.
