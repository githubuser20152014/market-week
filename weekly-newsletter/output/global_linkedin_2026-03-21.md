# LinkedIn Post — Global Investor Edition | March 21, 2026

---

This week, stocks fell and bond yields rose at the same time.

That doesn't happen often. When it does, it has a name: **stagflation** — the scenario where the economy slows while prices stay high. It's the hardest environment to invest in because the usual playbooks don't work cleanly.

Here's what stood out:

📉 **S&P 500 -1.9% | Dow & Nasdaq -2.1%** — broad-based selloff, every major index hit its weekly low on Friday (no late buyers stepping in)

📈 **10-Year Treasury yield rose to 4.39%** — bond markets tightening on their own, without the Fed having to officially raise rates

💵 **US dollar fell 1.1%** — the unusual part. Normally, investors flee to the dollar when markets get scary. This time, they didn't. That suggests money isn't just rotating between US assets — it may be leaving the US altogether.

🥇 **Gold dropped 9.5%** — not because gold became a bad investment, but because a wave of forced selling hit the market. When interest rates spike suddenly, leveraged investors sell their gold to cover losses elsewhere. The fundamentals of gold ownership haven't changed.

🌏 **Asia-Pacific held up** — Nikkei -0.4%, Hang Seng and ASX roughly flat. Cheaper valuations and a weaker dollar are making non-US markets more attractive.

---

**For long-term investors, the message isn't "panic" — it's "pay attention and adjust deliberately."**

The environment that rewarded simply holding US large-cap growth has shifted. That doesn't mean it's over, but it does mean concentration risk is real — and doing nothing is itself a choice.

Three moves worth considering:
→ Rotate some US growth exposure into defensives (utilities, healthcare, consumer staples) and international developed markets (Europe, Japan)
→ Use gold's dip as a buying opportunity — add gradually, not all at once
→ Shorten bond duration — long-dated Treasuries lose value when yields rise

The long-term investor's real edge is using volatility as a buying schedule, not a reason to abandon the plan.
