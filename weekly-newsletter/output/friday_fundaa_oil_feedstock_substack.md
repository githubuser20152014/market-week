**Friday Fundaa | Oil isn't just in your gas tank. It's in your t-shirt.**

*A short weekly moment of "huh, didn't know that" from the world of markets and money.*

---

Oil crossed $100 a barrel this week. You heard it on the news. And then you pulled into a gas station and saw $3.59 on the pump — up from $2.49 six weeks ago. The connection clicked. So that's what $100 oil feels like.

You're right that it does. A dollar-ten more per gallon, every fill-up, until the price comes down. That math is real and it stings.

But here's the part that didn't make the headline: the pump is actually the least expensive way oil is going to affect your life right now.

Most people stop there. Oil equals gas. Pay more when oil is expensive, pay less when it isn't. Clean transaction. Visible, immediate, done.

That's about 45% of what a barrel of crude actually becomes.

The other 55% doesn't go into your fuel tank. It goes into almost everything else you touch.

Oil is not just a fuel. It's a feedstock — a raw material that gets cracked, distilled, and reformed into the building blocks of modern manufacturing. Roughly 6,000 everyday products are derived from petroleum. Not products that use oil as energy to make them. Products where oil is the physical ingredient.

Here's what that actually looks like.

**Plastics.** Every bottle, bag, blister pack, and food container you interact with starts as naphtha — a light fraction of crude oil. Naphtha gets cracked into ethylene and propylene, which become polyethylene and polypropylene, which become the plastic shell around your yogurt, your shampoo, your leftovers. Your grocery store is mostly petroleum with a thin layer of food on top.

**Your clothes.** About 60% of all clothing made globally is synthetic. Polyester, nylon, acrylic, spandex — every one of them is a petroleum derivative. The fleece you wear on a cold morning? That's a refined barrel. The activewear you paid $80 for? Oil, shaped and dyed. Cotton and wool are the exceptions now, not the rule.

**Fertilizer and food.** Petroleum-derived pesticides and herbicides are applied across virtually all commercial farming. More directly, ammonia-based fertilizers, which feed half the world's crop yield, require enormous amounts of energy to produce — and in many regions, that energy comes from oil and gas feedstocks. When oil gets expensive, farm input costs go up. That increase reaches the grocery shelf 6 to 18 months later.

**Your medicine cabinet.** Aspirin, ibuprofen, many antihistamines, and a wide range of pharmaceutical compounds are synthesized from petrochemical precursors. The pill you took this morning traces back to a barrel.

Now for the part that actually matters.

When oil goes from $80 to $100 a barrel, ethylene feedstock costs — the building block of most plastics — rise from roughly $600 to $800 or $900 per ton. That's a 25 to 35% input cost increase on every plastic product in every supply chain simultaneously. It doesn't show up at the pump. It shows up 3 to 9 months later in the price of your shampoo, your garbage bags, your kid's lunchbox, your medication packaging.

This is the thing nobody explains clearly: the pump price and the shelf price run on different clocks.

When you fill up your car today, you're paying for today's crude. When you stand at the checkout in October wondering why your grocery bill hasn't come down even though you heard oil prices fell, you're paying for crude from April. Supply chains don't reprice overnight. Manufacturers lock in input costs months ahead. Retailers work through existing inventory before placing new orders at lower prices.

The lag is real, it's structural, and it runs in both directions. Oil spikes today, your bill spikes next fall. Oil drops today, your bill eases sometime next year. By the time you feel the relief, the headlines have moved on to something else entirely.

So the next time you see a headline about oil hitting $X a barrel, the question isn't just what it costs to fill the tank. The question is: what does the grocery bill look like in October? What's the price of a jacket going to be next winter? What's happening to every supply chain that runs on plastic, synthetic fiber, or fertilizer?

One barrel. Several thousand products. And a delay built in, so by the time you feel it, the number that caused it is already old news.

Now you know.
