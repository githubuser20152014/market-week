### War Premium Collapses, Risk Assets Surge on Ceasefire

The week of April 13-17 was defined by one of the most dramatic geopolitical whipsaws in recent memory. On Monday, the collapse of U.S.-Iran peace talks in Islamabad triggered a naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, sending **Brent crude above $105/barrel** and punishing global equities at the open. Indian and Asian markets bore the first wave of selling, with the Nikkei and Sensex gapping sharply lower. The blockade, targeting a chokepoint responsible for **21% of global oil supply**, immediately activated stagflation fears and dragged Treasury yields higher as energy-driven inflation looked set to reassert itself.

The pivot came mid-week as diplomatic channels reopened. A **Pakistani-brokered 14-day ceasefire between the U.S., Israel, and Iran** took effect on April 15, and by April 17 a separate **10-day Israel-Lebanon truce** was also in place. President Trump signaled a broader deal with Iran could follow within days. Oil reversed violently - WTI closed the week near **$83.85, down nearly 18% from its intraweek high of $105.63** - and that single move unlocked the rest of the rally. Cooler-than-expected **March PPI at 4.0% versus a 4.6% forecast** reinforced the disinflation narrative, the 10-year yield eased to 4.25%, and the S&P 500 crossed 7,000 for the first time in its history.

The counterintuitive read here is that **markets ended the week pricing a peace dividend despite the blockade remaining technically in force and Russia simultaneously launching its deadliest strikes of 2026 on Ukraine**. The IMF cut its 2026 global growth forecast to 3.1% and warned of a sovereign debt crisis, yet risk appetite surged. This is a market trading on geopolitical optionality - pricing the best-case resolution while a fragile, multi-front war remains very much live. VIX closed at 17.5, down sharply but still elevated enough to remind us this is a ceasefire rally, not a peace rally.

### Equity Markets - Tech Leads as the War Premium Unwinds

U.S. equities delivered one of the strongest weekly performances in years, with the narrative shifting from geopolitical panic on Monday to a historic milestone by Thursday. The **Nasdaq surged 7.09%**, its leadership driven by AI momentum - Meta's custom chip partnership with Broadcom, Amazon's **$11.6 billion acquisition of GlobalStar satellite infrastructure**, and BlackRock upgrading U.S. equities to overweight all contributed to the tech bid. The **S&P 500 gained 4.7% to close at 7,126**, while the Russell 2000 added **5.76%**, suggesting the rally had genuine breadth rather than being purely a mega-cap story. The Dow's comparatively modest **3.62%** gain reflects the index's heavier weighting toward energy and industrials, sectors that faced headwinds as oil collapsed off its highs.

Europe produced solid but more measured gains, with the **DAX up 4.84%** - supported by the political earthquake of Hungary's Tisza party landslide ending Viktor Orban's 16-year rule, a development expected to unlock **up to €18 billion in frozen EU funds** and reduce the political risk premium across Central and Eastern Europe. The CAC 40 added **3.02%** and Euro Stoxx 50 gained **2.75%**, while the FTSE 100's **0.62%** gain was the clear laggard - reflecting sterling's modest appreciation eating into export competitiveness and the UK's greater energy import sensitivity. In Asia-Pacific, **MSCI EM was the standout at +5.93%**, buoyed by China's **Q1 GDP beat of 5.0%** and the ceasefire rally. The Nikkei recovered **3.64%** from Monday's geopolitical lows, but the ASX 200 was essentially flat at **-0.15%**, a notable divergence given that Australia's commodity currency was simultaneously surging - suggesting domestic factors and the oil price collapse in energy names offset any risk-on tailwind.

### Currency Markets - Dollar Retreats as Safe-Haven Premium Fades

The U.S. dollar weakened broadly, with the **DXY falling 0.96% to close at 98.1** - touching an intraweek low of 97.63 as the ceasefire narrative took hold and PPI data reinforced the case for eventual Fed flexibility. The biggest winner in the G10 complex was the **Australian dollar, up 1.98% to 0.7159**, consistent with the broader commodity currency rotation highlighted by the Investing.com report noting AUD and NOK up over 7% year-to-date as investors price energy exporter premiums. The Swiss franc gained **1.24%** - an unusual combination of CHF strength alongside risk-on, but explicable given persistent geopolitical uncertainty: investors were buying risk assets while keeping one hand on the safe-haven handle.

Sterling added **0.94%** and the euro **0.82%**, the latter modestly supported by the Hungary political shift and its implications for EU fiscal cohesion. The yen's **0.70% gain** was the softest in the group, reflecting the continued complexity of the BoJ's normalization path and Japan's oil import vulnerability. For global investors, the dollar's softness this week is a tailwind for unhedged international exposure - it amplifies the already strong local-currency returns in European and emerging market equities when translated back to USD terms. The key question is whether this is structural dollar weakness driven by U.S. fiscal concerns (the IMF's warning on global debt approaching **100% of GDP by 2029** applies to the U.S. too) or simply a relief rally in risk appetite. The answer matters enormously for how long this EM tailwind persists.

### Commodities & Metals - Oil Crashes From War Premium, Gold Holds Records

**WTI crude oil fell 17.79% on the week**, the single most important market move of the period, and its trajectory tells the entire macro story in one chart. The contract printed a high of **$105.63** at the peak of blockade panic on Monday before collapsing to close at **$83.85** as ceasefire diplomacy progressed and Iran signaled it might allow Omani-side transit. This was not a fundamental demand story - it was pure geopolitical risk premium being inserted and then partially extracted within five trading days. The blockade remains technically in force, which means **the oil risk is not resolved - it is repriced**. Any diplomatic breakdown would send crude back toward triple digits rapidly, and the Strait of Hormuz still handles a fifth of global oil supply.

Gold told a different story, gaining **3.27% to close near $4,857** and testing resistance at $4,879 intraweek. JPMorgan and BofA analysts cited in the news context have floated a **$5,000 year-end target**, supported by record central bank buying and a strained U.S. credit market backdrop. Silver was the week's most explosive commodity, surging **12.28% to $81.74** - a move that typically signals industrial demand optimism layered on top of monetary metal safe-haven buying. The gold-silver ratio compression is a classic risk-on signal within the metals complex. Natural gas was essentially flat at **-0.74%**, suggesting domestic supply dynamics are holding even as the broader energy complex gyrated on Middle East headlines.

### This Week's Economic Events

Economic data was sparse relative to the geopolitical noise but delivered meaningful signals where it landed. The most important release was March PPI coming in at **4.0% annually versus a 4.6% forecast**, with core PPI rising just **0.2% month-over-month** - a result that directly undercut the stagflation narrative that had dominated Monday's trading. This data point, arriving on April 15 as ceasefire talks were simultaneously gathering momentum, gave markets permission to rally hard. The combination of cooling producer prices and easing oil (even if temporary) was enough to move 10-year yields from 4.35% to 4.25% on the week.

The trade balance widened to -$57.3 billion from -$54.7 billion previously, and industrial production printed at -0.5% - neither figure inspiring confidence in the underlying growth picture. These releases reflect an economy navigating genuine headwinds from the energy shock and supply chain disruption that the Hormuz blockade accelerated. The IMF's mid-week forecast cut to **3.1% global growth** provides important context: the data and the institutions are signaling caution even as markets celebrate a fragile ceasefire. New Home Sales data for the full week did not print a verified actual, leaving the housing picture incomplete. Next week's Industrial Production and New Home Sales readings will be watched for evidence of how much the early-week geopolitical shock actually transmitted into real economic activity.

### Next Week: What to Watch

The single most critical variable for next week is whether the **14-day U.S.-Iran ceasefire and the 10-day Israel-Lebanon truce hold**, and whether Trump's signaled "deal" with Iran materializes over the weekend. Any breakdown sends oil back toward $100 and unwinds much of this week's equity rally - the VIX at 17.5 implies the market is not fully complacent, but it is pricing a high probability of diplomatic progress. On the data side, **Industrial Production (April 20)** will be the first hard read on how the manufacturing sector absorbed the mid-month geopolitical shock, while **New Home Sales (April 24)** will test whether the housing market is holding up against still-elevated mortgage rates even as the 10-year dipped. Watch also for any Fedspeak that interprets the PPI beat and yield move - if officials signal growing comfort with the disinflation trajectory, that would add fundamental legs to what is currently a sentiment-driven rally. The Antalya Diplomacy Forum discussions on the fragmentation of the U.S.-led global architecture may also generate headlines that test market nerves.

### The One Trade: [GLD](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/GLD) - Long

Gold is the cleanest expression of the macro regime that this week's events have clarified. The metal gained **3.27%** even as risk assets surged - meaning it is no longer trading as a simple fear hedge that sells off when equities recover. Instead, gold is being bid on a structural thesis: a weakening dollar, record central bank accumulation, U.S. fiscal deterioration flagged by the IMF's warning on global debt approaching **100% of GDP by 2029**, and a geopolitical backdrop that remains genuinely unresolved despite the ceasefire headlines.

The bull case here is multi-layered. First, the ceasefire is fragile - the naval blockade is still active, Israel is still striking Lebanon, and Russia just launched its deadliest barrage of 2026 on Ukraine. Any re-escalation drives safe-haven demand. Second, the PPI miss and 10-year yield decline to **4.25%** reduce the opportunity cost of holding gold at a time when JPMorgan and BofA are both citing **$5,000 as a credible year-end target**. Third, the dollar's structural weakness - down nearly 1% this week with commodity currencies surging - is a powerful tailwind for gold priced in USD. Silver's **12.28% surge** this week is historically a leading indicator that the broader metals bull is gaining industrial as well as monetary legs, which further validates the GLD position.

The technical picture supports the thesis: gold closed at **$4,857** having tested and held the $4,704 low earlier in the week, and intraweek resistance is at $4,879. A clean break above $4,900 would open the path toward the $5,000 institutional target.

**Confirms:** Gold closes above $4,900 on sustained dollar weakness or any deterioration in ceasefire diplomacy that sends oil back above $95.

**Risk:** A comprehensive, verified U.S.-Iran deal that removes the Hormuz risk entirely, combined with a dollar recovery on stronger-than-expected U.S. economic data, could see gold retrace toward the $4,600-$4,650 range.
