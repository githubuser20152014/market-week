The Strait of Hormuz was pricing in a supply apocalypse at the start of the week. By Friday, Iran blinked on the brinkmanship. Oil crashed $13 in five days. Equities celebrated. The bond market abstained.

That divergence tells the whole story. **WTI crude fell 8.87%** from $109.47 to $96.60 because a specific geopolitical risk - the Hormuz supply threat - moved from "high probability" to "managed." Crude fell hard because supply risk is now less relevant. Growth expectations did not change.

The fixed income market did not miss this signal. **10-year yields fell only 4 basis points to 4.56%** despite equities rallying across every region. If this were a genuine growth acceleration, the bond market would have sold off hard. Instead it shrugged. That is not a confirmation of a new bull leg. It is the market politely suggesting that equity investors are celebrating the removal of a geopolitical tax, not the arrival of a macro upgrade.

**Gold fell 0.92%** as safe-haven demand unwound in an orderly fashion. The VIX dropped 13.25%. There was no panic exit. This was a measured de-risk trade, not a risk-on capitulation. The fear unwind was real, but the structural drivers of that fear remain.

European equities bore the brunt of the geopolitical premium and rebounded the hardest. The **DAX gained 4.43%** to 24,888, closing near weekly highs. Industrials and exporters - the most leveraged expression of a trade de-escalation play - led the charge. If this Hormuz relief is durable, German equities are structurally cheap relative to US peers and have the most to recover from tariff uncertainty.

But here is the second-order story nobody is talking about: the Dow hit **50,000** this week. Consumer sentiment hit a ditch at the same time. Record wealth at the top. Record anxiety at the bottom. The stock market is a machine that converts human anxiety into capital for the few. That gap does not close with central bank rate cuts. It closes when the consumer gets real wage growth or the market gives back 5-10%.

Which market read this week correctly? The bond market, which abstained. It is telling you that equities are celebrating a geopolitical relief, not a macro upgrade. The Dow 50,000 is a milestone. Consumer sentiment in the ditch is the context.
