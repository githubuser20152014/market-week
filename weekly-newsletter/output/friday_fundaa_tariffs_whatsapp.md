*Friday Fundaa 🎓*
_A short weekly moment of "huh, didn't know that" from the world of markets and money._

"Liberation Day" was a year ago. The tariffs are still here. The promise: the other countries will pay 🎉

Spoiler: they didn't. You did 🙃

Here's the boring truth: a tariff is an import tax collected at the US border. Paid by the US importer. Not the factory in China. Not the Vietnamese government. The *American company* importing the goods pays it, then does what any company does: passes it straight to you.

$25 sneaker + 46% tariff = $36 sneaker. Factory got their $25. US government collected $11. You're out $36. The factory in Hanoi didn't write a cheque. You did 🤷

One year later, here's your shopping list 🧾

Avocados: $0.87 to $1.45/lb (90% of US avocados come from Mexico, which got a 25% tariff - someone did the maths). Bananas: 50¢ to 54¢/lb. Levi's jeans: +$10 a pair (they literally said "tariffs" in their earnings call, bless their honesty). Nike shoes: +$5-$10 per pair. PlayStation 5: $499 to $649 after *two* price hikes in one year. And Yale Budget Lab says the average household is paying ~$2,400 more per year across all the tariffs. This is fine 🔥

"But what about the other country? Don't they suffer?"

Sure, a bit. If prices get high enough, buyers switch suppliers. That hurts their exports.

But losing a customer is different from paying a tax. The tariff money doesn't go to China. It goes to the US Treasury. Via your wallet.

And then they retaliate 😅 China hit back with 125% tariffs on US goods after Liberation Day. American farmers and manufacturers suddenly couldn't sell into their biggest overseas market. The bill circled back. Again.

The tariff is a tax on your shopping cart. The foreign country watches. And charges back.
