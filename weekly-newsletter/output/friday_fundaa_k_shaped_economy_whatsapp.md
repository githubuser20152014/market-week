_A short weekly moment of "huh, didn't know that" from the world of markets and money._

GDP grew 2.1%. Unemployment: 4.3%. Wages up 3.9%. America: thriving 🎉

Your rent is up $500 a month. Your grocery bill is up $130. Your health insurance is $120 more every month. Same apartment. Same supermarket. Same plan. Nothing changed — except the price of everything 🙃

Something doesn't add up. But it's not a glitch. It's maths.

GDP is an average. It adds up everything the economy produces and divides by population. Sounds reasonable. Except it's averaging two completely different realities.

Reality 1: Someone put $30,000 into a plain index fund in January 2020 and did nothing. Didn't pick stocks. Didn't time the market. Just sat on it. That $30,000 is now $67,000. They made $37,000 without a single salary negotiation. Just ownership. 📈

Reality 2: You. Rent went from ~$1,200 to ~$1,700 a month. Groceries from $450 to $580. Health insurance: $1,450 more a year. That's $750 extra every month, on things you can't cut out. Your 3.9% raise covered less than a third of it.

GDP averaged these two people together and said: the economy is fine 🤷

Here's why that's not wrong but also not the full story: the top 1% own nearly half the entire stock market. The bottom 50% own about 1% of it. When the market goes up, it mostly goes up into brokerage accounts, not paychecks. The top 1% also own 31% of all wealth. The bottom 50% own 2.5%.

So when asset prices compound and wages crawl, the average looks healthy — because one side of the average is doing very well indeed.

This is called the K-shaped economy. Two branches from the same spine. The top branch — asset owners — going up. The bottom branch — wage earners — going mostly sideways. GDP is the average of both. The average looks fine. The branches are not in the same conversation.

"The economy is doing great."

Just not yours.
