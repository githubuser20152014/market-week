The Dow added 1.73% yesterday. Don't let that fool you.

The Nasdaq flatlined. S&P futures are down 0.37% this morning and Nasdaq futures are off 0.87%. That is not a unified rally. That is one index dragging the headline number while the rest of the tape quietly disagrees.

Here's why the dissent matters more than the Dow print.

---

BONDS said slow down.

The 10-year yield eased to 4.477% - not because the economy is rolling over, but because the market is pricing a soft NFP number this morning. Bond buyers are making a bet: jobs data cools just enough to accelerate the Fed's rate-cut timeline. That bet only pays if the 8:30 AM print comes in below 85K. Everything hinges on one number.

GOLD said don't trust it.

Gold held $4,494 on a day the Dow was up 1.73%. That is not gold being dragged along by a risk-on rally. That is a separate, independent bid. Buyers are treating $4,490 as a structural floor with the Strait of Hormuz still contested, the dollar softening to 99.18, and geopolitical premiums nowhere near resolved. When gold refuses to sell off while equities party, one of them is pricing the future wrong.

CRUDE said the oil shock is not over.

WTI slipped to $92.86 but held above $90 for a seventh straight week as U.S. Navy ships quietly coordinated with tankers to navigate Hormuz exits. Trump floated a potential meeting with Iran's supreme leader. A diplomatic tweet is not a ceasefire. The energy premium stays in the price until there is an actual deal - not a rumor of one.

---

The corporate proof point of the week: Broadcom.

Projected Q3 AI revenue of $16 billion. Below expectations. That shortfall - not a collapse, just a miss - wiped $285 billion in market cap overnight and sent shockwaves through every semiconductor market in the world by morning.

Seoul absorbed the full hit. The KOSPI fell 5.5% and the Korea Exchange suspended program trading before 9:15 AM local time. Samsung lost 6.7%. SK Hynix dropped 8.8%. Jensen Huang flew into Seoul that morning with "some surprises" prepared. The market did not wait to find out what they were.

---

The angle nobody is covering: SpaceX.

The company is preparing for a Nasdaq debut on June 12 - 555 million shares at $135, one of the largest IPOs in history at a $1.75 trillion valuation. Global capital allocation does not happen in a vacuum. When that much money needs to show up somewhere by June 12, it has to come from somewhere else. Emerging market equity flows - including Korea - are the first to get trimmed. Foreign investors have now sold Korean equities for 20 consecutive sessions, pulling 70 trillion won out of the market this year. The SpaceX calendar is not a coincidence.

---

Which market got it right - the Dow's 1.73% or the KOSPI's -5.5%?

The answer is neither and both. The Dow is pricing a soft NFP that forces the Fed's hand on cuts. The KOSPI is pricing what happens when a 100%-YTD rally built on two stocks meets one disappointing AI revenue number. Both are correct about their own thesis. The problem is that both theses cannot survive a hot NFP print.

The 8:30 AM number settles it.
