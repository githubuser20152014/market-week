The ceasefire was supposed to be a clean risk-on trade. Gold disagreed.

Thursday's Iran de-escalation was textbook: Trump cancelled planned strikes, a 60-day ceasefire emerged, the Strait of Hormuz MOU promised to reopen one of the world's most critical oil chokepoints. Crude dumped 4.65% to $83.63. Equities ripped. Everything tracked exactly as expected.

Then gold closed at $4,244. Up 3.76%. On a ceasefire day.

That is not a celebration. That is a hedge. And the market is usually right about these things.

Here's why the gold move matters more than the equity move:

Bonds confirmed the narrative: the 10-year yield dropped 7.9 basis points to 4.463%, because lower crude is the closest thing to a gift the Fed has seen all year. The oil-as-inflation-tax argument just got materially weaker overnight.

But gold going up 3.76% while equities rallied is the market saying the MOU is unverified optimism. The deal is not signed. CPI is still at 4.2%. The June 17 FOMC meeting is live. Warsh has not signalled a pause. The buyers are celebrating and hedging simultaneously, which is what you do when you think the rally is real but fragile.

Small caps told the most honest story of the day. The Russell 2000 surged 3.02% - more than any major US index - because domestic-focused companies absorb the oil cost headwind most directly. Crude falling from $88 to $83 is a direct margin expansion event for the industrials, consumer names, and regional businesses that make up IWM. That relief is real regardless of whether the Iran deal holds.

The second-order story nobody is covering: European equities caught a peace dividend bid, with luxury names like LVMH up 5%. But the ECB just hiked to 2.25% into a Q1 GDP contraction. That is a stagflation setup. The luxury pop is a one-session headline trade, not a structural thesis. Anyone adding European exposure here is buying the wrong catalyst.

Which market got it right - equities celebrating the ceasefire, or gold refusing to fall?

Both. The ceasefire relief is real. The fragility of an unsigned deal with CPI at 4.2% and FOMC in five days is also real. The market is holding both ideas at once, and so should you.
