_A short weekly moment of "huh, didn't know that" from the world of markets and money._

You pulled up to the pump this week. $3.59. Six weeks ago it was $2.49. The news said oil crossed $100 a barrel, and you felt it 🙃

Here's the part nobody told you: the pump is actually the *cheapest* way oil is affecting your wallet right now.

A barrel of crude doesn't just become gasoline. About 45% does. The other 55% becomes... basically everything else you own 👇

Your water bottle? Oil. Your grocery packaging? Oil. The fleece you're wearing? Oil — literally. About 60% of all clothing globally is synthetic (polyester, nylon, spandex). You're wearing a refined barrel 🛢️

The shampoo on your shelf, the blister pack your ibuprofen came in, the garbage bag under your sink — all petroleum derivatives. Roughly 6,000 everyday products start as crude oil.

And here's where it gets interesting 🤔

When oil goes from $80 to $100 a barrel, the cost of making plastic goes up 25 to 35%. That increase doesn't show up at the pump. It shows up 3 to 9 months later in the price of your shampoo, your kid's lunchbox, your medication packaging.

The pump price and your grocery bill run on different clocks.

Fill up today, you're paying for today's crude. Standing at the checkout in October wondering why nothing got cheaper even though you heard oil prices fell? You're paying for April's crude 🗓️

Oil spikes today, your bill spikes next fall. Oil drops today, your bill eases sometime next year. By the time you feel it, the headline is ancient history.

So next time you see "oil hits $X a barrel" — the question isn't just what it costs to fill the tank. It's what's the grocery bill going to look like in October?
