$101 Oil. The Biggest Supply Shock in History. S&P Up 7 Points. Sure.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

Equities finished higher yesterday. Crude crossed $101. Gold fell.

The Saudi Aramco CEO went on record saying Hormuz normalization won't come before 2027. The IEA called it the biggest supply shock in history. Wall Street closed up 0.19% and called it a day.

Asia apparently read the same headlines and reached a different conclusion. KOSPI down 2.3%. India off 1.5%. Nasdaq futures down 0.7% pre-market. CPI at 8:30 ET now gets to settle the argument.

Here's my read:

The scorecard first: S&P 500 at 7,412. Dow at 49,704. Nasdaq at 26,274. Russell 2000 at 2,870 - the index that tends to know things early, and it barely moved. The actual session was happening elsewhere. WTI crude +3% to $101.03 - that's the market's invoice for a closed chokepoint with a 2027 reopening date. The 10Y yield climbed 4.6bps to 4.41% - the bond market doing the math on triple-digit oil and calling it inflation out loud. And gold dropped 0.23% to $4,707 on the exact day the IEA declared the biggest supply shock in history.

Gold is supposed to rally when oil supply gets strangled. Yesterday it fell. The dollar ticked up 0.32% - not much, but enough to keep gold in check. If CPI prints hot this morning, the dollar reverses. Gold follows. That's what the trade is.

The catalyst structure is clean. Iran rejected the latest ceasefire terms. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with Aramco's CEO publicly dating the disruption through 2027. Spirit Airlines just became the first confirmed corporate casualty - folding under fuel costs isn't an airline story, it's a supply shock arriving at the income statement. And Trump's Beijing summit with Xi - 16 CEOs, Taiwan arms sales, Hong Kong, a potential tech divorce order on Meta - adds a second live grenade to the session.

The One Trade: Long $GLD

Gold refused to rally on the biggest supply shock in history while oil ripped 3%. That suppression breaks violently if today's CPI print is hot and forces a stagflation narrative that weakens the dollar.

Confirms: GLD holds above $460 in the first 30 minutes post-CPI and moves toward $465 as the dollar index drops below 98.00 on the inflation read.

Kill switch: CPI prints below consensus, equities rally, dollar firms above 98.50, and gold fails to reclaim yesterday's close - stagflation thesis is off the table for now, get out fast.

What else I'm watching

  • $XLE and $XOP - don't you dare sell these into the open. WTI above $101 with a 2027 disruption timeline from Aramco's own CEO is a sustained earnings tailwind for integrated majors. Hold as long as crude holds $99.
  • $JETS - stay away from it. Spirit Airlines is the first casualty, not the last. This is not a buy-the-dip setup at $101 oil.
  • $TLT - the trade nobody is talking about is reducing duration ahead of 8:30. Yields at 4.41% with a hot CPI incoming eliminates any Fed cut narrative. Hold $SGOV instead.
  • Nasdaq tech into the Beijing summit - treat any pre-market bounce as a fade. A tech divorce order on Meta would reprice US mega-cap China exposure fast. $KWEB has the separate upside case if dialogue is constructive - but that's a different bet.
  • The second-order story nobody is covering: Spirit Airlines going down is the opening act. Airlines, transports, and any company with a high fuel-cost structure is now in a different business model than it was six months ago. $IYT is the tell.
