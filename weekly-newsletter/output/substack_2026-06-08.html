Middle East Goes Kinetic. Nasdaq Drops 4.2%. Gold Holds a Fire Sale.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

The April ceasefire between Israel and Iran made it all the way to June. Direct military strikes Monday morning ended that. WTI crude hit $94.59, up 4.5%. Brent spiked to $97.60. The Nasdaq dropped 4.2% to 25,709. The S&P 500 closed at 7,383, down 2.6%.

Gold fell 0.5% to $4,317. On a day with live military exchanges in the Middle East. The dollar did nothing. The 10-year yield climbed to 4.54% as equities fell. That is not a flight-to-safety signal. That is the stagflation fingerprint.

S&P futures are up 0.3% this morning because some people buy dips regardless of circumstances. The KOSPI collapsed 8.3% overnight on the same news. Pick which market you find more credible. One of them is wrong, and it is not the KOSPI.

Here's my read:

This session had everything the bulls did not want. Oil up 4.5% to $94.59 while the 10-year yield rose to 4.54%, yields and crude moving together on a day equities fell hard. The Nasdaq shed 4.2% to 25,709 - tech is taking the worst of it because growth stocks priced for a 2027 rate-cut cycle are now priced wrong. The Russell 2000 dropped 3.5% to 2,833: small caps are the canaries of domestic credit, and they are canaries with a cough. The Dow held relatively better at 50,867, off 1.3%, the defensive rotation already done before Friday's close even printed. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index had already shed over $1 trillion in value last week before this escalation hit. The disinflation trade that took the S&P above 7,600 is over. The repricing has started. It has not finished.

Gold's non-move is the tell. $4,317, down 0.5%, on a day with direct military exchanges in a region that supplies a meaningful fraction of global crude. Gold sitting on its hands while oil explodes is not a reassuring sign - it means the bid is not structural, just positional. The Strait of Hormuz premium in crude is real. The safe-haven premium in gold is apparently not. That asymmetry is the most important thing in this report.

The One Trade: Long $XLE

WTI at $94.59 with active conflict near the Strait of Hormuz is a supply shock with no near-term resolution. Energy earnings estimates have not caught up to the new price regime yet. The setup: energy is the only sector with a structural tailwind when oil reprices higher on a geopolitical shock, and the Hormuz premium stays in until there is a verified ceasefire with enforcement, not a Trump tweet.

Confirms: XLE holds above its opening print by 10:30 AM ET and WTI crude stays above $92 through the morning session.

Kill switch: A credible, verified ceasefire announcement from either the White House or UN before noon ET collapses the geopolitical premium and takes WTI back toward $88. Get out fast if that headline drops.
