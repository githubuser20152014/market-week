Yesterday the Nasdaq closed up 0.86%. Today futures are down 1.23%.

That's not a contradiction. That's the setup for CPI day.

The Cleveland Fed Nowcast is sitting at 4.18% ahead of the 8:30 ET print. That's the number that matters — just above the threshold that eliminates every remaining rate cut from the 2026 calendar. Kevin Warsh's first FOMC decision is June 17. A hot print today turns that debut into a hawkish signal with no cuts to cushion it.

Here's why yesterday's rally doesn't matter:

The Nasdaq's 0.86% gain was semiconductor-led — Micron and Nvidia doing the heavy lifting while the Dow shed 0.16% and the rotation into banks and pharma quietly started. That's not broad strength. That's a sector trade ahead of a macro catalyst.

Meanwhile the cross-asset signals were telling a different story. Gold dropped 1.60% to $4,191.80 despite a weaker dollar. The 10Y yield ticked up to 4.552%. The crisis hedge sold off on the same day the U.S. completed strikes on Iranian targets following an Apache helicopter attack near the Strait of Hormuz. Energy Secretary Wright publicly flagged rising Hormuz traffic as a supply risk. Oil is "choppy" at $87.55 — the market's polite word for no conviction either way.

The second-order story nobody is leading with: the Iran situation has moved from "ceasefire optimism" to active U.S. military engagement in under 48 hours. The geopolitical war premium in energy wasn't unwinding — it was repricing. That distinction matters for how you read the oil trade going forward.

Which market got it right yesterday — equities celebrating the AI rotation, or gold selling off while bombs dropped?

The answer arrives at 8:30 AM ET. 4.2% YoY is the line. Above it, the futures selloff is just the preview. Below 4.0% and the dip-buyers get their moment.
