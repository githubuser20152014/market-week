Nonfarm payrolls missed by 58,000. The Dow closed at a record high anyway. If your first reaction is "great, the market's telling us this is fine," you're reading it wrong.

Start with bonds. If everyone genuinely believed this jobs miss guarantees a rate cut, the 10-year yield should have dropped hard. Instead it crept up to 4.485%. That's not conviction, that's a market keeping its options open.

Then there's gold, up 1.25% to $4,165 on a day the dollar also firmed 0.2%. Those two don't usually move together, gold rallies when the dollar weakens, not when it strengthens. When both go up at once, it typically means something scarier than a soft jobs number is in the price. In this case: China tested an ICBM into the South Pacific, ran fresh naval drills with Russia near Qingdao, and a cargo vessel got hit in the Red Sea, all inside the same 24 hours.

Crude did the opposite of what the geopolitics would suggest, down 0.66% to $68.24 even with a live Red Sea incident and lingering Strait of Hormuz uncertainty on the table. Either the oil market thinks these flashpoints stay contained, or it hasn't caught up yet.

The clearest read-through sits in a deal, not a data point: Lockheed Martin is reportedly leading the race to buy naval defense firm Ultra Maritime for $3.5 billion. That's a defense prime committing real capital to naval capability in the same week NATO allies are being pressed on "3.0" spending pledges and China is testing ICBMs into the Pacific. Nobody needed a press release to tell them where the money is flowing.

The angle nobody's covering: this isn't really about missiles, it's about the naval and maritime supply chain quietly rebuilding around a more contested Pacific and Red Sea. Naval sensors, undersea infrastructure, unglamorous corners of the defense industrial base are about to get a lot more attention than chip export controls.

So which market got yesterday right: the equity rally that treated a jobs miss as good news, or gold and the bond market that both looked past the rate-cut story toward something else entirely? I'm with gold. A record-day Dow rally built on 57,000 jobs is a party standing on a folding table.
