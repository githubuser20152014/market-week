CPI
Consumer Price Index
What is it?

CPI measures how much a fixed "basket" of goods and services — groceries, rent, gasoline, medical care — costs compared to a base period. If the basket costs more today, inflation is rising.

CPI tracks price changes across 8 major categories: Food & Beverage, Housing, Apparel, Transportation, Medical Care, Recreation, Education, and Other.

The Fed watches CPI closely alongside PCE. When CPI runs persistently above 2%, the Fed considers raising rates to cool demand. When it falls sharply, it may signal a weakening economy.

Central Banks · Card 03
FFR
Federal Funds Rate
What is it?

The Federal Funds Rate is the interest rate at which banks lend money to each other overnight. The Federal Reserve sets a target range for this rate — it is the most powerful lever in U.S. monetary policy.

When FFR rises → borrowing costs increase → spending slows → inflation cools.
When FFR falls → cheaper credit → spending rises → economy stimulated.

The FOMC (Federal Open Market Committee) meets 8 times per year to review the rate. Every quarter-point move ripples through mortgages, car loans, credit cards, and bond yields within days.

NFP
Non-Farm Payrolls
What is it?

NFP counts how many jobs the U.S. economy added (or lost) in the prior month, excluding farm workers, private household employees, and non-profit organization employees. Released the first Friday of every month.

NFP > 150K/month → healthy labor market
NFP < 75K/month → potential slowdown
NFP negative → jobs being cut

Markets treat NFP as the most market-moving data release of the month. A strong print can delay rate cuts; a weak one can accelerate them. It's the first hard look at the prior month's economic activity.

PCE
Personal Consumption Expenditures
What is it?

PCE measures how much Americans spend on goods and services, and how those prices change over time. It is the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge — the one they explicitly target at 2%.

PCE differs from CPI: it adjusts for substitution behavior (when prices rise, consumers switch to cheaper alternatives) — making it a more realistic measure of actual purchasing patterns.

When Fed officials say "inflation is near our target," they're talking about PCE, not CPI. Core PCE (excluding food and energy) is the number that drives rate decisions.

Yield Curve
U.S. Treasury Yield Curve (10Y–2Y)
What is it?

The yield curve plots interest rates on U.S. Treasury bonds across different maturities. The 10Y–2Y spread — the difference between 10-year and 2-year yields — is the most-watched recession indicator in finance.

Normal: 10Y yield > 2Y yield (positive spread) → growth expected
Inverted: 10Y yield < 2Y yield (negative spread) → recession signal
Uninverting: spread returning to positive → mixed signal

Every U.S. recession since 1955 was preceded by a yield curve inversion. The curve inverted in mid-2022 and remained inverted for over two years — one of the longest inversions on record.

Purchasing Managers' Index
What is it?

PMI surveys purchasing managers at hundreds of companies, asking whether activity — new orders, production, employment, deliveries — is expanding, contracting, or unchanged versus the prior month.

Above 50 → sector is expanding
Below 50 → sector is contracting
Two separate indexes: Manufacturing PMI and Services PMI

PMI is released on the first business day of each month — making it one of the earliest reads on economic momentum. Because it's a survey, it reflects expectations and intentions, not just lagging results.

PPI
Producer Price Index
What is it?

PPI measures the average change in prices that domestic producers receive for their output — raw materials, intermediate goods, and finished products. It's CPI's upstream cousin: what businesses pay before consumers do.

PPI rising → production costs climbing → CPI likely to follow
PPI falling → deflationary pressure building upstream

PPI is considered a leading indicator for consumer inflation. When input costs rise for producers, those costs typically get passed downstream to consumers within 1–3 months.

VIX
CBOE Volatility Index
What is it?

The VIX measures the market's expectation of S&P 500 volatility over the next 30 days, derived from the prices of S&P 500 options. It is widely called the "fear gauge" of Wall Street.

VIX < 15 → calm, complacent market
VIX 15–25 → moderate uncertainty
VIX > 30 → elevated fear / market stress
VIX > 40 → crisis-level panic (seen in 2008, 2020)

VIX and the S&P 500 tend to move inversely — when stocks fall, VIX spikes. Savvy investors watch VIX for timing: extreme VIX spikes often mark capitulation lows, while extremely low VIX can signal complacency.

DXY
U.S. Dollar Index
What is it?

DXY measures the value of the U.S. dollar against a basket of six major currencies — primarily the Euro (57.6%), Japanese Yen (13.6%), British Pound (11.9%), and three others. A higher DXY means a stronger dollar.

Strong dollar → U.S. imports cheaper · exports more expensive · foreign earnings worth less in USD
Weak dollar → exports competitive · international ETFs get a currency boost

DXY is the global benchmark for dollar strength. It influences commodities (most priced in USD), emerging markets (dollar-denominated debt becomes harder to service), and multinational corporate earnings.

JOLTS
Job Openings & Labor Turnover Survey
What is it?

JOLTS counts how many job openings exist across the U.S. economy at month-end, plus how many workers were hired, quit, or laid off. It paints a picture of labor market dynamics, not just the headline count.

Key ratio: Job Openings ÷ Unemployed Workers
Ratio > 1.0 → more jobs than people looking → tight market
The "Quits Rate" — voluntary resignations — is the Fed's favorite confidence gauge

Fed Chair Powell watches JOLTS closely. When workers quit readily, it means they're confident they can find better jobs — a sign of a hot labor market that could sustain wage inflation.

Initial Jobless Claims (Weekly)
What is it?

Initial jobless claims counts the number of Americans who filed for unemployment benefits for the first time in a given week. It's the most timely labor market indicator available — data is just one week old.

< 250K/week → healthy labor market
250–350K → softening, watch closely
> 350K sustained → recession-level job losses

Released every Thursday morning, claims move markets immediately. The 4-week moving average smooths out holiday noise and weather distortions — it's the number economists focus on, not the weekly print.

Retail Sales measures total receipts at stores that sell consumer goods — from auto dealers to online retailers to gas stations. It is the most direct monthly read on consumer spending, which drives ~70% of U.S. GDP.

Because consumer spending is the economy's engine, retail sales moves can shift Fed expectations instantly. A sustained softening signals demand destruction; a rebound signals resilience.

Credit Spreads
High Yield & Investment Grade Bond Spreads
What is it?

A credit spread is the extra yield investors demand on corporate bonds above equivalent U.S. Treasury yields. It represents the price of default risk — how much extra return the market requires to lend to companies instead of the government.

Tight spreads (<300 bps HY) → market calm, credit conditions easy
Wide spreads (>500 bps HY) → stress, tightening financial conditions
IG spreads typically run 80–150 bps; HY spreads 250–600 bps in normal cycles

Credit spreads widen before recessions as investors price in rising default risk. They are a leading indicator of financial stress — often signaling trouble before it shows up in GDP or unemployment data.

Real Rate
Real Interest Rate (FFR minus Core PCE)
What is it?

The real interest rate strips out inflation from the nominal rate, showing what borrowers and savers actually earn or pay in purchasing-power terms. It tells you whether monetary policy is truly restrictive or just nominally high.

Real Rate = Nominal Rate − Inflation (Core PCE)
Positive real rate → policy is genuinely restrictive
Negative real rate → policy is stimulative (money is "cheap")

During 2022–23, the Fed raised the FFR sharply while inflation ran above it — keeping the real rate negative and policy looser than it appeared. Only when the FFR exceeded inflation did policy become truly restrictive. The neutral real rate is estimated at ~0.5%.

Consumer Confidence
Conference Board Consumer Confidence Index
What is it?

The Conference Board surveys ~3,000 households monthly, asking how they feel about current business conditions, the job market, and their expectations six months ahead. It produces an index benchmarked to 100 (1985 baseline).

Two components:
Present Situation Index — how things feel right now
Expectations Index — outlook for jobs and income in 6 months
When Present > Expectations: consumers confident today but worried about tomorrow

Consumer confidence drives spending, which drives ~70% of GDP. When it falls sharply, watch retail and consumer discretionary ETFs. The gap between Present Situation and Expectations is often more revealing than the headline number.

What is it?

The U of Michigan surveys ~500 households twice a month, measuring how Americans feel about their personal finances, buying conditions, and economic outlook. Released in preliminary and final form, it is the Fed's preferred sentiment gauge.

Breakeven Inflation
10-Year TIPS Breakeven Inflation Rate
What is it?

The breakeven inflation rate is what the bond market expects average annual inflation to be over the next 10 years. It's derived from the yield difference between regular Treasury bonds and TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities).

Breakeven Rate = 10Y Nominal Treasury Yield − 10Y TIPS Real Yield
If breakeven > Fed's 2% target → market doubts the Fed can hit its target
If breakeven < 2% → market expects below-target inflation

Unlike CPI or PCE, which measure inflation that has already happened, breakeven inflation shows what the market expects. It's a real-time signal watched by every fixed-income investor and Fed official daily.

Income & Spending
Personal Income & Personal Consumption Expenditures (BEA)
What is it?

The BEA's monthly Personal Income and Outlays report tracks how much Americans earned and spent. It's released on the same day as PCE — in fact, PCE inflation is derived from this same data. Together they tell the full consumer story.

Personal Income growth > Spending growth → savings rate rises → disinflationary
Spending growth > Income growth → consumers drawing down savings → unsustainable
Personal Savings Rate = (Income − Spending) / Income

When income growth outpaces spending, consumers are building a cushion — a healthy dynamic. When spending outpaces income, it signals either credit-fueled consumption or savings depletion, both of which are unsustainable.

U-6
Broad (Real) Unemployment Rate
What is it?

U-6 is the broadest official measure of unemployment. It counts not just the jobless (U-3 headline rate), but also discouraged workers who've stopped looking and people working part-time who want full-time work. It reveals labor market slack hidden in the headline.

U-3 (Headline): Unemployed ÷ Labor Force
U-6 (Broad): (Unemployed + Marginally Attached + Part-Time for Economic Reasons) ÷ Labor Force
Gap between U-6 and U-3 = degree of underemployment in the economy

What is it?

LFPR is the percentage of the civilian non-institutional population (age 16+) that is either employed or actively looking for work. It measures how much of America's potential workforce is actually in the labor market.

LFPR = Labor Force ÷ Civilian Non-Institutional Population × 100
Declining LFPR → workers leaving workforce → unemployment rate falls "artificially"
Prime-Age LFPR (25–54 year olds) = cleanest signal, less affected by aging demographics

The U.S. LFPR peaked at 67.3% in early 2000, dropped post-2008, and fell further during COVID. Its recovery is a long-term structural story — aging demographics make a full return to pre-2000 levels unlikely.

What is it?

Four times a year, each of the 18 FOMC members submits their anonymous projection for where the Federal Funds Rate should be at year-end. Plotted as dots on a chart, this "dot plot" gives markets a direct window into Fed thinking on the rate path.

QT
Quantitative Tightening / Fed Balance Sheet Runoff
What is it?

Quantitative Tightening is the Fed's process of shrinking its balance sheet by allowing Treasury bonds and mortgage-backed securities (MBS) to mature without reinvesting the proceeds. It is the opposite of QE (Quantitative Easing), which expanded the balance sheet by purchasing these assets.

QE: Fed buys bonds → adds money to system → lower yields, more credit
QT: Fed lets bonds mature → removes money from system → upward pressure on yields
Balance sheet peaked at $9.0T (Apr 2022); now ~$6.8T — reduced by ~$2.2T

QT puts upward pressure on long-term Treasury yields by reducing a major buyer from the market. It's a form of tightening that works independently from rate hikes, making the total policy stance tighter than the FFR alone suggests.

What is it?

M2 measures the total supply of money in the U.S. economy — physical cash, checking accounts, savings accounts, money market funds, and small CDs. It is the broadest widely-used measure of "how much money is sloshing around."

M1: Cash + checking accounts (most liquid)

Milton Friedman: "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" — M2 growth predicts inflation with a 12–18 month lag

M2 surged 27% in 2020–21 (COVID stimulus) and then contracted in 2022–23 as the Fed tightened. Its recent re-acceleration to ~4% growth is being watched closely as a potential leading indicator of renewed inflation pressure.

SLOOS
Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey on Bank Lending Practices
What is it?

Every quarter, the Fed surveys ~80 large U.S. banks and ~24 foreign banks on their lending standards. The key question: are you tightening or easing credit conditions for business loans, mortgages, and consumer credit? The net percentage is the signal.

Net % Tightening = % of banks tightening − % of banks easing
Positive number → more banks making credit harder to get
Negative number → more banks loosening credit (stimulative)
SLOOS leads economic activity by ~6–9 months

When banks tighten, businesses struggle to borrow and invest. SLOOS has predicted every recession since the 1990s — a sustained move above +30% net tightening is a reliable red flag. Fed Chair Powell references SLOOS in almost every press conference.

CAPE
Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings Ratio (Shiller PE10)
What is it?

CAPE divides the S&P 500 price by the average of the past 10 years of real (inflation-adjusted) earnings. Developed by Nobel laureate Robert Shiller, it smooths out business cycle volatility to give a cleaner picture of whether stocks are cheap or expensive historically.

CAPE = S&P 500 Price ÷ 10-Year Average Real Earnings
Historical average CAPE: ~17
CAPE > 30 → historically expensive; low expected 10-year forward returns
CAPE < 15 → historically cheap; high expected forward returns

CAPE is not a market timing tool — it has been "elevated" since the 1990s without predicting exact peak dates. But its long-run predictive power for 10-year returns is well documented: high CAPE → below-average returns over the next decade.

Trade Balance
U.S. International Trade in Goods & Services
What is it?

The trade balance measures the difference between U.S. exports and imports. When imports exceed exports (the U.S. norm), the result is a trade deficit. The U.S. has run a persistent trade deficit for decades — it is the world's largest goods importer.

Trade Balance = Exports − Imports
Negative = trade deficit (U.S. buys more from the world than it sells)
Widening deficit → GDP math drag (net exports subtract from GDP)
But reflects strong domestic demand — not necessarily a sign of weakness

The trade deficit is politically charged but economically nuanced. A widening deficit can actually signal a strong economy (consumers buying imported goods). However, tariffs aimed at reducing the deficit often trigger retaliatory actions that hurt U.S. exporters.

Tariff Rate
U.S. Effective Tariff Rate on Imports
What is it?

The effective tariff rate is the average tax the U.S. government collects on all imported goods — calculated as total tariff revenue divided by total import value. It reflects the aggregate impact of all tariff schedules across all trading partners.

Effective Tariff Rate = Tariff Revenue Collected ÷ Total Import Value
A 10% blanket tariff doesn't raise the effective rate by 10% immediately — some imports are exempt, others face retaliatory tariffs that reduce import volume
Pass-through to consumers: typically 80–100% within 6–12 months

The U.S. effective tariff rate averaged ~2.5% for two decades before 2025. The sweeping tariff actions of 2025–26 have pushed it toward levels last seen in the 1930s (Smoot-Hawley era), representing the most significant trade policy shift in nearly a century.

USD/CNY
U.S. Dollar vs. Chinese Yuan (Renminbi)
What is it?

USD/CNY measures how many Chinese yuan one U.S. dollar buys. A higher number means a weaker yuan (dollar stronger). China's central bank (PBOC) sets a daily "fixing" rate and allows the yuan to trade within a ±2% band around it — making this a managed, not freely floating, exchange rate.

Stronger yuan (lower USD/CNY) → Chinese exports more expensive · easier to import · PBOC loosening
Weaker yuan (higher USD/CNY) → Chinese exports cheaper · offsets some tariff impact · capital outflow risk
Offshore CNH rate (freely traded in Hong Kong) often diverges from onshore CNY

The yuan is the global barometer of U.S.–China trade tensions. When tariffs escalate, China often allows controlled yuan depreciation to offset competitiveness losses. Rapid depreciation beyond 7.50 historically triggers EM currency contagion and global risk-off moves.
