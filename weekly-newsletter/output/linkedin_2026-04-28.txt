The S&P closed up 0.12% yesterday. Don't trust it.

Here's why the headline number is the least interesting thing that happened in markets Monday.

---

Bonds didn't buy it. The 10Y yield crept to 4.34% - not because growth is accelerating, but because an oil shock is embedding stagflation expectations into every fixed income maturity. When equities celebrate and bonds don't, one of them is wrong.

Gold fell 1%. That's not a bearish signal - it's profit-taking into a risk-off overnight. Real yields are rising because the energy shock is raising the cost of everything, and the traditional inflation hedge is briefly yielding to that pressure.

Crude didn't just rise. WTI surged 3.47% to $99.71 - one dollar from $100. That's not a spike. That's the market pricing a sealed Strait of Hormuz for weeks, not days.

---

BP reported profits more than doubled on Iran-war oil prices. That's the corporate sector confirming what futures have been saying: the Hormuz blockade is a real earnings story, not a headline risk premium. The energy sector is the only one right now with a fundamental tailwind independent of Fed policy or tech multiples.

---

Here's the second-order story nobody is covering: airlines, trucking, and consumer staples are staring at a margin compression cycle that will show up in Q2 guidance. The BoJ already halved its 2026 growth forecast - resource-poor Asia absorbs the oil shock first, but the margin squeeze travels. Five Magnificent Seven companies report this week, and Nasdaq futures are down 0.6% heading into that gauntlet.

---

Equities said the economy is fine. Bonds said stagflation. Crude said the Hormuz tollbooth just slammed shut.

Which market got it right? The Touska seizure didn't just end a diplomatic opening - it removed the optionality that kept the supply premium capped. Rubio called Tehran's written proposal insufficient. The FOMC opens today with Powell walking into that problem.
