The equity market sold off yesterday. That's not the story.

The story is that bonds sold off too. And gold barely moved. And crude posted its fifth consecutive daily gain.

That cross-asset pattern has a name. It's stagflation.

Here's what each market was actually saying:

The bond market gave no comfort. The 10-year yield rose 2.9 basis points to 4.323% as equities sold off - both moving in the same direction. When stocks fall and bonds fall together, that is not a rotation into safety. That is a repricing of inflation risk. CPI just printed 3.3% headline on an 11% monthly energy surge. Core sits at 2.6% - the gap tells you this is entirely energy-driven. The Fed can see the difference but cannot cut into a 3.3% headline print without torching its credibility. Rate cuts are off the table.

Gold closed at $4,707 and did nothing. In a genuine stagflation scare, gold should be running. Its inertia is the anomaly in yesterday's tape. Real yields are creeping higher and capping the upside. Institutional money is sitting on its hedge and watching. That tells you the dollar hasn't broken down enough to give gold a tailwind yet - or the move is coming and the smart money is waiting for a cleaner entry.

WTI closed at $97.23 - fifth consecutive daily gain. Brent is above $106. The Strait of Hormuz has been locked for two months. Roughly 20% of global oil trade is bottled up. The Islamabad peace talks are off the table. This is not a sentiment trade. This is a structural supply shock with no visible exit, feeding directly into US inflation with no Fed offset available.

Then earnings split the AI tape in half.

Texas Instruments surged 19% - its best day since 2000. The driver: AI hardware demand is accelerating and showing up in earnings. TSMC hit a record high overnight. The Nikkei is approaching 60,000 on semiconductor strength. The chip cycle is real and it is now confirmed by multiple earnings prints.

ServiceNow and IBM went the opposite direction. Both disappointed on AI spending clarity. The software complex collapsed. The market is repricing the gap between AI infrastructure winners and AI application question marks. The divergence is not subtle and it is not a one-quarter event.

The story nobody is covering: airlines.

Brent above $106 means aviation fuel is a crisis for European carriers. Lufthansa already slashed 20,000 summer flights, calling them "unprofitable" due to fuel costs. That pressure hits every airline with European operations. The Hormuz story isn't just oil. It's aviation fuel, logistics costs, fertilizer production, food supply chains. Equity markets are pricing the first-order effects. The second and third-order effects have not arrived yet.

So which market got it right?

Bonds and crude are both saying this is structural. The 10-year won't rally until the Hormuz closure ends or core inflation cracks. Crude won't retreat until ships can physically move through the strait again. Gold's flat line is the most interesting signal of all - institutional money is not piling into the classic inflation hedge, which means either they're waiting for the dollar to break or they don't yet believe the full stagflation thesis has arrived.

One of these markets is wrong. Historically, it isn't crude.
