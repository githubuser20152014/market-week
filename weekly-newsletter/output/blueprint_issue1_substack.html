The Blueprint | The one decision that controls 90% of your returns

A new Wednesday series: practical investing guides for people who are serious about getting this right.

You probably already have money invested. A 401(k) through work, maybe an IRA. There's a number on a statement somewhere — but if someone asked how your portfolio is actually set up, you'd have to look it up.

A decision has already been made on your behalf, either by you years ago or by a default enrollment setting. That decision — how your money is divided between stocks, bonds, and cash — turns out to be the single most important factor in your long-term returns. Not which funds you picked. Not which companies are in them. The ratio.

A 1986 study by Brinson, Hood & Beebower tracked 91 large pension funds for a decade and found that 91.5% of return variability was explained by asset allocation policy — not stock selection, not market timing. The ratio is the decision. Everything else is noise around the edges.

Issue #1 of The Blueprint series covers what this means in practice: why it matters more at 48 than it did at 28, what the $170K gap between a 70/30 and a 40/60 portfolio looks like over 17 years, and one action to take this week.
