The ceasefire is over. WTI jumped 4.1% overnight. And the S&P 500 added 0.22%.

Read that again.

---

The Friday close looked like a shrug: Dow up 0.72%, S&P up 0.22%, Nasdaq up 0.20%. Equities decided the Hormuz ceasefire collapse is a "supply disruption" story, not an "Armageddon" story. Maybe they're right. But three other markets voted differently.

Crude: WTI closed at $90.94, up 4.1%. The war premium that evaporated over 60 days of diplomatic theater repriced in a single session. The Strait of Hormuz is a toll booth again, and it is collecting.

Gold: Down 0.70% to $4,528. During a genuine geopolitical escalation. Gold fell when the shooting started. That is either the market signaling confidence this stays contained, or it is telling you that real yields are the dominant force right now. Either read is bearish for the "safe haven" thesis.

Bonds: The 10-year yield barely moved, closing at 4.453%. The bond market watched a 60-day ceasefire end in live fire and moved 0.2 basis points. Kevin Warsh's Fed has established higher-for-longer as the posture on a 3.8% PCE print, and the bond market has accepted that as the ceiling regardless of what happens in the Strait.

---

The corporate proof point that makes the macro thesis concrete: Berkshire Hathaway just made a $6.8 billion housing bet, acquiring Taylor Morrison. Greg Abel's first major deal as Buffett's successor is a direct bet that 30-year mortgage rates stabilize. That is a bet against the Warsh higher-for-longer thesis. Berkshire is not a speculative outfit. When they put $6.8 billion into housing, they are telling you something about where they think rates are going.

---

The second-order story nobody is covering: the small-cap divergence. The Russell 2000 fell 0.59% on Friday while the large-cap indices rose. Small-caps are the canaries of domestic credit: rate-sensitive borrowers who also get hit when oil prices rise and consumer spending softens. The Warsh Fed plus a WTI shock is a double squeeze on the small-cap universe. That divergence is not noise. It is the leading indicator the equity bulls are ignoring.

---

Which market got it right?

Equities are pricing a supply disruption. Bonds are pricing higher-for-longer. Gold is pricing nothing. The honest answer: crude got it right. The Strait of Hormuz is a chokepoint for roughly 20% of global oil supply. The war premium does not vanish on a ceasefire. It waits. Now it is back.

The real risk is not the oil price itself. It is what $90+ oil does to the consumer spending data that feeds into the next PCE print. That is the feedback loop that could force Warsh's hand later in the summer, and the market is not pricing that scenario yet.
