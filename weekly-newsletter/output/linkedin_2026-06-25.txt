The Nasdaq fell 0.43% yesterday. That is the headline. It is not the story.

The story is that three other markets looked at the same session and disagreed completely.

Bonds: The 10-year Treasury yield barely moved, closing at 4.45%, down just one basis point. If this was genuine macro fear, duration buyers would have shown up. They did not. Yesterday was not about recession risk or credit stress. It was about a single earnings narrative creating a sentiment vacuum in chip names.

Gold: Gold closed at $3,996, unable to crack $4,000, going nowhere. Safe-haven assets catch bids when the macro picture darkens. Gold sat on its hands. The market was not scared. It was confused.

Crude: WTI dropped to $69.79, but not because of growth fears. The U.S.-Iran memorandum on IAEA inspections doubled Strait of Hormuz tanker traffic in 48 hours and evaporated a geopolitical war premium that had taken months to build. Iraq is now threatening to breach its OPEC quota. The oil move was a supply story, not a demand signal.

Then Micron posted earnings. Blowout. Margin leadership past Nvidia and Meta on AI memory demand. Pre-market surge of 17%. KOSPI reversed 5.42% overnight. The AI infrastructure thesis was never broken. It was interrupted for 48 hours while one stock missed expectations.

Here is the second-order story nobody is running: WTI at $69.79 is not just an energy sector event. It is a manufacturing and logistics story. Lower energy input costs expand margins for every industrial company and freight operator that is not an oil producer. The capital rotation is already happening. The headline has not caught up.

Which market got it right? Bonds said this was not a macro event. Gold agreed. Crude was telling a supply story. The one market that panicked was tech, specifically chip names. Micron just proved them wrong overnight. When three asset classes vote against a selloff narrative and the corporate data confirms it the next day, one of them was right, and it was not equities.
