Oil crashed 10% yesterday. Equities hit all-time highs. Gold surged 3.7%.

Pick one of those and tell me what kind of session it was. You can't. Because the market sent three completely different signals at once, and most people only read the one that confirmed what they already thought.

Trump paused Project Freedom - the U.S. naval mission in the Strait of Hormuz - and the war premium in crude evaporated in hours. Brent had been near $114 just days after missile strikes on the Port of Fujairah. Yesterday it didn't matter. WTI closed at $91.68, down 10.4%, the sharpest single-session drop since the early pandemic shock. China is pressing Iran for a Hormuz reopening ahead of the Trump-Xi summit. The geopolitical trade unwound fast. But gold didn't get the memo - and that's the only thing that matters today.

Here's my read:

The equity rally is real. The S&P 500 closed at a fresh all-time high of 7,259 (+0.8%). Nasdaq added 1.0% to 25,326. The Russell 2000 outperformed at +1.75%, which matters - small caps don't lead in a fake rally. AMD printed a 15% single-session gain on a data center revenue blowout, dragging the Nasdaq higher with it. Disney added 5% on streaming and parks. Risk appetite was genuine.

And yet. Gold hit $4,723, up 3.7% on the same day. The 10-year yield fell only 3 bps to 4.416% - not the kind of bond rally you'd expect if the market truly believed the inflation war was over. The dollar fell 0.78%. That combination - equities up, gold up, bonds flat, dollar down - is not a risk-on session. That's a stagflation hedge wearing a growth mask.

The FOMC is split 8-4 on rate hikes. PCE is running at 4.5%. Central banks bought 244 tonnes of gold in Q1. The Fed cannot hike because growth is fragile, and cannot cut because inflation is sticky. Every dip toward $5,000 gold gets structurally bought. The bond market knows this. Yields barely moved while equities celebrated. One of them is pricing the world correctly. I know which one I trust.
