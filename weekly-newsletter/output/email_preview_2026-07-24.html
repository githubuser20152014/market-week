The Morning Brief - 2026-07-24

Daily Edition · Market intelligence at the open

The Brief

Three converging shocks hit markets yesterday: Alphabet and Tesla torched post-earnings on AI capex blowout concerns, Brent crude punched through $100 on Red Sea tanker attacks, and the White House dropped sweeping new tariffs on 60 trade partners. The cross-asset read is unambiguous: gold holding near $4,062 while the dollar barely moved and 10-year yields jumped 4.6 basis points tells you the bond market is pricing in stagflation, not a soft landing. Futures are bouncing modestly pre-open, but with KOSPI down 5.7% and Nikkei off 2.7%, the overnight signal is a relief rally built on thin air.

The S&P 500 closed at 7,408.30, down 1.21%, with the Nasdaq leading the damage at -2.15% to 25,137. The Dow shed 0.97%. Small caps underperformed again, the Russell 2000 off 0.67% to 2,940. The 10-year Treasury yield climbed 4.6 basis points to 4.703%, gold added 0.38% to close at $4,062, and WTI crude dropped 3.46% to $89, despite intraday Brent spiking to $102. The dollar index was essentially flat at 101.36.

Alphabet and Tesla reported Q2 results that confirmed the market's worst fear: AI infrastructure spending is accelerating while near-term ROI remains opaque. Think of it like a commercial real estate developer pouring concrete on a skyscraper with no signed leases. Alphabet's capex guidance reset expectations across every cloud hyperscaler. Simultaneously, the White House enacted 10%-12.5% tariffs across 60 trade partners, adding a fresh input-cost layer on top of energy inflation already running hot. The Red Sea tanker strikes, now in their 12th consecutive night, pushed Brent briefly above $100, which is the threshold where headline CPI gets re-priced across importing economies. The Fed does not cut into $100 oil and rising import tariffs. The 10-year knows this, which is why bonds never bought the equity rally of the prior weeks.

What it means for you

The playbook here separates into two buckets. Defensives and real assets are the only logical shelter: gold (GLD) held its bid even as risk assets cracked, signaling genuine safe-haven demand rather than a tactical rotation. Energy (XLE) is a split story: upstream producers benefit from $89-plus WTI with potential for more upside if the Bab-el-Mandeb Strait situation worsens, but yesterday's WTI pullback from intraday highs shows the market is also pricing in demand destruction from tariff-driven slowdowns. Avoid mega-cap tech (QQQ) until Microsoft and Amazon report next week and clarify whether capex discipline is possible. Rate-sensitive sectors like utilities (XLU) and REITs (XLRE) face headwind from the yield jump. Defense (ITA) gets a structural tailwind from both the Middle East conflict and the NATO Brussels meeting Monday.

S&P futures are up 0.27% pre-open to 7,465, a modest bounce off yesterday's lows. KOSPI's 5.7% overnight collapse is the sharpest single-market signal in the data, reflecting South Korea's acute exposure to both tariff disruption and tech export contraction. Europe is flat in early session, providing no directional conviction. The key swing factor today is the 10:00 AM New Home Sales print. A weak number would compound the stagflation narrative by confirming that 4.70% yields are already biting into rate-sensitive household demand. Watch whether the S&P can hold above 7,400 on any early bounce fade.

Positioning Notes

  • GLD: Stay long or add on any pre-market dip. Gold held its bid even as equities sold off hard and yields rose, a divergence that signals institutional buying into macro uncertainty. If the Red Sea situation produces another tanker headline before noon, gold likely retests $4,100.
  • XLE: Conditional hold. WTI at $89 with Brent volatility near $100 supports upstream producers, but yesterday's crude selloff from intraday highs suggests demand-destruction fears are real. Add only on a WTI reclaim above $91; cut exposure if crude slips below $87 on tariff-driven growth concerns.
  • QQQ: Avoid new longs ahead of Microsoft and Amazon earnings next week. The Alphabet and Tesla capex reset is not isolated; it reprices the entire hyperscaler cohort. Any pre-open bounce in Nasdaq futures is a gift to trim existing positions, not a re-entry signal.
  • ITA: Defense ETF warrants fresh attention. The 12th consecutive night of U.S. strikes on Iranian assets, NATO meeting in Brussels Monday, and zero diplomatic progress at the Manila ASEAN forum build a multi-week revenue visibility case for defense contractors. Entry on any broad market weakness today.
  • TLT: Do not chase a bond rally here. The 4.703% 10-year reflects tariff-driven inflation repricing, not a one-day anomaly. TLT rallies are likely to be sold until the Fed signals it can look through tariff CPI. Sit out long-duration Treasuries until yields stabilize above 4.75% and show a reversal pattern.
