The Morning Brief - 2026-07-21

Daily Edition · Market intelligence at the open

The Brief

Night seven of U.S. airstrikes on Iran, and the market's response was to buy stocks in the pre-market and gold in the same breath, like a trader hedging his own optimism. S&P 500 closed at 7,443.28, down 0.19%. Dow shed 307 points to 51,839.26, off 0.59%, and Russell 2000 dropped 0.67% to 2,942.43, because small-caps feel tariff pain first and complain about it loudest. Gold ripped to a record $4,068.30, up 1.45%, while the 10-year yield climbed to 4.598%, up nearly 6 basis points. Gold and yields rising together used to mean something was broken. Apparently it still does.

Three things did the damage. Seventh consecutive night of U.S. strikes on Iran, with the Strait of Hormuz still a live fuse and commercial shipping rerouting around the entire Arabian Peninsula, because nothing says business as usual like tankers taking the scenic route. Jamie Dimon said he wouldn't buy stocks or Treasuries at current prices, which from the man who runs the country's largest bank is less a warning than a diagnosis. And Trump slapped 50% tariffs on select Canadian goods, just so trade friction didn't get lonely next to the geopolitical kind. WTI crude somehow slipped 0.66% to $82.68 through all of it, which means oil has either priced in peace or stopped watching the news.

S&P futures are up 0.54%, Nasdaq futures up 1.36%, both betting today is about earnings, not Iran. General Motors reports before the bell and gets to referee the tariff-pass-through argument for everyone else. Watch 4.65% on the 10-year: cross it and the "stocks rally through anything" thesis gets a very public retest. Overnight, APAC decided none of this mattered: Nikkei up 3.26%, KOSPI up 3.56%, proving optimism travels faster across the Pacific than bad news does.

What it means for you

Gold at $4,068 with yields rising is the tell, not a footnote. Higher yields are supposed to choke gold's appeal, since a non-yielding asset should lose to a 4.6% Treasury every time. Instead gold is winning anyway, which means the Strait of Hormuz premium currently outranks the entire U.S. bond market's opinion. That makes (GLD) and (IAU) active positions right now, not insurance you forgot you were paying for. (XLE) is the trap: WTI is down on the day despite a live war near a major chokepoint, so either the oil market knows something it isn't saying, or it's about to be very wrong very fast. Wait for confirmation, don't front-run it. (IWM) stays untouched: small-caps are eating both the tariff headline and the credit tightening implied by 4.6% yields, and nothing in today's setup gives them a reason to bounce. (ITA) is the quiet beneficiary of a war entering its second week, since defense budgets don't get cut mid-airstrike.

The session's real swing factor is General Motors earnings before the bell, which settles whether tariff costs are landing on consumers or just on headlines. If the 10-year punches through 4.65% intraday, the "buy everything" premise cracks and today's futures gains evaporate fast. APAC's enthusiasm, Nikkei up 3.26% and KOSPI up 3.56%, is either a preview of the U.S. session's tech optimism or a market that hasn't checked the news since Tokyo closed. Watch for anything out of the UN Security Council emergency session: one diplomatic sentence moves oil, gold, and defense names at once, and not gently.

The One Trade

GLD - Long

Gold is rising into a risk-on futures open while yields climb, a decoupling that signals the Strait of Hormuz geopolitical bid is overpowering normal rate headwinds. That kind of dislocation rarely reverses intraday without a concrete diplomatic breakthrough.

Confirms: GLD holds above $375 (the equivalent of spot gold sustaining above $4,050) through the first hour of trading with no confirmed ceasefire headline out of the UN emergency session. Kill switch: A concrete ceasefire announcement or Hormuz shipping normalization signal out of UN Security Council proceedings, that breaks the fear bid and sends gold back below $3,980 fast.

Positioning Notes

  • Hold or add GLD: gold is breaking out against rising yields, which only happens when geopolitical fear dominates rate logic. The Strait of Hormuz escalation is the specific driver. Stay long unless Hormuz shipping normalizes with a confirmed ceasefire.
  • Watch XLE conditionally: WTI pulled back to $82.68 despite active conflict near a major shipping chokepoint. If Houthi threats to Saudi shipping materialize or Hormuz traffic data drops, energy reprices fast. Enter XLE on a WTI close back above $85, not before.
  • Reduce or avoid IWM: the Russell 2000 is down 0.67% and small-caps face a double headwind from Canadian tariff fallout and tightening credit conditions implied by the 10-year at 4.598%. No clear catalyst for a reversal today.
  • Consider ITA for a tactical position: U.S. conducting seventh night of Iran airstrikes, with an emergency UN session scheduled, means defense budget signals are building. ITA holds Lockheed, RTX, and Northrop. This is a geopolitical risk premium trade, not a valuation trade.
  • Nasdaq longs (QQQ) are viable intraday if GM earnings are clean and tech futures hold pre-market gains, but the Jamie Dimon warning and rising yields set a ceiling. Trim into strength above 7,530 on the S&P futures rather than chasing the open pop.
