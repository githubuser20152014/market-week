KOSPI Cratered 7.89% Overnight. Wall Street Shrugged and Bought the Dip.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

Korea's stock market just had its worst session in memory, down nearly 8% overnight, and the reaction from US futures was a shrug and a half-point green candle. That is either supreme confidence or supreme denial.

Meanwhile the actual US session closed lower across the board: the S&P 500 fell 0.2% to 7,483, the Nasdaq dropped 0.7% to 26,040, and gold ripped to $4,080 while Fed Chair Kevin Warsh refused to say a single useful word about rates. Somebody in this market is wrong, and Nonfarm Payrolls settles the argument in ninety minutes.

Gold already made its call. The rest of the market is stalling for time.

Here's my read:

The selling in tech and small-caps traces to two catalysts landing in the same 24 hours. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh declined to offer forward guidance on rates, and ISM Manufacturing and ADP private payrolls both missed estimates, the kind of one-two punch that turns "soft landing" into a phrase people stop saying out loud. Gold's move above $4,080 is the tell: the economy sneezes and gold's already reaching for the tissues. Oil keeps sliding too, down nearly 2% to $67.24 for a fourth straight weekly loss, as Kushner and Witkoff's Doha talks with Iran drain the geopolitical premium out of crude. And then there's KOSPI, down 7.89% in a single session, a move that looks like Korea's own market plumbing breaking rather than a macro signal, but it's still the kind of number that belongs in a headline whether or not it belongs in your portfolio.
