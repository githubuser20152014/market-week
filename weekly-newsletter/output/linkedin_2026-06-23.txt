The Nasdaq fell 1.3% on Friday. Small-caps gained 0.83%. You might think the market is fine.

It is not fine.

The overnight data is the tell. Seoul's KOSPI triggered a circuit breaker at minus 8% and still closed down 10%. SK Hynix fell 12%. Samsung fell 7.5%. Foreign investors pulled $2.6 billion from Korean equities before lunch. The Nikkei fell 3.5%. Nasdaq futures are pointing down 2.8% into Monday's open. This is the AI chip rally unwinding in real time, and the correlation to US mega-cap tech is not subtle.

The dissenting markets told the story first.

Bonds said nothing. The 10Y yield fell just 1.2 basis points to 4.451% on a day when equities were already under pressure. A genuine flight to safety would have moved the long end. It didn't. That's the Fed's hawkish hold making itself visible: higher-for-longer is a discount rate, and every uncut basis point is a tax on Nasdaq multiples.

Gold fell 1% to $4,140. Not because geopolitics improved - the Swiss talks produced another unsigned draft and Trump publicly questioned whether Iran would redirect oil windfall profits to military rebuilding - but because the dollar ticked up 0.22%. Gold is still holding structurally above $4,100. The dip is not a breakdown.

Crude slid 1.36% to $73.80. The US Treasury authorized sweeping Iran oil sanctions waivers through August, adding real supply to the market. WTI fell. But Trump's simultaneous Strait of Hormuz warning prevents a clean short. Two opposing forces. Neither wins cleanly right now.

The corporate proof point: Target's chair Brian Cornell just received the lowest shareholder support ever recorded for an S&P company chair. Investors called it "reward for failure." When governance scorecards start printing results like that, it signals something broader about how capital allocators are feeling about management accountability. It is not just about one retailer.

The story nobody is covering: Washington is actively fighting Brazil for China's soybean market. That's an agricultural trade war embedded inside the Iran-Hormuz geopolitical narrative. While everyone watches crude prices and ceasefire drafts, the protein supply chain is quietly being redrawn. This one compounds slowly until it doesn't.

Which market got it right?

Bonds called it. The 10Y barely moved because the Fed is not cutting. Nasdaq futures are opening down 2.8% today. The AI chip rally in Seoul that ended with a circuit breaker is not a Korean-specific event - it's the preview of what happens when a momentum trade runs out of incremental buyers. The rotation from long-duration growth into domestic cyclicals that showed up Friday is a thesis, not a one-day data point.
