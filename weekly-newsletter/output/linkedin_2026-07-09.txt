The Dow dropped 1.09% to 52,348 yesterday. Gold jumped 1.03% to $4,113. Nasdaq futures are up 0.57% this morning like none of it happened. Here's why you shouldn't trust the tech tape over the other three.

Start with gold. A 1% single-session move on a day the dollar also weakened is not a hedge fund rebalancing, it's a market pricing three separate threats at once: a collapsed Iran ceasefire, a Fed that can't agree on anything, and a dollar too tired to defend itself. Gold doesn't move that cleanly by accident.

Then there's the dollar index, which slipped to 100.97 despite a genuinely hawkish set of FOMC minutes. Hawkish minutes are supposed to support the currency. They didn't. That's not noise, that's the market deciding the Fed's hawkishness doesn't matter if the Fed can't agree on a direction to be hawkish about.

Crude told a quieter version of the same story. WTI added 0.34% to $73.77, a modest move for a session where the Strait of Hormuz went from "watch this" to "US airstrikes on 80+ targets." A market pricing a real supply shock does not move 34 basis points. This is oil holding its breath, not oil that has decided anything yet.

The corporate proof point that got buried under the geopolitics: PepsiCo missed earnings, flagging North American consumers pulling back on spending. That's not a snacks story. That's a household balance sheet story landing in the same week a divided Fed just told you it isn't in a hurry to make borrowing cheaper again.

The angle nobody's covering: the Strait of Hormuz isn't just an oil chokepoint, it's the transit route for roughly 20% of global LNG trade, most of it Qatari cargo bound for a Europe that spent three years rebuilding its energy security around exactly that supply line. An extended disruption doesn't just move WTI, it reopens a gas-security conversation European utilities thought was closed.

So which market got yesterday right: small caps and gold, which priced real stress, or the Nasdaq, which is pricing a Tuesday? I'm with gold. A tech tape that shrugs off an active Gulf escalation and a split Fed isn't confidence, it's a rally that hasn't checked its phone yet.
