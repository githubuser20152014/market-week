## The Brief

Night seven of U.S. airstrikes on Iran, and the market's response was to buy stocks in the pre-market and gold in the same breath, like a trader hedging his own optimism. **S&P 500 closed at 7,443.28, down 0.19%**. **Dow shed 307 points to 51,839.26, off 0.59%**, and **Russell 2000 dropped 0.67% to 2,942.43**, because small-caps feel tariff pain first and complain about it loudest. **Gold ripped to a record $4,068.30, up 1.45%**, while the **10-year yield climbed to 4.598%**, up nearly 6 basis points. Gold and yields rising together used to mean something was broken. Apparently it still does.

Three things did the damage. Seventh consecutive night of **U.S. strikes on Iran**, with the **Strait of Hormuz** still a live fuse and commercial shipping rerouting around the entire Arabian Peninsula, because nothing says business as usual like tankers taking the scenic route. **Jamie Dimon said he wouldn't buy stocks or Treasuries at current prices**, which from the man who runs the country's largest bank is less a warning than a diagnosis. And Trump slapped **50% tariffs on select Canadian goods**, just so trade friction didn't get lonely next to the geopolitical kind. WTI crude somehow slipped 0.66% to $82.68 through all of it, which means oil has either priced in peace or stopped watching the news.

S&P futures are up 0.54%, Nasdaq futures up 1.36%, both betting today is about earnings, not Iran. **General Motors reports before the bell** and gets to referee the tariff-pass-through argument for everyone else. Watch **4.65% on the 10-year**: cross it and the "stocks rally through anything" thesis gets a very public retest. Overnight, APAC decided none of this mattered: **Nikkei up 3.26%, KOSPI up 3.56%**, proving optimism travels faster across the Pacific than bad news does.

Instead gold is winning anyway, which means the **Strait of Hormuz** premium currently outranks the entire U.S. bond market's opinion. That makes ([GLD](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/GLD)) and ([IAU](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/IAU)) active positions right now, not insurance you forgot you were paying for. ([XLE](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/XLE)) is the trap: WTI is down on the day despite a live war near a major chokepoint, so either the oil market knows something it isn't saying, or it's about to be very wrong very fast. Wait for confirmation, don't front-run it. ([IWM](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/IWM)) stays untouched: small-caps are eating both the tariff headline and the credit tightening implied by 4.6% yields, and nothing in today's setup gives them a reason to bounce. ([ITA](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/ITA)) is the quiet beneficiary of a war entering its second week, since defense budgets don't get cut mid-airstrike.

The session's real swing factor is **General Motors earnings before the bell**, which settles whether tariff costs are landing on consumers or just on headlines. If the 10-year punches through **4.65%** intraday, the "buy everything" premise cracks and today's futures gains evaporate fast. APAC's enthusiasm, **Nikkei up 3.26% and KOSPI up 3.56%**, is either a preview of the U.S. session's tech optimism or a market that hasn't checked the news since Tokyo closed. Watch for anything out of the UN Security Council emergency session: one diplomatic sentence moves oil, gold, and defense names at once, and not gently.
