Equities are buying the 60-day ceasefire fiction, but the smart money is voting with gold at $4,570. Here's why you shouldn't trust the headlines just yet.

The Strait of Hormuz war premium is deflating as WTI crude shed 2.2% on supplies. APAC indices like the Nikkei (+2.5%) and KOSPI (+3.6%) hit record highs on the supplying relief. But gold's refusal to pull back while the dollar holds flat is the institutional tell. Sophisticated money isn't closing hedges; it's just paying more for the vault space.

Exxon is already warning that inventories could hit dangerously low levels within weeks. The ceasefire is a supply relief drug, but the looming inventory hangover is real. Meanwhile, a Russian drone hitting NATO member Romania and fresh Iran missile reports are reminders that the "geopolitical floor" is made of glass.

The second-order story is regional security and defense budgets. NATO Article 5 language being dusted off makes aerospace and defense infrastructure look like a long-term bargain, not just a one-day trade.

Which market got it right: the rallying equities or the surging gold? History says gold usually sees the shrapnel before the S&P 500 does.
