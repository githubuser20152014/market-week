Friday Fundaa | "Oil prices" isn't actually a thing

A short weekly moment of "huh, didn't know that" from the world of markets and money.

You've seen the headlines. "Oil surges on Middle East fears." "Crude crashes as peace talks progress." You nodded. You moved on. You had absolutely no idea what any of it meant for you.

That's fine. Here's what's actually going on.

First, a fact most people don't know: there is no single "oil price." There are two. And right now, they're telling very different stories.

WTI — West Texas Intermediate — is the American one. It's priced at a pipeline hub in Cushing, Oklahoma. It doesn't cross oceans. It doesn't go through the Middle East. It's the price that moves when US shale drillers are having a good week.

Brent Crude is the global one. North Sea origin, tanker-shipped, and the benchmark for roughly 70–80% of the world's oil contracts. When a headline says "oil hit $X," they almost always mean Brent. It's the one exposed to everything happening out in the world — especially chokepoints.

Normally, Brent trades $2–5 above WTI. When that gap widens, it means global supply is under stress that the US isn't feeling. When it narrows, the opposite.

That spread has been all over the place in 2026 — and here's why.

Oil entered this year looking pretty calm. A supply surplus was building. US production was near record highs. OPEC+ was starting to ease its output cuts. Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan, and the EIA were all forecasting Brent in the $55–65 range for the year. The story was: more oil than the world needs, prices drift lower.

Then the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed.

The Strait is a narrow waterway off the coast of Iran. About 20 million barrels pass through it every day — roughly one in five barrels of oil consumed globally. When US and Israeli airstrikes on Iran began in late February, that chokepoint became uncertain. Brent spiked — because Brent is seaborne, global, and travels through that part of the world. WTI followed, but more slowly. Oklahoma doesn't care about Hormuz.

Brent went from the mid-$60s to above $100. A "geopolitical risk premium" — analysts put it at $10–15 per barrel — got baked into every contract. Oil prices swung 10% in a single session when Trump hinted at peace talks, then bounced right back when Iran said there weren't any.

Here's the part that actually matters for you: that spike is sitting on top of fundamentals that still point the other way. Goldman's base case for 2026 is Brent averaging around $60 for the year. The underlying market is oversupplied. If the Strait normalises, that risk premium evaporates — and prices fall back toward where they'd be without the conflict.

So when you fill up your tank and wince at the number, you're partly paying for a geopolitical risk premium. A $10–15 surcharge on every barrel, reflecting the market's fear of something that might not even persist. Higher gas prices, pricier flights, more expensive anything-that-gets-shipped — all of it tracing back to a narrow waterway in the Persian Gulf.

The jargon to know: when oil "crashes on peace talk hopes," that's the risk premium coming out. When it "spikes on escalation fears," that's the risk premium going back in. The actual supply-and-demand story underneath — which is what drives prices over months, not days — is still one of surplus.

Two prices. Two stories. One waterway most people couldn't find on a map — moving what you pay at the pump.

Now you know.
