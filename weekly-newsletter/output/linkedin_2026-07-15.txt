Everyone's celebrating a cooler CPI print this morning. I'd pump the brakes.

June CPI came in at -0.4% MoM and +3.5% YoY, beating consensus by a wide margin. The market did what markets do: it rallied. Then Fed Chair Kevin Warsh testified in front of Congress and told everyone the celebration was premature, reiterating zero tolerance for persistent inflation. By the close, the S&P was down 0.79%, the Dow down 0.26%, and the Nasdaq, the most AI-exposed of the three, down 1.55%. The relief rally didn't survive the day.

Three other markets are telling a different story than the equity tape, and they're worth listening to.

Bonds: the 10-year yield rose 4 basis points to 4.609% on a day inflation cooled. That is not what a market convinced of imminent rate cuts looks like. Traders are still pricing a 70-73% chance of a September hike, not a cut.

Gold: down 0.6% to $4,037.70. If this were a clean "inflation is beaten" story, gold should be catching a bid on real-rate relief. It isn't.

Crude: WTI surged past $80 a barrel, not on demand, but because the U.S. Navy reinstated a naval blockade on Iranian ports in the Strait of Hormuz. Iran has roughly 48 hours to decide whether to retaliate or stand down.

The corporate proof point sits in semiconductors: ASML raised its sales forecast for the second time this year on AI chip demand and popped 4%. Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan are both being flagged as emerging AI winners. The AI trade doesn't need a rate cut to keep working, it has its own momentum.

Here's the angle nobody's covering: maritime insurance. A naval blockade in one of the world's busiest oil shipping corridors doesn't just move crude, it repriced shipping risk globally overnight. War-risk premiums on tankers transiting the Gulf are climbing, and that cost eventually shows up in freight rates well beyond the energy complex.

So which market has this right: the AI single-name winners still catching a bid, or the index itself closing down across the board, bonds, and gold all pricing a Fed that isn't moving and a geopolitical shock that isn't resolved? I'm siding with the bond market. Rate cuts aren't coming as fast as the initial CPI reaction suggested, and the crude story has 48 hours left to play out.
