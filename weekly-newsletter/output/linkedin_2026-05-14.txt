The stock market thinks Jensen Huang is the new Secretary of State, but the bond market isn't so sure. Here's why you shouldn't trust the Nasdaq's 1.2% rip just yet.

Yesterday, equities celebrated the news that the U.S. cleared Nvidia's H200 chip sales to China. It’s a massive revenue unlock, and having Jensen Huang physically in Beijing for the Trump-Xi summit is the ultimate "chip-diplomacy" signal. But while tech investors were buying the euphoria, the other markets were sending a much darker message.

Gold held firm at $4,702. In a true risk-on environment, you’d expect the yellow metal to catch a bid to the downside. It didn't. The geopolitical floor isn't coming off the table, because while the chips are flowing, the oil isn't. Crude remains capped by the Strait of Hormuz closure, and the bond market is bracing for the energy-induced hangover.

The corporate proof point? Burberry’s turnaround is being fueled by China growth, confirming that the consumer side of the trade deal is real - but the industrial side, represented by a Dow that still can't clear 50,000, is still choking on high rates and margin pressure.

The second-order story is the bond market's reaction to Kevin Warsh’s confirmation as Fed Chair. Markets are pricing a "Warsh Era" dovish pivot to combat the energy shock, but with CPI at 3.8%, that is a dangerous game of stagflation chicken.

So, which market got it right? The Nasdaq’s chip-fueled optimism or Gold’s geopolitical fear? My bet is on the floor. Until the Strait of Hormuz reopens, the rally is built on sand.
