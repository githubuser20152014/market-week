Oil crashed 10% yesterday. Equities hit all-time highs. Gold surged 3.7%. All on the same day.

Most people saw the stock market headline and called it a risk-on session. They missed the story.

---

Here's what bonds were doing while equities celebrated:

The 10-year Treasury yield fell exactly 3 basis points. Three. On a day when the S&P 500 closed at a fresh all-time high and the Russell 2000 outperformed at +1.75%, the bond market barely moved. That's not a bond rally. That's the rates market refusing to endorse the equity story.

Here's what gold was doing:

Gold surged 3.7% to $4,723 on the same session risk assets ripped higher. Gold up, oil down, equities up, dollar down - that combination doesn't happen in a clean risk-on environment. It happens when the market is simultaneously pricing a geopolitical relief trade and a structural inflation problem. The FOMC is split 8-4 on rate hikes. PCE is running at 4.5%. Central banks bought 244 tonnes of gold in Q1 alone. The structural bid isn't going anywhere.

Here's what crude was doing:

WTI collapsed 10.4% to $91.68 - the sharpest single-session drop since the early pandemic shock. Trump paused Project Freedom, the U.S. naval mission in the Strait of Hormuz, and the war premium evaporated in hours. China is pressing Iran for a Hormuz reopening ahead of a Trump-Xi summit. The geopolitical trade that had Brent near $114 just days ago unwound in a single session.

---

The corporate proof point that made the macro thesis concrete:

AMD reported a 15% single-session surge on a data center revenue blowout. AI infrastructure spending is not plateauing - it's accelerating. That print matters beyond AMD. If Nvidia and Broadcom trade in sympathy at the open, the entire semiconductor sector re-rates. Nasdaq futures are up 1.5% pre-market. KOSPI ran +6.5% overnight - the strongest session in the APAC complex. The Hormuz de-escalation read is global.

---

The second-order story nobody is covering:

Airlines and industrials just got a silent earnings upgrade. A 10% oil collapse is a direct margin expansion event. Input costs fall, earnings revision cycles turn positive, and the catch-up trade opens for sectors that had been penalized by $100+ crude. The market is focused on semiconductors and energy. The quietly improving story is in transport and industrials.

---

Which market got it right yesterday - equities celebrating the peace trade, or gold refusing to sell off despite risk assets ripping?

The answer is both. And that's the problem. Equities are pricing Hormuz reopening and AI acceleration. Gold is pricing a Fed that cannot move in either direction with PCE at 4.5%. These two views cannot both be correct for long. One of them reprices.

The bond market's 3-basis-point shrug suggests the rates market already knows which one.
