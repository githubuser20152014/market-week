Yesterday's Nasdaq rally had one problem: gold and bonds didn't buy it.

While semiconductors partied on a $26.5 billion IPO and a 1.3% index pop, three other markets were quietly hedging the exact opposite bet. Here's why you shouldn't take yesterday's close at face value.

Start with the dissenting markets. The 10-year yield eased 3 basis points to 4.539%, which is the opposite of what you'd expect on a genuine risk-on day. Gold slipped just half a percent, a token move that shows the metal is refusing to break down even as equities celebrate. And crude held at $72, well off its spike toward $79, but not collapsing either. All three are behaving like markets that got a temporary reprieve, not a resolution.

That reprieve has a name: a 48-hour diplomatic window in the Strait of Hormuz that U.S. Central Command opened by pausing strikes. It expires today. Oil's calm is borrowed, not earned.

The corporate proof point makes the tech side of the story concrete. SK Hynix debuted on the Nasdaq at $149 a share, raised $26.5 billion, and was seven times oversubscribed. That is real institutional capital voting that AI hardware demand is structural, not a speculative froth. Micron, AMD, and Marvell rode the same wave. This part of the rally isn't fake.

Here's the angle nobody's covering: war-risk insurance premiums on tankers transiting Hormuz are still elevated, and have been for weeks. That's a quiet tax on global shipping that never shows up in a Nasdaq headline but eventually shows up in freight costs, and from there, in the prices of everything that moves by sea. The equity tape says calm. The insurance market says otherwise.

So which market got it right? Tech is telling you the AI infrastructure build-out has a gravity of its own that geopolitics can't touch. Bonds and gold are telling you the ceasefire underneath yesterday's rally is fragile and temporary. With the window closing today, I'd bet on the dissenters. The AI trade is real, but yesterday's price action was priced for peace that hasn't been signed yet.
