The Strait of Hormuz is closed, April CPI just hit 3.8%, and the Fed has officially left the building. If you were looking for a sign that the "higher for longer" regime has turned into "higher forever," this is it.

Here is why you shouldn't trust the resilience in US large-caps:

**The Dissenting Markets**
While the S&P 500 pretended everything was fine (+0.3%), the bond market was screaming. The 10-year Treasury yield verticalized 20 basis points to 4.59%. Gold fell 3.6% and Silver cracked nearly 10% as real yields crushed the inflation hedge case. The markets aren't pricing growth; they are pricing a policy corner.

**Corporate Proof Point**
The divergence between the Nasdaq (+0.3%) and the Russell 2000 (-2.5%) is the story of the week. Small-cap businesses with high leverage and high energy input costs are being liquidated. Large-cap tech is holding on pricing power, but that is a defensive rotation, not a bull market.

**The Second-Order Story**
Emerging Markets (MSCI EM) dropped 4.07% this week. A surging dollar (+1.25%), rising oil import bills, and a Fed that can't cut until 2027 is a toxic cocktail for global credit. The EM spillover is the risk nobody is talking about yet.

**The Verdict**
Which market got it right? The bond market. You cannot have $105 oil and 3.8% CPI without breaking something in the domestic economy. Small caps are the first to go. Large caps are just the last to know.
