Yesterday, equities had a bad day. The story you're being told is "Hormuz plus tariffs equals risk-off." That's not wrong. It's just incomplete.

Because risk-off sessions don't usually come with yields rising. They come with yields falling. Yesterday, the 10-year climbed 3.6 bps while stocks fell. That is the tell. The market isn't pricing recession. It's pricing stagflation. And stagflation is a fundamentally different problem.

---

Gold hit $4,492, up 1.25%, while crude fell 1.17% to $94.90. Gold going up while oil goes down is the contradiction at the heart of the session. Gold is pricing the inflation shock from a 94-day Hormuz blockade. Oil is pricing the demand destruction from 12.5% tariffs on 60 economies. Both are right. They're just looking at different parts of the same problem.

Bonds confirmed the read. The 10-year at 4.491% is not a growth signal - it's the bond market pricing a CPI spike it knows is coming. The dollar barely moved at 99.42. When yields rise and the dollar doesn't follow, the market is not pricing a Fed hike. It's pricing a Fed that has no good move at all.

---

Palo Alto beat earnings estimates. Broadcom beat earnings estimates. Both sold off. That is the data point nobody wants to sit with. The market is no longer awarding premium multiples to AI-adjacent names when the macro backdrop is repricing discount rates higher. Solid numbers weren't enough. The question isn't "did they beat?" anymore. The question is "at what discount rate does this earnings stream make sense?" Yesterday, the answer moved.

---

The angle nobody is connecting: Partners Group warned it could cap fund withdrawals after a private equity rout. This is what happens when illiquid assets, marked at last quarter's valuations, meet a rate environment where yields climb 3.6 bps in a single session and gold is at new highs. The public markets talk first. The private markets are starting to confirm it.

Which market got it right yesterday - equities pricing a bad day, or gold pricing a structural shift?

Gold doesn't do earnings calls. It doesn't have an IR department. It just prices the world as it is. At $4,492 with yields rising and stocks falling, it's not telling you this is a dip to buy. It's telling you this is a regime to understand.
