"Best first half since 2020" is the headline everyone is running with today. It is also the headline that tells you the least about what actually happened underneath it.

The S&P 500 and Nasdaq closed out H1 at record paces, up 0.79% and 1.52% on the day. Five stocks did most of the lifting. That's not new information. What is new: a currency broke overnight, and equities barely noticed.

Here's why you shouldn't trust the H1 victory lap just yet.

Gold didn't buy it. Gold fell 0.84% to $3,989 as equities rallied into the close - fine on its own, until you remember gold has been pricing a Strait of Hormuz risk premium for weeks. That premium is supposed to unwind on good news. Instead, U.S.-Iran talks cracked again overnight per CNBC, less than two weeks after the market priced in peace. Gold sold off into the wrong headline.

Bonds barely moved either. The 10-year eased just 2 basis points to 4.372%. That's a market waiting on Friday's payrolls, not one convinced the growth story just improved.

Oil told the real story. WTI dropped 1.18% to $68.68, which only makes sense as the Hormuz de-escalation premium draining out, not as a demand signal. Oil is now trading with almost no geopolitical cushion left, right as the diplomatic backdrop got shakier, not calmer.

The proof point behind the tech leg of this rally: South Korea's $500 billion semiconductor and AI investment plan, announced June 30. That's a real multi-year demand signal for the chip supply chain, not sentiment. It's also why the KOSPI fell 2.04% overnight, a classic buy-the-rumor, sell-the-announcement reaction to South Korea's own headline.

The second-order story nobody is covering: the yen crossed 162.40 to the dollar overnight, a 40-year low, after Japan spent $74 billion defending it and lost anyway. The longer that currency stays broken, the more pressure builds on the Bank of Japan to act, and a BOJ move unwinds the carry trade that has quietly been funding a chunk of this year's U.S. equity positioning.

So which market got it right? Equities are saying H1 was a clean win. Gold, oil, and a 40-year-low currency are saying the geopolitical floor never actually left the room. One of them is wrong. History says it's rarely the currency market.
