$105 crude and a Nasdaq at 25,114 closed on the same day Friday. The market is betting both can last. History says that's a bad bet.

Here's why the divergence matters more than either number alone.

---

Bonds didn't celebrate.

The 10-year yield slipped just 1.2 basis points to 4.378% on a day when equities rallied. That's not the behavior of a market confident in soft landing. Bonds are sitting on their hands, waiting. When oil is at $105 and PCE is running at 3.5%, a yield this low is either a gift or a trap. The Fed is not cutting into this environment.

Gold said something different too.

Gold dropped 1.22% to $4,573 on Friday, in a session where the U.S. Navy was actively running a guidance operation in the Strait of Hormuz. That's not a normal selloff. When gold falls in a live geopolitical standoff, it means someone with a large position needed liquidity fast. That's forced rotation, not a macro signal. Sellers here are tactical, not structural. The inflation case for gold hasn't changed.

Crude told the real story.

WTI surged 3.17% to $105.17 after OPEC+ hiked output by just 188,000 bpd, its first meeting without the UAE after the bloc's May 1 breakup. The market read that increment for what it is: too small to matter against a Hormuz supply shock. The cartel is fractured. There is no ceiling in sight.

The earnings proof point the market needed.

Big Tech beat expectations Friday and the market explicitly rewarded AI capex spending. SoftBank is circling an AI IPO. The Nemotron Coalition launched. The private market is pricing a multi-year infrastructure buildout. That's what pulled the Nasdaq up 0.89% while the Dow fell 0.31%, with rate-sensitive and energy-cost-exposed businesses are being repriced in real time against the AI growth narrative.

The second-order story nobody is covering.

KOSPI ripped 5.1% overnight. South Korea is acutely exposed to both energy costs and semiconductor cycles. That move tells you institutional money in Asia is chasing the AI chip rally hard, even as $105 oil eats into manufacturing margins across the region. Something has to give in that trade, and it won't be quiet when it does.

Which market got it right?

Oil is saying inflation is structural and supply is constrained. Tech is saying growth is durable and AI capex justifies the multiple. Both cannot be true at the same time for long. The 10-year at 4.378% is not priced for a world where WTI stays above $100 and PCE stays above 3%. One of these markets is going to force a repricing of the other.
