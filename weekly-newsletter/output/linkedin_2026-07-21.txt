The S&P closed down 0.19% yesterday. Futures are up 0.54% this morning, betting today is about earnings, not Iran.

They're pricing the wrong session.

---

Gold closed at a new record: $4,068.30, up 1.45%. Not momentum chasing. The commodity complex read the same seventh-night-of-airstrikes headlines as equities and landed on the opposite conclusion. When the fear asset sets a record on a day equities are basically flat, that is not noise. That is capital voting.

Bonds did something equally uncomfortable: the 10-year yield climbed to 4.598%. Yields do not normally rise alongside a gold rally - one is a fear trade, the other is a growth or inflation trade, and they are supposed to fight each other, not cooperate. When they move together, the bond market is telling you it does not know which risk to price first.

Crude, meanwhile, slipped 0.66% to $82.68, despite a live war sitting on top of the Strait of Hormuz for a seventh straight night. Either the oil market has quietly priced in a resolution nobody has announced, or it has stopped paying attention to its own headline risk. Neither is comforting.

---

Then Jamie Dimon said the quiet part out loud: he would not buy stocks or Treasuries at current prices. That is not hedge-fund chatter. That is the head of the country's largest bank declining to participate in the rally everyone else is buying into.

---

The angle nobody is covering: commercial shipping is now rerouting around the entire Arabian Peninsula to avoid the Strait. That reroute does not just move oil tankers, it moves freight costs, insurance premiums, and delivery timelines for everything else on those routes, autos and auto parts included, on the same morning General Motors reports earnings shaped partly by tariff costs. The freight bill from a war nobody has declared is landing before the oil price does.

---

So which market got yesterday right? Gold at a record. Yields climbing. Oil looking the other way. Or equity futures betting today is business as usual?

One of these is going to look very wrong by the close. General Motors earnings and the 10-year's reaction to 4.65% start settling it before lunch.
