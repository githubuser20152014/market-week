## The Brief

The dominant mood is a cautious, ceasefire-fueled risk-on rally sitting on top of a structural energy crisis that hasn't gone away. The standout cross-asset signal is the disconnect between a record S&P 500 and gold pushing toward $4,820 — both bid simultaneously, which tells you investors aren't fully buying the peace story. Asia sold the news overnight (Nikkei -1.75%), Europe is flat, and U.S. futures are nudging higher — the open looks constructive but fragile.

Yesterday's session saw U.S. equities close at all-time highs across the board: the **S&P 500 settled at 7,041**, the Nasdaq at 24,103, the Dow at 48,579, and the Russell 2000 at 2,720. The real headline was elsewhere: **WTI crude crashed 6.2% to $88.84**, while **gold held firm near $4,819** — rising 0.7% even as yields ticked up 2.7 bps to 4.31% and the USD slipped modestly to 98.10.

The catalyst was geopolitical de-escalation — specifically the **U.S.-brokered 10-day Israel-Lebanon ceasefire** and Trump's public suggestion that a deal with Iran could come as soon as this weekend. That combination hit oil like a freight train: the war risk premium that had been embedded in crude pricing began to unwind. Think of it like a hurricane insurance policy being cancelled mid-storm — premiums drop fast. But notice what didn't sell off: gold. That's the tell. The **Strait of Hormuz remains partially blocked**, the IMF just warned of a global debt crisis by 2029, and Russia launched its deadliest missile strikes of 2026 on Ukraine. The ceasefire is a truce, not a resolution, and the bond market and gold market both know it.
