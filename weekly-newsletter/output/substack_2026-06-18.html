The New Fed Chair's First Word Cost 1.2% of Your Portfolio. Second Word Pending.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

Kevin Warsh showed up to his first Fed press conference and delivered the most hawkish message possible by saying almost nothing. Nine of 19 policymakers penciled in a rate hike for 2026. Warsh declined to push back. That non-denial repriced everything.

The Iran deal hit the same afternoon. Trump and Pezeshkian signed the Versailles Memorandum, reopened the Strait of Hormuz, and evaporated the war premium in crude and gold simultaneously. WTI dropped 4%. Gold dropped 1.6%. Two completely different types of risk got unwound in one session.

Markets spent the night deciding which story to lead with. They chose the peace deal. Futures are up 2.7%. The Warsh problem is still there.

The relief trade is real. So is the ceiling it's rallying into.

Here's my read:

The session was a double catalyst that both went the same direction. Warsh's hawkish debut killed the rate-cut story: markets now price a 78% probability of a December hike, and the dot plot showing 9 of 19 policymakers expecting a 2026 hike is the official baseline, not an outlier. This is the Fed put being publicly disavowed on day one. The S&P fell 1.2% to 7,420, Nasdaq dropped 1.3% to 26,021, and the 10Y Treasury climbed to 4.49%. High-multiple growth just found its ceiling.

The Versailles Memorandum compounded it. Reopening the Strait removes the structural floor from WTI, now at $73.72 after a 4% single-day drop, and strips the geopolitical bid from gold, now at $4,289. The 60-day negotiation window turns the next oil catalyst from a supply shock into a supply surge. Anyone long energy or precious metals on the conflict trade got repriced in both directions simultaneously.

This morning's bounce - S&P futures at 7,563, Nasdaq futures up 2.7% - is an Iran relief trade, not a Warsh reversal. The Nikkei added 1.6% and KOSPI surged 2.3% on energy import savings. The Hang Seng fell 1.6%, because Beijing's property developer defaults do not negotiate peace treaties.

The One Trade: SHORT $QQQ

Nasdaq futures are up 2.7% on Iran relief. The Warsh hawkish signal that broke the market yesterday is completely unresolved. A 78% December hike probability does not evaporate because crude is cheaper.

Entry confirm: QQQ fails to hold above 535 after the first 30 minutes of trading. The S&P cannot reclaim 7,500 - the level it broke on the Warsh press conference. The gap-up is a fade, not a breakout.

Kill switch: Warsh or another Fed speaker delivers a clarifying dovish comment before noon ET. Or QQQ holds above 537 through the 10 AM New Home Sales release. If either happens, get out fast.

What else I'm watching

  • Don't you dare buy the XLE dip. WTI at $73.72 is now priced without war risk. The Strait is open for 60 days minimum and the next catalyst is a supply surge, not a shock. The energy bull case was geopolitical. That case just signed a peace deal.
  • Hold GLD, don't short it. The Iran premium unwound, but the Warsh premium - fiscal credibility concerns, a Fed Chair actually fighting inflation, dollar uncertainty - creates a new floor. Gold at $4,289 with a hawkish Fed is not the same as gold at $4,289 with an easy one. The smart money does not short gold when bonds are repricing simultaneously.
  • New Home Sales at 10 AM ET is the one number Warsh will read closely. He cited housing as the sector where restrictive rates are working. A weak print validates his hawkish hold, it does not change it. Watch whether any Fed official comments afterward.
  • European defense is the trade nobody is covering. Hegseth's NATO 3.0 ultimatum - threatening some allies will "fail" the U.S. defense review - means sovereign defense budgets in Europe are a policy-locked expenditure for the next decade. EUDF is a structural long, not a tactical one.
  • Watch 7,500 on the S&P intraday. That's the level the market broke through during the press conference. Reclaiming it means something. Failing to hold it means the gap-up was the trade, not the signal.
