Geopolitical anxiety is being priced as equanimity.

The U.S. struck Bandar Abbas yesterday. Iran claimed a retaliatory strike on a U.S. base in Kuwait. And yet the S&P 500 closed up a rounding-error 0.02% while WTI crude surged 2.84% to $91.20 — the single most important cross-asset divergence heading into today.

Here's what the dissenting markets tell you:

Oil traders believe the Strait of Hormuz just moved from "geopolitical risk" to "kinetic conflict zone." 20% of global oil supply threads through that chokepoint, and traders are not waiting for de-escalation. Brent is back near $97 overseas.

Gold traders are not convinced. The metal pulled back 0.67% to $4,417 despite a genuine shooting war escalating in the Persian Gulf. That's a notable failure for a "safe haven" in an actual crisis.

Bond traders are even more skeptical. The 10-year yield dipped 1.2 basis points to 4.48% — they're pricing cooling inflation, not energy-driven spiral.

One clear corporate proof point: Snowflake surged 36% on a $6 billion AWS commitment while Zscaler collapsed 31% on guidance cuts. This is not a broad risk-off. It's a K-shaped divergence where AI infrastructure gets funded and everything else gets cut.

The second-order story nobody's discussing: if gold is failing to rally into an actual war, and equities are shrugging geopolitical chaos, then energy — not equities, not gold — is where the real inflation hedge lives. The market is not doubting the oil story. It's doubting everything else.

The verdict: Today's 8:30 AM PCE and GDP print decides which market gets proven right. A hot PCE print confirms energy-driven inflation is structural, validates the oil move, and crushes equities. A soft print suggests the geopolitical premium is overdone and sends equities higher on rate-cut hopes.

Nasdaq futures are down 0.48%, ASX 200 fell 1.43%, Hang Seng dropped 1.27%. The tape is coiled around that 8:30 number.
