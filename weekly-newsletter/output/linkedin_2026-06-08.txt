The equity market took an oil shock to the face yesterday. That is the obvious read. Here is the one nobody is saying out loud.

The April ceasefire between Israel and Iran collapsed in direct military exchanges over the weekend. WTI crude hit $94.59, up 4.5%. Brent spiked to $97.60. The Nasdaq dropped 4.2% to 25,709. The S&P 500 fell 2.6% to 7,383. But look at what did not move.

Gold fell 0.5% to $4,317 - on a day when the Strait of Hormuz was in active conflict. Gold is the geopolitical hedge. It sat out its own catalyst. When the safe-haven asset cannot rally on the exact event it was built for, it is telling you the position is overcrowded, the inflation picture is too complex, or both. In this case, both.

Crude told the opposite story. WTI at $94.59, Brent at $97.60, the market pricing in a supply shock that does not resolve quickly. The Hormuz premium is structural until there is a verified ceasefire with enforcement mechanisms, not a diplomatic statement.

The 10-year Treasury yield climbed to 4.54% - on an equity-down day. Bonds should rally when equities sell off. They did not. Yields rising alongside an equity collapse is the stagflation fingerprint: the shock is inflationary, not deflationary. The Fed cannot cut into $94 oil without reigniting the inflation it just spent two years fighting. The rate-cut timeline just got pushed out again.

Foreign investors have spent 2026 dumping Korean equities despite a record KOSPI rally. Yesterday the KOSPI collapsed 8.3% in a single session. Samsung and SK Hynix - two of the most globally connected semiconductor names - are at the center of this. The AI infrastructure thesis that propped up tech valuations last year is running out of road at exactly the wrong moment.

The angle the equity market is completely ignoring: the U.S. confirmed a second Texas screwworm case over the weekend, and Canada has already restricted livestock imports. This is a food supply chain story at a moment when energy inflation is threatening to re-embed in consumer prices. Agricultural commodities and food producers are not in the inflation conversation yet. They probably should be.

So which market got it right? Crude, which priced in a structural supply shock. Not equities, which are attempting a 0.3% futures bounce while the KOSPI is down 8.3%, the Nikkei down 3.8%, and gold doing nothing. The overnight session processed this news more honestly than the pre-market session is this morning.
