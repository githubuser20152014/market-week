Oil Breaks $100, Natural Gas Jumps 11%: Why the S&P Didn't Care

Here's the read that doesn't fit the headline.

**Bonds aren't buying it.** 10Y Treasury +7 bps to 4.38%. Markets aren't fleeing to safety - they're pricing more inflation. The Hormuz Premium is baked in, and it stays baked until the Strait reopens.

**Gold fell.** Down 1.65% to $4,629 with an active war running. Rising real yields are suppressing the safe-haven bid. The market is calling this an inflation shock, not a war shock. That's the more corrosive read - inflation shocks don't resolve on a ceasefire announcement.

**The UAE left OPEC.** Effective May 1. Sixty years of Saudi-led supply management, fractured. WTI swung from $94 to $110 in a single week. That's not a tactical spike - it's a structural break in global energy pricing, and it doesn't reverse when the headlines calm down.

The AI earnings that carried equities this week are real. But OpenAI's revenue came in short mid-week. PCE printed at 3.5%. Personal savings hit 3.6%, a multi-year low. The consumer has almost no buffer left for energy pass-throughs.

Which market got it right? My read: both. Tech earnings are real. The energy repricing is also real. The only question is which one outruns the other - and that margin is very thin.
