Hormuz Deadlock Rewrites the Inflation Playbook

The dominant story this week was Iran's renewed blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, a chokepoint carrying 20% of global oil supply. After the U.S. Navy seized the Iranian-flagged cargo vessel Touska, Tehran reversed its weekend de-escalation and re-closed the strait. WTI crude surged 6.07%, touching a weekly high of $98.39 before cooling slightly on ceasefire extension hopes. This was not a slow-burn macro shift. It was a discrete geopolitical shock with immediate transmission into energy prices, bond yields, and equity risk premiums.

The counterintuitive signal: bonds did not rally on the geopolitical shock. The 10-year Treasury yield rose 5 basis points to 4.31%, and the 30-year touched 4.94%. Safe-haven demand was overwhelmed by the inflation implications of $95+ oil. That tells you the regime has shifted. The market is no longer buying Treasuries when geopolitical risk spikes. It is selling them, because the primary fear is persistent energy-driven inflation, not recession. Kevin Warsh's Senate confirmation hearings as Fed Chair nominee amplified this dynamic, with markets parsing his "gradual balance sheet reduction" language as confirmation that rate relief is not imminent.

Beyond oil, the Hormuz blockade is threatening fertilizer and petrochemical shipments, raising the prospect of a second-round inflationary shock to global food prices by early 2027. The Islamabad peace talks remain the single most important variable for near-term market direction. VP JD Vance led a U.S. delegation, but no Iranian delegation had arrived by mid-week. The ceasefire was extended by Trump at Pakistan's request, but Tehran's refusal to confirm attendance keeps the risk premium elevated and the trade live.

What it means for you

Critically, bond markets did not rally on the geopolitical shock: the 10-year Treasury yield rose 5 basis points to 4.31%, signaling that the primary fear is now energy-driven inflation, not recession. European equity ETFs including FEZ and EWU are absorbing a double hit from local index declines and a commodity import shock that the Bank of England and ECB cannot easily offset. Japan's Nikkei gained +1.52% and stands out as the cleanest equity exposure in the current regime, supported by defense sector tailwinds from the lifted weapons export ban. Patient investors should treat the Islamabad talks outcome as the binary event of the coming week: a failure there puts $100 oil, higher yields, and renewed pressure on European equities back on the table simultaneously.

The One Trade: $USO - Long

WTI crude closed the week at $94.40 after touching $98.39, with the Hormuz blockade still active and no Iranian delegation confirmed at the Islamabad talks as of mid-week. The supply shock has a named, discrete, ongoing cause. Iran re-closed the strait after the U.S. seized the Touska. That is not a rumor or a sentiment shift. It is a physical constraint on 20% of global oil supply. The blockade is also now affecting fertilizer and petrochemical shipments, which means secondary commodity markets are beginning to price in cascading supply disruptions.

The asymmetry here favors the upside. A ceasefire extension has already been priced in once this week, yet WTI only pulled back to the low $88s before recovering. The floor is holding. If the Islamabad talks fail outright, the path to $100+ is open and USO moves with it. If talks partially succeed with a conditional ceasefire, oil likely stabilizes in the $90-95 range, which is still above the entry. The only clean downside scenario is a full diplomatic resolution that reopens the strait and collapses the risk premium entirely. That scenario requires Iran to formally engage, which has not happened.

Confirms: WTI holding above $90 into the Islamabad deadline, or a fresh breakdown in diplomatic contact between Tehran and Washington.
Risk: Iran confirms attendance at Islamabad talks and a credible ceasefire framework is announced, sending WTI back toward $85 and erasing the geopolitical premium.

Equity Markets - Tech Holds, Europe Folds, Japan Quietly Wins

US equities split along familiar lines. The Nasdaq gained +1.72%, closing at 24,836, as tech earnings provided a floor. The S&P 500 added a modest +0.67%, while the Dow slipped -0.39% as energy cost fears weighed on industrial and consumer-facing names. Small caps via IWM were largely flat, up +0.57%, consistent with a week where macro uncertainty discouraged risk-taking in rate-sensitive domestics. UnitedHealth beat estimates and jumped 7%. Apple fell 2.5% on the leadership transition from Tim Cook to John Ternus. These idiosyncratic moves mattered more than the index tape.

Europe was the week's clear underperformer. The FTSE 100 fell -2.71%, the CAC 40 dropped -1.88%, and the Euro Stoxx 50 lost -1.78%. The energy import exposure of European economies made the Hormuz shock particularly acute: the UK, with headline CPI already running at 3.3% YoY and growth forecasts cut to just 0.8%, has almost no policy room to absorb another commodity shock. The Bank of England is effectively pinned. One bright spot beneath the surface: Hungary's political shift, with Péter Magyar's Tisza Party ousting Orbán after 16 years, stabilized the Forint and injected new optimism around frozen EU fund flows. Japan was the quiet outperformer in APAC, with the Nikkei up +1.52% to 59,716, supported by the government's historic decision to lift its post-WWII ban on lethal weapons exports. The Hang Seng slipped -0.86% as Taiwan airspace tensions signaled renewed China-Taiwan diplomatic friction.

Currency Markets - Dollar Steadies as Energy Shock Complicates Every Central Bank

The USD Index gained a marginal +0.23% to close at 98.51, a modest move that understates the week's complexity. Every major currency pair saw the dollar strengthen, but only slightly: EUR/USD fell -0.11% to 1.1726, GBP/USD dropped -0.13% to 1.3466, and JPY/USD slipped -0.11%. The dollar's bid came not from growth optimism but from the repricing of Fed rate cuts. With WTI above $90 and Warsh signaling a measured approach to balance sheet reduction, the case for dollar weakness has weakened.

For global ETF investors, a sticky dollar at current levels is a headwind to unhedged international exposure. European equity ETFs like FEZ and EWU are getting hit twice: local index declines plus a slight currency drag on USD-denominated returns. EM held up better than expected, with EEM gaining +1.0% to $63.74, suggesting that some EM commodity exporters are benefiting from the oil price spike even as the broader geopolitical backdrop creates noise.

Commodities & Metals - Oil Dominates, Gold Retreats, Gas Collapses

WTI crude surged 6.07% to $94.40, touching a weekly high of $98.39, the single most important price move of the week. The catalyst was unambiguous: Iran's re-closure of the Strait of Hormuz following the U.S. seizure of the Touska. Brent briefly traded near $96.49. The implied path to $100 is short if the Islamabad talks fail. Shipping rates are spiking, and the blockade's impact on fertilizer and petrochemical transit creates a second-order commodity story that markets have not yet fully priced.

Gold pulled back -1.49% to $4,722, a counterintuitive move given the geopolitical intensity. The explanation is mechanical: with real yields rising on the back of the 10-year climbing to 4.31%, the opportunity cost of holding gold increased. Spot gold did recover to $4,829 on Monday morning before settling near $4,764 mid-week as ceasefire extension hopes provided temporary relief. Silver fell harder, down -3.96%, consistent with industrial demand concerns. Natural gas dropped -6.32% on supply dynamics unrelated to the Hormuz story.

This Week's Economic Events

The economic data calendar was thin on confirmed releases. The trade deficit widened to -$57.3 billion from -$54.7 billion the prior period, consistent with an economy still importing at elevated volumes despite price pressures. Industrial production fell -0.5%, a soft print that fits the narrative of a manufacturing sector under pressure from input cost inflation and uncertain demand.

The data point markets actually traded this week was the anticipated 1.4% increase in March retail sales, flagged by analysts as a key gauge of consumer resilience under sustained energy inflation. The UK's 4.9% unemployment rate looked headline-strong but masked a rise in economic inactivity, not genuine labor market health. With UK CPI at 3.3% and growth at 0.8%, the Bank of England has no clean options. The Fed confirmation hearings for Warsh were the week's most market-relevant event in the macro calendar.

Next Week: What to Watch

Two catalysts dominate the upcoming week. First, the Islamabad peace talks outcome: if Iran sends a delegation and a ceasefire extension is formalized, oil could retreat sharply toward $85-88, triggering a relief rally in European equities and a pullback in the inflation-risk premium embedded in Treasury yields. If talks collapse, $100 Brent becomes the base case and the equity tape gets uglier fast. Second, the industrial production print and new home sales data will test whether the domestic economy is absorbing rising energy costs or beginning to buckle. Kevin Warsh's Senate testimony is a rolling event: any signal of greater tolerance for inflation will reprice the short end of the curve sharply.
