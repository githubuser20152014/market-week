Nasdaq closed at a record 26,121 yesterday, up 1.12%. The Dow crossed 53,000. Then overnight, Iran allegedly opened fire on commercial ships in the Strait of Hormuz, and "rate cuts fix everything" hit its first real test. Here's why you shouldn't trust yesterday's close as a clean signal.

Start with bonds. If the market genuinely believed the soft jobs report guaranteed a rate cut, the 10-year should have moved meaningfully. Instead it crept up one basis point to 4.485%, essentially a shrug. That's not conviction, that's a market keeping its options open while equities went and partied without it.

Then there's gold, which slipped 0.25% to $4,145 into yesterday's equity euphoria. Normally that reads as the safe-haven bid fading. Except gold at this level has spent weeks defending itself on every dip, and a fresh geopolitical shock overnight is exactly the kind of catalyst that reopens the position rather than closes it.

Crude actually told you something was coming before anyone else did. WTI rose 0.85% to $69.13 yesterday, ahead of the Hormuz headlines breaking overnight. In hindsight, that wasn't noise, that was the oil market quietly pricing a risk premium before the news existed.

The corporate proof point that got buried under the record-high headlines: Microsoft cut 4,800 jobs this week. A megacap trimming headcount in the same week the Nasdaq closes at records isn't the AI-capex supercycle story that index gains implied. It's cost discipline dressed up as innovation spend.

The angle nobody's covering: marine insurers are already repricing war-risk premiums for tankers transiting the Strait of Hormuz, the corridor for roughly 20% of the world's oil supply. That premium moves before crude futures do, and it moved overnight.

So which market got yesterday right: the equity rally that treated a jobs miss and a SpaceX index inclusion as reasons to hit records, or the bond and oil markets that were already pricing something else hours before the headline confirmed it? I'm with bonds and oil. A record Nasdaq built on a 57,000-job print is a rally standing on a folding table, and the folding table is now next to the Strait of Hormuz.
