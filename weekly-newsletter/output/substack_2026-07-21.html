Stocks Voted Optimism. Gold Voted Fear. Night Seven of Iran Strikes Settles Nothing.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

Night seven of U.S. airstrikes on Iran, and equity futures decided this was a buying opportunity anyway. Nasdaq futures up 1.36%. S&P futures up 0.54%. Somewhere between night seven and night eight of a shooting war, Wall Street ran out of reasons to care.

Gold did not get that memo. Gold ripped to a record $4,068.30, up 1.45%, while the 10-year yield climbed to 4.598% in the same session. Those two are not supposed to move together - one says fear, the other says growth, and right now both of them are lying to somebody. S&P 500 closed at 7,443.28, down 0.19%. Dow shed 307 points to 51,839.26. Russell 2000 dropped 0.67% because small-caps feel tariff pain first and complain about it loudest.

General Motors reports before the bell and gets to referee the tariff-pass-through argument for everyone else. Watch 4.65% on the 10-year: cross it and the "stocks rally through anything" thesis gets a very public retest.

Here's my read:

Gold at $4,068 with yields rising is the tell, not a footnote. Higher yields are supposed to choke gold's appeal, since a non-yielding asset should lose to a 4.6% Treasury every time. Instead gold is winning anyway, which means the Strait of Hormuz premium currently outranks the entire U.S. bond market's opinion. Commercial shipping is rerouting around the whole Arabian Peninsula, because nothing says business as usual like tankers taking the scenic route. Jamie Dimon said he wouldn't buy stocks or Treasuries at current prices, which from the man who runs the country's largest bank is less a warning than a diagnosis.

Trump slapped 50% tariffs on select Canadian goods, just so trade friction didn't get lonely next to the geopolitical kind. WTI crude somehow slipped 0.66% to $82.68 through all of it, which means oil has either priced in peace or stopped watching the news. One of those two things is about to be very wrong. Overnight, APAC decided none of this mattered: Nikkei up 3.26%, KOSPI up 3.56%, proving optimism travels faster across the Pacific than bad news does.

The One Trade: Long $GLD

Gold is rising into a risk-on futures open while yields climb, a decoupling that signals the Strait of Hormuz geopolitical bid is overpowering normal rate headwinds. That kind of dislocation rarely reverses intraday without a concrete diplomatic breakthrough.

Confirms: GLD holds above $375 (spot gold sustaining above $4,050) through the first hour of trading with no confirmed ceasefire headline out of the UN emergency session.
Kill switch: A concrete ceasefire announcement or Hormuz shipping normalization signal out of UN Security Council proceedings breaks the fear bid and sends gold back below $3,980. Get out fast.

What else I'm watching

  • Hold or add GLD, don't you dare sell it here. Gold is breaking out against rising yields, which only happens when geopolitical fear dominates rate logic. Stay long unless Hormuz shipping normalizes with a confirmed ceasefire.
  • XLE is the trap everyone's about to walk into. WTI pulled back to $82.68 despite active conflict near a major shipping chokepoint. If Houthi threats to Saudi shipping materialize or Hormuz traffic data drops, energy reprices fast. Enter on a WTI close back above $85, not before.
  • Stay away from IWM today. The Russell 2000 is down 0.67% and small-caps face a double headwind from Canadian tariff fallout and tightening credit conditions implied by a 4.598% 10-year. No clear catalyst for a reversal.
  • ITA is the trade nobody's talking about. Seven nights of Iran airstrikes plus an emergency UN session means defense budget signals are building. ITA holds Lockheed, RTX, and Northrop. This is a geopolitical risk premium trade, not a valuation trade.
  • GM earnings before the bell decide the tape. Nasdaq longs are viable intraday if the print is clean and tech futures hold their gains, but the Jamie Dimon warning and rising yields set a ceiling. Trim into strength above 7,530 on the S&P futures rather than chasing the open pop.
