Nasdaq Hit 26,121. Then Someone Started Shooting at Tankers.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

The market closed at record highs yesterday on rate-cut optimism. The Nasdaq surged 1.12% to $26,121, the S&P 500 gained 0.72% to $7,537, and the Dow crossed and held 53,000, because gravity is optional when payrolls miss badly enough. Then, overnight, Iran allegedly started shooting at commercial ships in the Strait of Hormuz, and optimism didn't get the memo in time.

Nasdaq futures are down nearly 1% pre-market, gold is holding near $4,145, and oil is bid: the textbook risk-off reflex, except the dollar is also firming while the yen keeps sliding further, so apparently nobody read the same textbook.

On the surface, a tidy bull day. Then the Gulf happened.

Here's my read:

Blame last week's soft payrolls print: 57,000 jobs added in June, a miss clean enough to supercharge rate-cut bets, plus SpaceX's fast-track entry into the Nasdaq-100, which forced non-discretionary buying into QQQ and QQQM. Broadcom and the rest of the AI crowd rode the wave. But this morning that story hits a wall: CNBC is reporting Iranian forces attacked commercial ships in the Strait of Hormuz, the choke point for roughly 20% of global oil supply, not a headline you shrug off before the open. Layer on Trump en route to Ankara for the NATO summit while Russia keeps shelling Kyiv, and the alliance is accelerating defense spending commitments as if the missiles were a sales pitch. The rate-cut trade and the war-premium trade are now fighting for the same tape.

The One Trade: Long $XLE

Iranian forces reportedly attacked commercial ships in the Strait of Hormuz overnight, and WTI is already holding $69.13 while a market near record highs hasn't priced a real supply-disruption premium into energy equities.

Confirms: WTI crude trades above $70.00 within the first 45 minutes of the US open and XLE holds above its prior session close on above-average volume.
Kill switch: Hormuz attack reports are officially denied or characterized as isolated and non-escalating before 11 AM ET, sending WTI back below $68.50 and removing the geopolitical bid. Exit fast.

What else I'm watching

  • Long XLE or XOP conditionally: if WTI holds above $69.50 through the first hour and Hormuz headlines escalate, energy has room to run toward levels last seen before OPEC+ noise dragged oil to four-month lows. If the reports prove unconfirmed, trim quickly, this is event-driven, not a trend.
  • Reduce QQQ or hedge with SQQQ into the open: Nasdaq futures are off 0.93%, the SpaceX flow is absorbed, and Microsoft's 4,800-job cut says even megacaps are trimming, not spending. Don't you dare chase this open.
  • Stay long ITA or XAR: the NATO summit in Ankara and Lockheed's $3.45 billion bid for Ultra Maritime confirm defense capex is accelerating in real contracts, not just pledges. Multi-week tailwind, size accordingly.

  • Stay away from chasing broad index exposure (SPY, IVV) until Hormuz clarifies. The S&P is within 0.5% of all-time highs with a geopolitical wildcard live, and asymmetry favors patience.
