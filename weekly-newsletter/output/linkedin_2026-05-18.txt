An Iranian drone struck a nuclear power plant yesterday. The 10-year yield jumped 13 basis points. Gold closed flat.

Here's why you shouldn't trust the calm in futures this morning.

---

The bond market never believed the S&P 7,500 rally. It said so loudly on Friday: the 10-year spiked 13.4 basis points to 4.595% in a single session - the sharpest one-day repricing in weeks - after PPI came in at 6.0% year-over-year. That print locked the Fed out of cutting. Every rate-cut narrative priced into growth stocks since January just got quietly repriced out.

The dissenting markets told the real story:

Bonds sold off hard even as equities were already falling. The 10-year at 4.595% is not compatible with S&P 7,500 valuations - one of them is wrong, and bonds have been right all year.

Gold closed flat at $4,556 while the S&P fell 1.24%, the Nasdaq fell 1.54%, and a nuclear plant got hit by a drone. Gold is not going up on catastrophic news. That means gold is done going down. The non-move is the signal.

WTI crude fell 3.34% to $101.90 even as Iran threatened to close the Strait of Hormuz. The market is saying demand destruction now outweighs supply disruption risk. When energy falls on a Hormuz threat, that is a recession signal in geopolitical clothing.

The corporate proof point: Ryanair's CFO didn't use "challenging environment" or "headwinds." He used the word "armageddon" - specifically to describe the jet fuel crunch scenario if Hormuz closes. That is an airline with 600 aircraft telling you what the energy supply chain looks like from the inside.

The second-order story nobody is covering: the US-China $17 billion soybean and rare earths deal signed after the Trump-Xi summit. In a session where everything else was on fire, agricultural commodities got a genuine structural tailwind. Soybeans, fertilizers, and the rare earths supply chain all got a deal that cuts through the noise. That story is getting buried under Iran headlines and it shouldn't be.

The verdict: which market got it right - equities holding near 7,400 or bonds repricing to 4.595%? Bonds have been right every time this cycle. Futures down only 0.29% this morning is suspiciously calm given that the G7 is meeting today specifically to assess Hormuz closure consequences. The complacency in futures is the tell. The real test is whether S&P buyers defend 7,400 at the open - or whether the yield spike finally breaks the growth trade.
