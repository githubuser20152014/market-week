Equities near all-time records. Iran just mined the Strait of Hormuz. The S&P 500 went up 0.13% anyway. Here's why you shouldn't trust that move.

---

Crude said what equities wouldn't.

WTI surged 3.08% to $96.65 with Brent testing $98. That is not a "geopolitical premium" - that is the physical commodity market pricing a real supply disruption. The Strait of Hormuz carries roughly 20% of global oil. Secretary Rubio told Congress it has mines in it. The oil market listened. Equities looked the other way.

Gold told a different story.

Gold fell 0.33% to $4,474 in a session where Iran was hitting airports and the U.S. was running strikes on Qeshm Island. Gold falling during an active Gulf conflict is unusual. What it signals: the inflation trade is rotating from store-of-value to real commodity. The market is not scared - it is front-running the energy price spike. That is a more dangerous read than fear.

Bonds shrugged.

The 10-year yield dipped just 2 basis points to 4.455%. Not a flight-to-safety rally, not a rate-cut bet - just a market with no strong conviction on where rates go next. Which makes sense, given that the Fed is now watching $96 oil, 10-12.5% tariffs on 60 economies, and a new chair whose first hire wrote a blueprint for restructuring the central bank.

---

Palo Alto Networks beat earnings on AI-driven cybersecurity demand. Apparently the world is getting hacked faster than it is going broke. That beat kept the AI complex bid, but the Nasdaq gained just 0.03% while the Russell 2000 gained 0.90%. The AI momentum trade is narrowing. Two names - Palo Alto and Marvell - are carrying the index. That is not a healthy market. That is a two-stock bet dressed up as a trend.

---

The story nobody is covering: the Fed's institutional moment.

New Fed Chair Warsh just made his first hires - including the author of the Project 2025 chapter on restructuring the Federal Reserve. Meanwhile, the administration just proposed tariffs on 60 economies simultaneously. The traditional Fed response to a supply shock (hold rates, wait it out) becomes politically complicated when the institution itself is being restructured from the inside. The bond market's 2 bps dip is not a verdict. It is paralysis.

---

So which market got it right?

Not equities. The S&P at 7,609 with a mined oil corridor, a fresh round of tariffs, and an increasingly constrained Fed is pricing a very specific benign scenario. Crude and small-caps are pricing something closer to reality: a supply shock that does not resolve this week, inflation inputs that do not switch off, and a central bank that cannot cut into $96 oil without reigniting the very problem it spent two years solving.

One of them is wrong. The oil market has a better track record.
