The Ceasefire Worked. Gold Went Up 3.76% Anyway.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

Peace broke out Thursday. Trump cancelled the Iran strikes. A 60-day ceasefire. A proposed MOU to reopen the Strait of Hormuz and lift oil sanctions. The war premium that had pushed Brent above $94 last week evaporated in a single session.

Equities ripped. Crude dumped. Luxury stocks spiked 5% because apparently peace is good for handbags. The relief rally was logical, clean, and exactly what you would expect.

Then gold closed at $4,244. Up 3.76%. On a ceasefire day.

One of these markets is not celebrating. It is hedging.

Here's my read:

S&P 500 closed at 7,394, up 1.75%. Nasdaq added 2.54%. Russell 2000 surged 3.02%, because small caps are the canaries of domestic credit, and the oil tax just got repealed. WTI crude collapsed 4.65% to $83.63 - the fastest single-session repricing of the Hormuz risk premium in recent memory. The 10-year yield dropped 7.9 basis points to 4.463%, because lower oil is the Fed's best friend right now, and they did not even have to ask.

Here is the contradiction: gold at $4,244 went up 3.76% on the same day equities celebrated a de-escalation. That is not a risk-on rotation. That is the options market saying the MOU is a memorandum of unverified optimism. The deal is not signed. CPI is still at 4.2%. The June 17 FOMC meeting is a live grenade. The smart money voted with its wallet, and the wallet voted for gold while also buying stocks, because the fragility is real and the buyers know it.

KOSPI surged 4.63% after the Yoon sentencing cleared a domestic political overhang, which tells you how much of the APAC selloff was political noise versus macro signal. S&P futures at 7,421 say the open holds the rally. The swing factor today is the SpaceX $75 billion IPO - retail allocation was cut to the low 20% range, and Senator Warren's oversight letter is circling the exchanges. If it prices well, risk appetite confirms. If it does not, you will see a sentiment shock in a market that just repriced aggressively higher in a single session.

The One Trade: Long $IWM

Crude dropped 4.65% to $83.63: the Hormuz reopening just eliminated the oil tax that was quietly crushing domestic margins. Small caps absorb energy cost pain first and relief fastest. IWM is the cleanest expression of that reversal.

Confirms: IWM holds above 2,900 through the first hour of trading and crude does not reclaim $86 by noon ET.

Kill switch: WTI crude reverses above $87 on any sign the Iran MOU is collapsing or unsigned by end of day, reinstating the energy cost headwind that small caps cannot absorb. Get out fast.

What else I'm watching

  • Don't you dare chase $XLE here. The Hormuz reopening prices in supply normalization. Crude at $83 heading toward $78-80 removes the structural bid that was keeping energy stocks elevated. Trim into the strength if you still hold it.
  • Hold $GLD, do not chase the breakout. Gold at $4,244 on a risk-on day is a structural warning, not a momentum trade. The 4.2% CPI print and a live FOMC meeting on June 17 mean real rates could spike if Warsh surprises hawkish. Add only on a pullback toward $4,180.
  • Stay away from $EZU despite the LVMH pop. The ECB hiking to 2.25% into a Q1 GDP contraction is a textbook stagflation trap. European luxury caught a bid on the Iran peace headline - that is a one-day trade, not a thesis.
  • The trade nobody is talking about: $TLT as a June 17 setup. Yields dropped 7.9 bps yesterday on oil deflation reducing near-term inflation pressure, but the Fed is not cutting with CPI at 4.2%. TLT is a short-duration tactical trade if yields bounce back above 4.50% ahead of the FOMC. Not a long-term hold. Watch the level.
  • New Home Sales at 10:00 AM ET is your first look at whether the consumer was already cracking before crude fell. Probably yes - but now with cheaper gas, which changes the severity. Worth watching before adding consumer discretionary exposure.
