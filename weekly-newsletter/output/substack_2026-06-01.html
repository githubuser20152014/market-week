Sixty Days of Ceasefire. One Weekend of Strikes. The Bond Market Filed It Under "Whatever."

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

U.S. and Iranian forces exchanged strikes over the weekend. The 60-day Hormuz ceasefire, the one the market had quietly priced as permanent, is over. WTI jumped 4.1% to $90.94. The war premium that evaporated across May is repricing in a single session.

Gold dropped 0.70% to $4,528. The S&P added 0.22%. The bond market moved 0.2 basis points. The Strait of Hormuz is a live conflict zone and Wall Street is debating whether this is a supply disruption or a buying opportunity.

One of these markets is about to look very foolish.

Here's my read:

The Friday close tells the story in miniature: Dow +0.72% to 51,032, S&P +0.22% to 7,580, Nasdaq +0.20% to 26,972. Everything green, except the Russell 2000, down 0.59% to 2,919, because small-caps are the canaries of domestic credit, and right now they are canaries with a cough. The 10-year yield sat at 4.453%, down 0.2 bps. The bond market watched a ceasefire collapse and filed it under "mildly interesting."

The read is two-handed. First: the Strait of Hormuz is a toll booth again. The ceasefire had compressed Brent to $91.53 and stripped the war premium out of oil over 60 days of diplomatic theater. That compression is reversing violently. WTI at $90.94 is the opening bid. There is no diplomatic off-ramp in sight: Trump says he's "not in a hurry" for a deal and fresh strikes resumed Sunday. Second: Kevin Warsh's Fed is running a 3.8% PCE print on a June hold. Higher-for-longer is the posture regardless of what happens in the Strait. The ceasefire was a coiled spring. The strikes let it go. The question is not whether the war premium reprices. The question is whether the Warsh Fed lets equities shrug it off.

The One Trade: Long $XLE

The Hormuz ceasefire just collapsed under live fire, and the war premium that evaporated over the past month is now rebuilding from a base of $90.94 oil with no diplomatic off-ramp in sight.

Confirms: WTI holds above $91.00 through the 10:30 AM ET check and XLE trades above Friday's close with above-average volume in the first hour.

Kill switch: WTI reverses below $89.00 on a renewed ceasefire announcement or back-channel diplomacy signal. That single headline collapses the supply-disruption thesis. Get out fast.

  • Don't you dare fade $USO before 10:30 AM ET. The WTI move is live and the session is young. If WTI holds $91 through the morning check, the energy rotation accelerates into the afternoon. If it fades below $89, someone picked up the phone to Tehran overnight.
  • Stay away from $IWM until Warsh blinks or IWM reclaims 2,940 on volume. Small-caps are caught between higher-for-longer borrowing costs and an oil shock that hits domestic consumer spending.   • Watch $GLD at $4,550: the trade nobody is talking about. Gold dropped 0.70% during a genuine escalation. That is unusual and suspicious. If GLD reclaims $4,550 intraday while Hormuz headlines are still running hot, buyers are stepping back in and the dip was technical. If it cannot reclaim $4,550, real yields are in control and the dip-buy thesis is dead.
  • The Berkshire housing bet is the contrarian signal. Greg Abel's first major deal as Berkshire CEO: a $6.8 billion acquisition of Taylor Morrison.   • $ITA and $XAR are the durable structural trade. Fresh Middle East escalation plus IISS Shangri-La Dialogue signals keep U.S. defense contractors in focus, even as European names cool after the spending boom. The kill switch is a credible ceasefire: one headline deflates the thesis in a session.
