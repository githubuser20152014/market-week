The S&P dropped 1.6% yesterday. Nasdaq fell 2.0%. The headline explanation looks obvious: CPI printed 4.2% YoY and the U.S. struck Iranian targets near Bandar Abbas, prompting Tehran to declare the Strait of Hormuz closed.

But the three markets that didn't move the way they should have tell the more important story.

Bonds didn't rally.

The 10-year yield fell just 2.4 basis points to 4.53% on a day when equities sold hard. In a genuine flight-to-safety move, yields fall as money rotates into Treasuries. They didn't. The bond market isn't calling this recession panic. It's calling it inflation. When bonds refuse to rally on an equity selloff, it means the same force crushing stocks is also threatening bonds: a Fed that cannot cut.

Gold didn't move.

Closed basically flat at $4,104. A stagflationary print plus a closure of 20% of global oil supply, and gold's answer was a 0.09% decline. Either the geopolitical risk premium was already priced in at these levels, or gold is fatigued as a safe haven. Neither is a bullish read for the traditional "buy gold on chaos" trade.

Crude told the real story.

WTI fell 0.6% to $89.52 on the session - but Brent spiked to $94.55 intraday on the Hormuz announcement. The Brent-WTI spread blowing out is the honest signal. This affects Middle Eastern export flows first. Iran controls the only exit route for roughly 20 million barrels per day. That's not a temporary disruption.

The corporate proof point nobody wanted.

Oracle beat earnings expectations. It dropped anyway, after announcing a $20 billion capital raise for AI data centers. The market is now systematically penalizing AI infrastructure dilution - this is the third major tech name to see this pattern. The AI revolution's bill has arrived, and it's being paid in equity.

The second-order story: food and fertilizer.

A Hormuz closure doesn't just affect oil. Iran is a significant fertilizer exporter, and elevated Persian Gulf shipping costs ripple into agricultural supply chains across South and Southeast Asia. Food inflation in emerging markets is the lagged consequence nobody is modeling yet.

Which market got it right?

The bond market's verdict - paralysis, not a rally - is the most honest read. This is stagflation: inflation that prevents the Fed from cutting into a slowdown. In that environment, both bonds and equities face headwinds. The market worth watching today isn't futures. It's crude. At $89 WTI, oil is pricing in some risk premium but not the full Hormuz catastrophe scenario. Watch where crude opens.
