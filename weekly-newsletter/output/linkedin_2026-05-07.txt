The Hormuz framework just got priced into the market in real time—and everyone's looking at the wrong trade.

Oil dropped 2.55% to $92.66. Tech ripped 2%. Airlines are up because jet fuel just became cheaper. That's the obvious thesis. But look at what happened in the other markets yesterday.

Gold climbed 1.27% *alongside* the equity rally. The dollar index fell to 97.84. The 10-year yield dropped 6 basis points to 4.356%. That's not a clean risk-on session. That's stagflation hedging. Markets are saying: "De-escalation is priced into oil. But I don't trust the Fed's inflation fight."

PCE is running at 4.5%. Central banks bought 244 tonnes of gold in Q1 alone. The Fed's own FOMC showed an 8-4 split on rate hikes. That's not a July cut. That's a defensive positioning call.

Here's where it gets interesting: the oil drop is real, and airlines benefit from lower jet fuel. But the simultaneous bid in gold, the weakness in the dollar, and the flat futures open (+0.08% on S&P) tell a deeper story. The market is rotating out of energy into airlines and industrials—but it's *also* hedging the stagflation risk that the ceasefire doesn't solve.

Shell posted earnings yesterday and beat estimates, but the beat was driven by the war premium that's now evaporating. Strip out the energy price surge and the numbers are ordinary. Energy names are now under pressure as the forward curve reprices lower.

The key risk: if Iran rejects the Hormuz framework or any naval incident escalates before noon ET, WTI reclaims $97 immediately. That flips the entire thesis. The Strait premium isn't gone—it's paused.

The second-order angle nobody is talking about: wind and renewables names caught a bid yesterday. The Iran war accelerated the EU energy independence narrative, and wind giants reported profit beats. That's the trade that could compound if the ceasefire holds beyond today.

So which market got it right? The bond market. Yields fell, but they're still above 4.3%, which means the Fed's stagflation trap is still in play. Gold is the inflation hedge. Airlines are the oil hedge. Energy is the short. But the real position is: prepare for rate hikes, not cuts.
