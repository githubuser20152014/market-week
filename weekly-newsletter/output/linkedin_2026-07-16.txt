The S&P 500 closed half a percent from an all-time high yesterday. Here's why you shouldn't trust it.

Cooling PPI data, a soft CPI print earlier in the week, and a clean earnings beat from the big banks gave equities every excuse to celebrate. The problem is that three other markets showed up to the same party and refused to drink the champagne.

Start with bonds. The 10-year yield fell just 4 basis points to 4.545%, a rounding error dressed up as a rally. If the disinflation story were real conviction rather than a relief bounce, yields would have moved harder. Instead, Fed Chair Warsh spent the day reminding Congress the Fed answers to no one, and traders are still pricing 70-73% odds of a September hike. Bonds aren't buying the "inflation is dead" narrative. They're renting it.

Then there's gold, sitting flat at $4,044 while risk assets ran higher. In a genuine risk-on session, gold usually gives something back. It didn't. That's not boring price action, that's a market that can't decide whether the inflation fight is over or the Middle East is about to reopen it.

And then there's oil. Iran declared a red line on the Strait of Hormuz. The U.S. Navy has been running an active blockade for five consecutive nights. WTI crude closed at $79.72, up a whopping 0.15%. A market pricing a real probability of supply disruption through one of the world's most critical shipping corridors does not sit still. This one did.

The corporate proof point that ties it together: United Airlines beat earnings estimates but flagged $6 billion in added fuel costs for the year. That's not a headline about one airline. That's the real-economy translation of what oil traders are still refusing to price in. When the input cost shows up in guidance before it shows up in the crude tape, the tape is behind, not the company.

The second-order story nobody's covering: tanker war-risk insurance. Every day the Hormuz standoff continues without a resolution, the premium shippers pay to move crude through that corridor compounds, quietly, off the exchange, long before it ever hits a futures print. That cost gets passed through eventually. It always does.

So which market got it right? Equities, betting the disinflation trade is real and geopolitics is noise? Or bonds, gold, and the corporate earnings tape, all quietly pricing in a world where the Fed stays hawkish and the Gulf stays hot?

My money's on the dissenters. Equities are pricing a clean narrative. Everything else is pricing a messier one, and messier usually wins.
