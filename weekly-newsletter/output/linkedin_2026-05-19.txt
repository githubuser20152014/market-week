Trump postponed the Iran strike and oil fell 5%. The Dow added 160 points and celebrated. But Nasdaq dropped 0.51%. Russell 2000 fell 0.65%. Here's why you shouldn't trust yesterday's relief rally.

The bond market said no.

The 10-year yield climbed to 4.623% while equities barely moved - a clear rejection of the "peace is bullish" narrative. G7 Finance Ministers met in Paris specifically to flag extreme bond volatility driven by the energy shock. The BoE and ECB are both signaling June rate hikes into stalling GDP growth. When bonds refuse to follow equities higher on "good news," the good news isn't being priced correctly.

Gold refused to celebrate.

Crude crashed 5% and gold held at $4,545. That's buyers defending it on stagflation and geopolitical grounds, not momentum. The signal: the structural inflation equation never went anywhere. It just got temporarily rebranded as "not an emergency today."

Crude is still elevated.

Oil at $103 is elevated with Iran conflict live. The immediate Strait of Hormuz closure risk is off the table, but the structural premium remains. This wasn't vindication. It was a pause.

The proof is global.

KOSPI fell 3.25% while the Dow climbed. South Korea prices in energy import costs and AI supply chain disruptions faster than most markets. When Seoul agrees with bonds over Wall Street, central banks are watching supply and liquidity, not equity momentum.

Here's the verdict:

Four markets voted stagflation yesterday. One voted relief. Bonds, gold, crude-at-elevated-levels, and small caps all said the same thing: this peace is a delay, not a solution. Which market usually gets the macro right on inflation, credit, and supply?

The bond market. And it's screaming stagflation: expensive oil, rising yields, shrinking growth.
