The Morning Brief, 2026-07-16

Daily Edition · Market intelligence at the open

The Brief

The S&P 500 parked half a percent from an all-time high last night, because nothing says confidence like ignoring a war. KOSPI didn't get the memo: down 6.4% after the Bank of Korea yanked rates higher, and Nasdaq futures are following its lead, off 0.8% pre-market. Wall Street is celebrating cooling inflation while the rest of the world prices in the Strait of Hormuz, and one of them is about to be wrong.

U.S. equities closed higher across the board Tuesday, because two soft inflation prints in a row is apparently all it takes to forget the Fed exists. The S&P 500 gained 0.38% to 7,572, the Nasdaq led at +0.62% to 26,269, the Dow added 0.29% to 52,659, and the Russell 2000 tagged along at +0.39%. 10-year yields dropped 4 basis points to 4.545%, a rounding error dressed up as a rally. Gold sat flat at $4,044, refusing to pick a side, oil ticked up to $79.72 like it knows something equities don't, and the USD Index barely blinked at 100.53.

The twin engines were cooler-than-expected PPI data, on the heels of Tuesday's CPI surprise of 3.5% year-on-year against a 3.8% forecast, and a clean earnings beat from the big banks. Think of the bond market as a smoke detector: two soft inflation prints in a row and it stopped beeping, so equities took that as the all-clear to push toward record highs. Fed Chair Warsh didn't get the same signal. He kept his hawkish posture intact, insisted the Fed answers to no one while confirming he meets often with the Trump administration, and traders still price 70-73% odds of a September hike. The rally has a chaperone standing in the corner.

What it means for you

For positioning, the disinflation trade is alive, but it's on a leash. Falling yields favor rate-sensitive ETFs (TLT, XLRE, XLU), except Warsh is still holding the leash and hasn't decided whether to let go. Financials (XLF) got a clean earnings catalyst from BlackRock and Morgan Stanley, proof that printing money on other people's money never goes out of style. Tech (QQQ) takes the direct hit today: the Strait of Hormuz escalation means energy costs bleed into airline and industrial margins, and Iran's threat to shut the corridor entirely is a supply shock that oil bulls (XLE, USO) are already frontrunning. TSMC's $100 billion Arizona investment is a genuine tailwind for domestic chip exposure (SOXX), but the KOSPI's 6.4% collapse on the Bank of Korea's surprise hike is a reminder that the rest of Asia's tech supply chain didn't get invited to the party.

Going into today, Nasdaq futures are off 0.83% and S&P futures are down 0.24%, a soft open that's about geopolitics, not a macro reversal. The KOSPI's crash is the sharpest overnight signal, a tightening surprise that mugged export-heavy Korean tech and auto names on its way out the door. There's no scheduled U.S. data today, so the Strait of Hormuz headline is the only thing that matters. Watch WTI: a break above $80.50 on any escalation would accelerate the energy rotation and lean on Nasdaq. The S&P's technical level is 7,500, old resistance playing a new role as support. Hold it through the first hour and the bull trend survives another day.

The One Trade

XLE: Long

Iran's Hormuz red line declaration, now backed by five nights of U.S. airstrikes and an active naval blockade, is a supply disruption that WTI at $79.72 has not fully priced: energy is the one sector where the geopolitical catalyst and the technical setup align today.

Confirms: WTI crude holds above $79.50 through 10 AM ET and XLE opens above its 5-day moving average near $95.50, confirming buyers are not fading the Hormuz headline. Kill switch: Iran signals a diplomatic off-ramp or U.S. officials walk back strike threats before the open, sending WTI below $78.50 and collapsing the geopolitical premium in energy names.

Positioning Notes

  • Energy (XLE, USO): The Strait of Hormuz threat from Iran is not priced in at $79.72 WTI. A fifth consecutive night of U.S. airstrikes and a naval blockade cutting Iranian shipping create a supply shock setup. Add exposure on any dip below $79.50 WTI; reduce if Hormuz rhetoric softens and crude fades back below $78.
  • Semiconductors (SOXX): TSMC's $100 billion Arizona commitment signals long-term domestic chip investment, but KOSPI's 6.4% crash on the Bank of Korea hike signals near-term pain for Asian semiconductor supply chains. Hold domestic SOXX but avoid Korea-heavy EM tech ETFs (EWT, KORU) until won stabilization is confirmed.
  • Financials (XLF): UnitedHealth and airline earnings beat, but United flagged $6 billion in added fuel costs, which is a margin warning for the broader industrial economy. XLF is supported by strong bank earnings; trim UnitedHealth-adjacent healthcare exposure (XLV) if oil spikes on Hormuz news and rekindles inflation fears.
  • Rates and Duration (TLT, XLRE): Two consecutive soft inflation prints pulled the 10-year down 4 basis points to 4.545%. If today's session holds without a hawkish catalyst from Warsh or another Fed speaker, TLT can extend the bounce. Fail condition: yields reverse above 4.58% on renewed September hike pricing, which flips the trade.
  • Nasdaq/Tech (QQQ): Futures are down 0.83% pre-market. The combination of Warsh's hawkish independence signal, Iran's energy threat raising input costs, and no relief catalyst on the calendar today makes QQQ the most vulnerable major index at the open. Reduce exposure above Nasdaq 26,400; re-engage only if the index holds the 26,000 level through 11 AM ET.
