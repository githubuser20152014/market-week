Hormuz Closed. WTI Fell 1.88%. The Market Has Already Written the Peace Deal.

The Morning Brief · Market intelligence at the open

The Strait of Hormuz is closed. China just sanctioned dozens of US firms. Kevin Warsh is threatening a 2026 rate hike. Nasdaq futures are up 1.5% pre-market. Welcome to risk-on Monday, where bad news is apparently the new buying signal.

On Friday, the Nasdaq gained 1.9% and the Russell 2000 gained 2.1%. The Dow added an insulting +0.14%, because industrials and financials noticed the Fed's dot-plot and decided to sit this one out. The S&P 500 closed at 7,500. Gold did absolutely nothing at $4,227, up 0.09%. When gold goes flat during a broad risk-on session, that is not complacency. That is institutional money keeping the insurance policy active and not saying why. WTI crude dropped 1.88% to $75.16 on the same day Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz. Oil traders have already written the resolution. The resolution has not been written yet.

The market is pricing a peace deal on a closed shipping lane. One of those things is real.

Here's my read:

The theory is Burgenstock. Swiss-mediated US-Iran talks reported "major progress" last week, Trump softened his posture on Anthropic as a signal of broader de-escalation from Washington, and the crude market decided to price in a diplomatic outcome that has not been signed, formalized, or announced. That is the whole trade. The 10Y yield sits at 4.45%, anchored by Warsh's hawkish hold and a dot-plot that whispers about a 2026 hike - which is why the Dow, stuffed with rate-sensitive industrials and financials, could not keep pace with Nasdaq. S&P futures are up 0.66% to 7,557, Nasdaq futures up 1.5% to 30,717, Dow futures up a ceremonial 0.04%. The Nikkei closed up 1.55% on the same risk-on tone. The Hang Seng fell 0.65% on China's trade curbs - because the US-China friction did not go on holiday just because US equities are green.

The One Trade: Long $IWM

Russell 2000 led the tape by 100 basis points over Nasdaq on Friday, signaling genuine domestic small-cap rotation. These names carry zero direct exposure to China's new trade curbs on US firms and zero Hormuz shipping risk that could reprice megacap tech lower. That is the trade: get the growth bid without the geopolitical baggage.

Confirms: IWM holds above 207.50 through the first 30 minutes of trading and the Dow does not close the gap with Nasdaq futures, confirming the rotation is real rather than futures noise.

Kill switch: Burgenstock talks produce a negative headline, or China escalates trade curbs to include broader consumer tech. That triggers a broad growth sell-off that pulls small caps down with the tape regardless of their fundamental insulation. Get out fast.

What else I'm watching

  • QQQ - take the open, set a stop. Nasdaq futures up 1.5% on Anthropic de-escalation and AI capex tailwinds is a real bid. But China's new sanctions on US tech firms are also live, and those two things are going to fight each other at the open. Long bias into the open, stop below Friday's close. Don't marry it.
  • Stay away from XLE until Hormuz has a formal resolution in writing. WTI down 1.88% on a closed shipping lane means the crude market is long a peace deal. If that deal does not arrive in headline form today, energy snaps back hard and you are holding the bag. The risk-reward is asymmetric to the downside.
  • Don't you dare sell GLD. Gold at $4,227 refused to move in either direction during a broad risk-on session with a slightly firmer dollar. That is not stale. That is institutional money holding an insurance position and not telegraphing why. If Burgenstock talks break down, gold reprices $50 to $100 higher in a session. Hold it.
  • TLT - the trade nobody should be making. The Fed is hawkish, T-Note futures are down 0.5% pre-market, and there is no near-term catalyst to flip the duration trade until core PCE forces Warsh's hand. Holding long duration here means fighting the Fed and the data calendar simultaneously. Stay out.
