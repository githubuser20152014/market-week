The Morning Brief — 2026-03-24

Daily Edition · Market intelligence at the open

The Brief

Monday's relief trade is already unwinding: Brent back above $100 overnight, the 10Y yield down 5.7bps to 4.334%, and gold near $4,406 sitting unmoved through yesterday's equity celebration — the bond market and commodities never bought the rally. Oil is the swing factor equities chose to ignore on Monday; this morning, it's choosing not to be ignored. Overnight APAC was broadly strong (Hang Seng +2.8%, KOSPI +2.7%), but Europe's early session is flat to mildly mixed, and U.S. futures are pointing to a soft open that questions whether Monday's rally has any follow-through.

Monday's U.S. session closed with broad-based gains: S&P 500 +1.15% to 6,581, Nasdaq +1.38% to 21,947, Dow +1.38% to 46,208, and small-caps led with Russell 2000 +2.29% to 2,494. WTI crude surged 2.62% to $90.44, gold was nearly flat at $4,406, the 10Y Treasury yield fell 5.7bps to 4.334%, and the USD Index edged up 0.34% to 99.29. Cross-assets painted a split picture: equities celebrated while bonds and gold quietly flagged caution.

The driver is straightforward and uncomfortable: oil is the tail wagging the dog. Brent reclaiming $100 and WTI at $90+ is being attributed to stalled Iran de-escalation and active threats to the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint through which roughly 20% of global oil flows. Think of the Strait as the world's most important pipeline valve; even a partial closure or credible threat shuts off supply to Asia, Europe, and the U.S. simultaneously. Monday's equity rally was likely a relief bounce on initial ceasefire optimism that has since faded, which is why crude is re-accelerating overnight while futures fade. The Chevron CEO's comment that the Iran war premium is 'not fully priced in' is a forward warning, not a backward observation.

For investors, the energy trade is back on the table with real structural support — not a speculative spike (XLE, XOP). Energy ETFs with upstream exposure benefit directly from $90+ WTI, and integrated majors like Chevron and Exxon provide dividend cushion if oil retreats. On the flip side, the White House killing East Coast wind projects in favor of what appears to be fossil-fuel-friendly policy is a headwind for clean energy ETFs (ICLN, FAN) — that $1B TotalEnergies payment to exit offshore wind is a political signal, not just a financial one. Defensive positioning makes sense alongside energy: TLT is worth watching as yields drift lower on safe-haven demand, though a renewed inflation shock from sustained $100 Brent would quickly reverse that. Gold (GLD) near all-time highs with minimal reaction to yesterday's risk-on suggests smart money isn't selling the hedge.

Going into today's open, S&P futures are off ~0.17% and Dow futures -0.20%, pointing to a muted-to-soft open that digests Monday's gains against a persistently hot oil market. APAC's strength (Hang Seng +2.8%, KOSPI +2.7%) is yesterday's news and unlikely to carry forward given Europe's flat early session. The sole calendar item — Existing Home Sales at 10:00 AM ET — is low-importance in this environment. The real swing factor today is oil: watch whether WTI holds above $90 or extends toward $93-95, as that level would start pricing in a more severe supply disruption scenario and pressure margins for consumer discretionary and transport sectors. Any Strait of Hormuz headline — escalation or de-escalation — is the binary event risk for the session.

Positioning Notes

  • Energy (XLE, XOP): Stay long or add on any intraday dip toward yesterday's open levels. WTI at $90+ with the Chevron CEO explicitly flagging underpriced Iran risk means the fundamental bid is real. XOP gives you higher-beta upstream leverage if you believe $95+ is in play; XLE is the steadier hand with integrated majors providing dividend support if oil pulls back.
  • Clean Energy (ICLN, FAN): Reduce or avoid. The White House paying $1B to kill offshore wind projects is a policy signal that the regulatory tailwind for renewables is reversing. This isn't a one-day story — it's a structural headwind for permitting, subsidies, and investor confidence in the sector.
  • Treasuries (TLT, IEF): Watch, don't chase. The 10Y yield falling 5.7bps while equities rallied is a yellow flag — bonds are pricing in risk-off that stocks haven't fully acknowledged yet. If oil extends above $92-93 and inflation fears reignite, TLT gets hit from both sides. Hold existing positions as a hedge but don't add aggressively until the oil/inflation picture clarifies.
  • Gold (GLD): Maintain. Gold near $4,406 with virtually no reaction to Monday's equity rally tells you institutional investors are not releasing their hedge. The combination of geopolitical risk, dollar softness (EUR/USD at 1.16, GBP above 1.34), and a Fed that can't hike into an oil shock keeps the floor under gold firm.
  • Small Caps (IWM): Caution after Monday's +2.3% surge. Russell 2000 outperformance in a risk-on day is normal, but small-caps are disproportionately exposed to higher energy input costs and tighter credit conditions — and the Apollo private credit fund restricting 55% of withdrawal requests is a quiet liquidity warning for the leveraged end of the market. The failure condition is specific: if WTI extends above $93 or the Apollo credit restriction story gains traction, IWM gives back Monday's gains first.
