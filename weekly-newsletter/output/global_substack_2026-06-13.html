$84.88 Oil, 4.2% Inflation, and a New Fed Chair Walking Into an Ambush.

Framework Foundry · Global Investor Edition

The war premium took five sessions to build and one Thursday to die. Trump cancelled the follow-on Iran strikes. The Strait of Hormuz reopened. WTI crude collapsed 8.73% to $84.88, erasing almost every dollar the geopolitical panic had added. The S&P 500 celebrated with a 1.8% single-session surge. The market got the resolution it wanted.

Then May CPI came in at 4.2% year-over-year. Three-year high. Monthly gain of 0.5%. PPI confirmed the pipeline is still on fire. The rate-cut narrative is not on pause. It is dead.

Kevin Warsh walks into his first FOMC meeting on June 16-17 carrying a 4.2% inflation print, a ceasefire nobody is sure will hold 60 days, and markets pricing in a live surprise hike. The man inherited quite a welcome gift.

Here's my read:

The market's thesis this week was simple: ceasefire = risk-on. And it was right, for about 24 hours. The Russell 2000 surged 2.84% - the standout performer - while the Nasdaq fell 0.68% under the weight of AI equity dilution from SMCI (-17% on a $7 billion share issuance), Alphabet, and Meta. The S&P 500 was essentially flat at -0.12%. The relief rally happened. It just happened very specifically in the parts of the market most punished by the Hormuz shock, not in the parts being slowly diluted by megacap capital raises.

The USD Index dropped 0.4% to 99.75, which should not have happened given a 4.2% CPI print. The ceasefire redirected capital back into risk assets faster than the inflation data could reinforce safe-haven dollar demand. EUR/USD gained 0.42% to 1.1573 despite the ECB hiking into a Q1 GDP contraction. Europe tightened into a growth hole and its markets still outperformed: CAC 40 +2.67%, Euro Stoxx 50 +2.28%. The world is running on relief, not fundamentals.

Gold dropped 2.53% to $4,215 despite the hottest CPI print in three years. That is the tell. When gold sells off on 4.2% inflation, the market is saying it believes the ceasefire story more than the inflation story. One of those two things is going to be proven wrong by June 17.

The One Trade: Long $IWM

The Russell 2000 closed at 2,943.99, up 2.84% - the strongest week of any major U.S. index. The setup is specific: small caps got crushed hardest by the Hormuz shock (rate-sensitive balance sheets, domestic focus, no geopolitical hedge), and snapped back the hardest once the ceasefire was announced. The intraday range of 2,795 to 2,969 tells you buyers absorbed the panic and recovered almost all of it. That is not a bounce. That is positioning.

The structural case is rotation. The Nasdaq is absorbing AI equity dilution from SMCI, Alphabet, and Meta flooding the market with new shares to fund data center buildouts. Small caps carry none of that overhang. IWM is a direct play on a Fed hold: the 10-year yield at 4.49% is already below its weekly high of 4.56%, and every basis point that doesn't go higher is a refinancing tailwind for the rate-sensitive companies dominating IWM.

Confirms: IWM holds above 2,900 into the FOMC decision and the 10-year yield stays below 4.65%.

Kill switch: Warsh delivers a surprise 25 basis point hike on June 17. The 10-year spikes above 4.70%. Small cap refinancing fears re-emerge and get out fast.

What else I'm watching

  • GLD - gold gave back 2.53% to $4,215 on the ceasefire unwind. But a 4.2% CPI print with hot PPI means the inflation case for gold is very much alive. Don't you dare sell it into weakness before the FOMC. If Warsh holds and signals patience, gold reasserts hard.
  • FEZ / EWQ - Europe outperformed on both the ceasefire relief and the weaker dollar. The ECB hike into a GDP contraction is aggressive policy in a fragile growth environment. Stay long but trim into strength if EUR/USD stalls at 1.16 - the stagflation overhang limits how far this runs.
  • EEM - MSCI EM posted +2.74%, the strongest regional return this week. Lower oil eases import bills across Southeast Asia and South Asia; dollar softness adds the currency tailwind. The trade holds while the ceasefire holds. The moment Hormuz talks break down, EM gets hit from both sides.
  • USO - stay away from energy exposure until the Hormuz memorandum of understanding is formally signed. The war premium is mostly gone at $84.88. A ceasefire breakdown reprices WTI to $90+ immediately. You are not getting paid to hold that tail risk right now.
  • TLT - the trade nobody is talking about. If Warsh hikes on June 17, long-duration bonds get crushed. If he holds and signals patience, the relief rally in TLT could be sharp. The asymmetry cuts both ways. This is a watch-and-react, not a pre-position.
