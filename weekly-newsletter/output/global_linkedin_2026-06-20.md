The peace deal happened. Markets celebrated for 48 hours. Then Kevin Warsh published a dot plot with nine colleagues who want to raise rates, and the dollar climbed 1.32% to 100.85.

The "War Premium" is dead. The "Warsh Premium" is alive. Most investors have not made the switch yet.

**The dissenting markets:**

Crude told the clean story. WTI fell 5.97% to $76.54 after the Versailles MOU reopened the Strait of Hormuz. The $8 geopolitical risk premium mechanically decompressed. That part is straightforward.

Bonds told the uncomfortable story. The 10-year Treasury held at 4.49%. The 30-year at 4.97%. Nine of 19 FOMC members officially penciled in a rate hike for 2026 at Warsh's debut meeting. The bond market is pricing a world where the peace dividend arrives alongside a Fed that is not done tightening. Those two things are not compatible for long.

Gold told the most interesting story. It fell 2.30% to $4,172 despite 4.2% CPI, its second consecutive weekly decline. When gold sells off on the hottest inflation print in three years, the market is saying it believes the peace deal more than the inflation data. Gold's weekly floor at $4,138 held for a reason.

**The corporate proof point:**

Accenture cut its full-year revenue growth forecast from 3-5% to 3-4% and fell 17% in a single session on June 18. The specific causes: U.S. Federal procurement delays and geopolitical headwinds. This is the enterprise IT bellwether. If Capgemini and India's major IT firms (Infosys, Wipro) confirm the same pattern in upcoming results, the AI spending supercycle has a credibility problem at the implementation layer, even while SpaceX trades at a $2.8 trillion post-IPO valuation. Both data points exist simultaneously. Only one can be right.

**The second-order story nobody is covering:**

Japan raised rates to 1% for the first time since 1995, and the Nikkei gained 6.69% in a single week to close at 71,250. That combination is counterintuitive only if you have forgotten that Japan has been trapped in deflationary stagnation since the Clinton administration. The 1% rate is the first credible signal in 30 years that idle corporate cash loses purchasing power. Japanese corporations have been hoarding capital for decades waiting for exactly this message. A structural re-rating of Japan's equity risk premium downward is a slow-moving event, not a one-week trade. Meanwhile, the yen weakened further this week (the Warsh dollar surge overpowered the BoJ signal), keeping exporter margins padded while domestic sentiment recovers. Most global portfolios are underweight Japan. That is the positioning gap worth watching.

**The verdict:**

The equity market is pricing the peace dividend. The bond market is pricing the Warsh premium. One of them is right.

My read: the equity market is correct that the energy shock is over. The bond market is correct that nine Fed officials want to raise rates into a falling-inflation, peace-dividend quarter. The problem is that both positions cannot be fully priced simultaneously. Something in this setup reprices before August.
