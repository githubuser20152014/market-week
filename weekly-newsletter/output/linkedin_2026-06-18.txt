Yesterday the market sold off on two pieces of good news. That's worth sitting with.

Peace in the Gulf knocked $4 a barrel off oil. A new Fed Chair who might actually fight inflation knocked 1.2% off the S&P. Both are net positives for the long-term economy. Neither was good for anyone positioned for the old regime.

Here's why I don't trust today's 2.7% bounce:

---

Bonds said no.

The 10Y Treasury yield rose to 4.49% on Wednesday. That's the bond market agreeing with Warsh, not fighting him. When equities sell off and yields rise simultaneously, both asset classes are telling you the same thing: the easy money era is over, and markets are still priced for it.

---

Gold said the same thing.

Gold dropped 1.6% to $4,289. Some of that is the Iran deal removing the war premium. But gold held above $4,200 even as geopolitical risk evaporated - because fiscal uncertainty and a Fed Chair who means business on inflation created a new floor. Gold is not collapsing. It's repricing. That distinction matters.

---

Crude told a cleaner story.

WTI fell 4% to $73.72 on the Strait of Hormuz reopening. The 60-day negotiation window before the uranium dilution deadline means the next oil headline is a supply surplus story, not a supply shock story. The geopolitical floor is gone for now.

---

The corporate proof point:

JetBlue announced it's cutting its footprint at Newark and LaGuardia, retreating to lower-cost Fort Lauderdale operations. When airlines start abandoning premium northeast hubs, the consumer spend assumptions baked into discretionary sector valuations deserve a second look. This is what rate-sensitive, high-cost operations look like under sustained monetary pressure.

---

The second-order story nobody is covering:

Hegseth warned NATO allies that some nations will "fail" the U.S. defense review. This is not diplomatic noise. It's a structural budget mandate. European sovereign defense spending is a policy-locked expenditure for the next decade regardless of what happens in the Strait or at the Fed. That tailwind has a 10-year runway.

---

Which market got it right?

Bonds and crude are saying the structural regime changed yesterday, not just the tactical setup. Today's futures bounce is the market choosing to celebrate the peace deal and ignore Warsh. That works until Warsh speaks again. The rate-cut story is dead. The question is whether equities have fully priced that in.
