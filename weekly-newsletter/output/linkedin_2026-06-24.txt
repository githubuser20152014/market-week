The Nasdaq dropped 2.2% yesterday and this morning futures are up 0.3%.

Here's the part that should concern you: the bounce has no catalyst behind it.

The AI capex reckoning that drove two consecutive sessions of methodical selling hasn't been resolved overnight. It's been paused.

---

The dissenting markets told the real story.

Bonds didn't move. The 10-year yield fell just 1.2 bps to 4.451%. A bond market pricing a growth collapse would have moved more than that. This isn't a macro emergency — it's a repricing of growth expectations within a sector.

Gold fell 1.1% to $4,085 alongside equities while the dollar hit a 13-month high. Gold is supposed to rally when equities sell off on uncertainty. It didn't. That means this isn't geopolitical panic or systemic stress — it's a valuation reset in high-beta growth, and cash is the destination, not precious metals.

Crude dropped 1.8% to $71.87 and Brent fell below $76. The Iran-IAEA interim deal is compressing supply disruption premiums, and softening macro demand signals are layering on top. Energy isn't a safe haven here — it's a second front opening in the same risk-off trade.

---

The corporate proof point that makes it concrete: Micron fell 13% on earnings.

Two years of hyperscaler AI capex announcements, and the semiconductor supplier closest to the actual chip demand is signaling the revenue model hasn't caught up with the infrastructure spend. Alphabet, Amazon, and Broadcom all took multi-session hits for the same reason: the market is asking whether this capex produces earnings or produces press releases, and the answer is moving in one direction.

---

The angle nobody is covering: Brent below $76 is partly an Iran story, but it's also a dollar story.

A dollar at a 13-month high makes dollar-denominated commodities more expensive for every non-US buyer. That feedback loop — US tech sells off, dollar strengthens, global commodity demand softens, energy stocks take a second hit — is the transmission mechanism that turns a tech re-rating into something broader. Watch the dollar index, not just the Nasdaq.

---

Which market got it right?

Gold said: this isn't a crisis, it's a correction. Bonds said: growth isn't collapsing, just repricing. Cash said: I'll wait until someone answers the revenue question.

All three are probably correct. The pre-market bounce is the market testing whether anyone has a better answer than "wait." Until the Nasdaq reclaims its prior close on volume this morning, nobody does.
