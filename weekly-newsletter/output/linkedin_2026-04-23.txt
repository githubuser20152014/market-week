The market rallied hard yesterday. Here's why you shouldn't trust it.

S&P +1.05%. Nasdaq +1.64%. Risk-on everywhere. The catalyst? Trump extended the Iran ceasefire.

That's it. One tweet. One announcement. No Iranian delegation in Islamabad. No signed agreement. No lifted blockade. Just a pause on the fire alarm — and the market decided to celebrate.

Here's what the other markets were saying at the exact same time:

The bond market didn't move. The 10-year yield barely twitched during a +1% equity surge. That almost never happens. When stocks rally hard on geopolitical relief, bonds usually sell off as money rotates into risk. They didn't. The fixed income market looked at the ceasefire extension and shrugged.

Gold held at $4,719. A genuine risk-off resolution sends gold down. It didn't go down. Institutional money sat on its hedge and watched equities celebrate without them.

Crude didn't give back a single point. WTI closed at $94.54 — up 1.70% — even as peace optimism supposedly swept through equities. Because the Hormuz naval blockade is still physically active. Ships still can't move freely through the strait. The energy market trades facts, not headlines.

And then earnings told the rest of the story.

ServiceNow dropped 14% because Iran-war disruption is now directly hitting enterprise software subscription revenue. A top-tier SaaS company. Not an oil major. Not a defense contractor. A cloud software business. The conflict has moved from a geopolitical risk to a corporate cash flow event — and the market hasn't finished pricing that across the sector.

There's also a second-order story almost nobody is covering: fertilizer.

The Hormuz blockade isn't just disrupting oil. It's disrupting the petroleum-derived products used to manufacture fertilizer. Analysts are quietly flagging a potential food supply shock by early 2027. Agricultural commodity prices will feel this before equity markets do.

So here's the real question going into today's open: which market got it right yesterday?

If you believe bonds, gold, and crude — yesterday's rally was a trade, not a trend. The structural risks are intact. The ceasefire is a pause button, not a peace treaty.

If you believe equities — the worst is behind us and risk assets are going higher.

One of them is wrong. The bond market has a better track record.
