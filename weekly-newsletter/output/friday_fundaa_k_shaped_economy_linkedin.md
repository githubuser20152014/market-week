# LinkedIn Post — Friday Fundaa: K-Shaped Economy

**The economy is doing great. Just not yours.**

GDP grew 2.1% last year. Wages are up 3.9%. Unemployment is 4.3%. By every official measure, the American economy is resilient.

Your rent, grocery bill, and health insurance premium beg to differ.

Here's what's actually happening.

**Your 2026 cost of living vs 2020:**
- Rent: $1,200/month → $1,700/month. That's $500 more, every month, for the same apartment.
- Groceries: $450/month → $580/month. Same items, same store, same list.
- Health insurance: $1,450 more per year, or about $120 more a month.

Total: $750 more every month. $9,000 more every year. On things you cannot cut.

**Now the other side of the ledger:**

$30,000 sitting in a plain S&P 500 index fund in January 2020. No stock-picking. No active management. Just ownership. That $30,000 is worth $67,000 today. A $37,000 gain without a single salary negotiation.

GDP averaged these two realities together and said: the economy is fine. And it's not wrong. GDP is a weighted average of every dollar of output in the country. The problem is the two groups being averaged are moving in completely opposite directions.

**Who owns what:**

The top 1% of US households own 31% of all wealth and nearly half the entire stock market. The bottom 50% own 2.5% of total wealth and just 1% of all stocks. When asset values compound, the gains flow overwhelmingly to one side of that ledger.

This is the K-shaped economy. Two branches from the same starting point in 2020. The top branch (asset owners): diverging upward. The bottom branch (wage earners): moving mostly sideways. GDP is the average of both. The average looks fine. The branches are not having the same experience.

The economy isn't lying. It's just describing the people who own it, averaged with the people who work it.
