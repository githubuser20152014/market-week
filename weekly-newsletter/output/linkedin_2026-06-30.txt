The Nasdaq's 2.07% Friday gain is one of the more misleading headlines of the month.

Alphabet got added to the Dow Jones Industrial Average. Index funds had no choice but to buy it and sell Verizon. Mechanical rebalancing. Not a macro signal. Not an AI thesis. Not a vote of confidence in the economy. The committee moved a ticker on a list, and the Nasdaq obliged.

Here's why you should look past the headline number.

Bonds didn't buy it. The 10-year Treasury yield slipped just 2 basis points to 4.372%. That is not what yields do in a genuine risk-on session. They rise. The muted move signals fixed income is not convinced this rally has staying power, and it is watching Kashkari's 2026 rate hike warning with more suspicion than equities are.

Gold didn't buy it either. Gold closed at $4,038. The dollar firmed. Equities rallied. Gold held flat. That combination - gold refusing to sell into dollar strength and a risk-on tape - is the session's most important signal. It happens when geopolitical risk premium is durable, not transient. The Iran IRGC drone and missile strikes on U.S. bases in Bahrain and Kuwait were intercepted. But the Doha diplomatic talks are not resolved. The Strait of Hormuz floor remains priced.

Oil sat at $70.97 and told you the same story. The supply glut narrative pulls it lower. Geopolitical premium from the Iran confrontation provides a floor. Oil is not trending. It is waiting for a headline.

The corporate proof point came from defense. AeroVironment surged 19% on an earnings beat with backlog growing to $1.2 billion. That is not a coincidence in a session where drone and missile intercepts were the morning's top story. Defense spending is not decelerating.

The second-order story nobody is covering: the yen hit a 40-year low at 162.34. Japanese exporters are winning. But the longer the yen stays this weak, the more pressure builds on the Bank of Japan to intervene - and a BOJ intervention would send shockwaves through global carry trades that are currently funding a lot of risk-on positioning in U.S. equities.

Which market got it right? Gold and bonds are telling a different story than the Nasdaq's headline print. One of them is wrong. The market that moved on mechanical index rebalancing has a thin thesis. The market that refused to sell through two geopolitical incidents and a rate hike warning has a durable one.
