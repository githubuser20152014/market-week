### Hormuz Blockade Rewrites the Global Energy Order

The dominant story this week was not a data release or a central bank meeting. It was the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and the UAE's formal exit from OPEC, two events that together represent the most significant structural break in global energy markets in decades. **WTI Crude surged 6.6% to close at $101.94, having touched $110.93 intraday, while Brent peaked at $126/bbl mid-week before retreating as hedge funds locked in profits.** The UAE's departure, effective May 1, liberates ADNOC's $150 billion capacity expansion from quota constraints and fractures the Saudi-led pricing mechanism that has governed oil markets since the 1960s.

The Federal Reserve held rates at **3.5%–3.75%** in what is expected to be Jerome Powell's final meeting, while the Bank of England held at **3.75%** but split 8-4, the widest dissent in years. Both decisions reflect the same impossible arithmetic: war-driven energy inflation is pushing headline CPI higher while slowing global growth pulls the other way. **March PCE inflation printed at 3.5% year-over-year, almost entirely attributable to the Hormuz Premium.** Kevin Warsh's pending confirmation as the next Fed Chair signals a harder line on price stability, which matters for duration and rate-sensitive equity valuations going forward.

**The counterintuitive signal this week: equities shrugged.** The S&P 500 closed up 1.08% and the Nasdaq gained 1.27%, even as crude hit a four-year high and inflation expectations deteriorated. The market is betting that AI-driven productivity, evidenced by strong tech earnings, can outrun stagflation. That is a high-conviction bet with a narrow margin for error. Personal savings sitting at a multi-year low of 3.6% leaves the consumer with little buffer if energy costs keep compounding.

### What it means for you

The Strait of Hormuz closure and the UAE's formal OPEC exit are reshaping energy markets in ways that will take months, not weeks, to resolve. **WTI Crude closed above $100 for the first time in years, and natural gas surged over 10% in a single week.** The Fed held rates steady but made clear that war-driven oil inflation is now a structural obstacle to cutting, and Kevin Warsh's expected confirmation as the next Fed Chair adds a hawkish policy layer heading into summer. Equities in the US held up because AI-driven tech earnings are carrying large-cap indices, but the Russell 2000's underperformance via **IWM** signals that smaller, energy-exposed domestic businesses are already feeling the squeeze. **Gold's -1.65% decline despite the conflict is a warning sign**: rising real yields are suppressing the safe-haven bid, meaning bonds are not the refuge they once were either. For a patient, globally diversified investor, the priority this week is reviewing unhedged energy import exposure in European equity positions like FEZ and EWG, and considering whether energy commodity exposure via USO or UNG belongs in the portfolio as a structural inflation hedge rather than a tactical trade.

### Equity Markets - Tech Carries the US; Europe Stalls

US equities led the world this week, with the Nasdaq's **+1.27% gain** pulling the S&P 500 to close at **7,230**, just off a mid-week high of 7,272. The move was driven by AI-related tech earnings overcoming the energy shock. **Russell 2000 gained only 0.87%**, underperforming large caps, which makes sense: smaller domestic companies face more direct input-cost pressure from $100 crude than the mega-cap tech names that dominate QQQ and SPY. The Dow's more modest **+0.79%** reflects the same dynamic, with industrial and energy-sensitive components lagging software.

Europe was essentially flat to slightly negative. The **DAX eked out +0.31%** while the Euro Stoxx 50 gained a negligible **+0.05%**. The FTSE 100 slipped **-0.14%** and the CAC 40 fell **-0.64%**, the latter dragged by French energy-importing industries repricing to the Hormuz shock. Asia-Pacific was similarly directionless. **Nikkei added only +0.18%** as the Bank of Japan was forced into emergency FX intervention with USDJPY breaching the 160 level. Hang Seng gained +0.17% and ASX 200 fell **-0.65%**, the Australian market likely pressured by commodity-currency volatility rather than domestic factors. The divergence between US tech-led gains and the rest of the world is clean: the AI narrative is a US-listed story, and the energy pain is a global one.

### Currency Markets - Dollar Slides as Yen Forces BoJ's Hand

The USD Index fell **-1.13% to 98.21**, a meaningful move that touched a weekly low of 97.72. The driver was not dollar weakness in isolation but a combination of the Fed's hawkish-pause posture creating a ceiling on near-term rate expectations, and the BoJ's forced intervention as USDJPY breached 160. **The Japanese yen gained +1.61% against the dollar**, the strongest G10 performer of the week, as the Bank of Japan drew its intervention line in the sand. The Swiss franc gained +0.63%, consistent with its safe-haven role in a week defined by Middle East conflict.

The Australian dollar added **+0.81%**, benefiting from commodity exposure as oil and natural gas surged. GBP gained +0.45% despite the BoE's deeply divided hold decision, suggesting markets read the 8-4 split as a pause before cuts rather than a pivot toward hikes. **EUR/USD moved only +0.15% to 1.1723**, the euro's muted response reflecting Europe's acute vulnerability to energy-import inflation from the Hormuz disruption. For globally diversified ETF investors, the broad dollar slide this week is a tailwind for unhedged international holdings, but the structural energy shock argues against chasing that move aggressively.

### Commodities & Metals - Oil Above $100 Changes the Calculus

**WTI Crude's +6.63% surge to $101.94 is the defining commodity move of the week**, and the weekly range tells the full story: from $94.59 to $110.93, a $16 swing driven entirely by the Strait of Hormuz closure and the UAE's OPEC exit. This is not a speculative spike. It reflects a genuine structural break: the cartel that managed supply for six decades has fractured, the world's most critical oil chokepoint is blocked, and the U.S. Navy is enforcing that blockade. Natural gas gained **+10.76% to $2.78**, a direct consequence of the same supply shock cascading into LNG markets. UNG is the cleanest expression of this move.

Gold fell **-1.65% to $4,629.90**, which is counterintuitive given the geopolitical backdrop but explicable: the 10-year Treasury yield rose 7 basis points to **4.38%**, raising the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets, and profit-taking from elevated levels was overdue after gold's extended run. Silver gained a modest +0.92%, holding near $75.95. The gold weakness is worth watching: if it persists while oil keeps climbing, it signals the market is treating this as an inflation shock rather than a risk-off event, with real yield pressure overwhelming the safe-haven bid.

### This Week's Economic Events

The formal economic calendar was sparse this week, but the macro data that did cross was significant. **March PCE inflation printed at 3.5% year-over-year**, almost entirely attributed to energy costs embedded in the Hormuz Premium, and personal savings dropped to **3.6%**, a multi-year low that constrains the consumer's ability to absorb further price increases. The Fed's decision to hold at 3.5%–3.75% was widely expected, but the FOMC statement's explicit reference to oil volatility and the Iran conflict as structural obstacles to returning to 2% inflation was a meaningful shift in language. The BoE's 8-4 split hold at 3.75% is similarly revealing: four members voted to cut, signaling that the growth risk is being taken seriously even as energy inflation complicates the picture. The World Bank's warning of a potential **25% surge in average energy prices this year** is not a forecast that fits neatly into current consensus rate-path models.

The dominant variable next week is whether the Strait of Hormuz blockade shows any signs of resolution or escalation.
