The S&P hit an all-time high yesterday. The Dow set a record. The Nasdaq ripped 3%.

Here's why you shouldn't trust the move: gold didn't sell.

A US-Iran peace deal just removed a war premium from oil (crude fell 4% in a single session) and equities partied like the danger is over. Gold, the asset whose entire job is pricing residual fear, refused to participate. It held firm near its highs instead. When every other risk asset rallies and gold doesn't budge, that's not noise. That's one market telling you the other one is early.

The dissenting voices were everywhere if you looked past the headline numbers:

Gold stayed bid through a risk-on rally that should have crushed it, which only happens when buyers think the underlying risk (a 60-day nuclear negotiation window that hasn't closed, an active military posture in Lebanon that hasn't changed) is still live.

Long bonds barely moved even as oil's collapse handed the Fed a disinflationary gift. If the bond market believed this was structural, yields would already be sprinting lower.

Crude did exactly what it was supposed to do on the news, which is the one market here that's actually behaving rationally. The other two are the ones worth questioning.

The corporate proof point that made the macro story concrete: SpaceX, two days into trading, added another 19.6% to hit a $2.1 trillion valuation, a company that still doesn't turn a profit. That's not a fundamentals story. That's a liquidity story, and liquidity stories are exactly what shows up at the top of risk-on rallies.

The angle nobody's covering: China's retail sales just posted their first year-over-year drop in three years, the same week Wall Street is throwing a party on a different continent. And South Korea's defense stocks are rallying on a peace deal, because nothing says "the war is over" like buying more weapons makers. Somebody in this picture is mispricing the world, and it's probably not the country whose retail sales just rolled over.

So which market got it right: equities celebrating a peace deal, or gold refusing to celebrate with them? My money's on the one that's been right more often. Gold doesn't throw parties. It throws caution, and right now caution is the trade nobody on a trading floor wants to admit they're watching.
