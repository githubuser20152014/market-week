## LinkedIn

**There's a chart that has predicted every US recession since the 1970s. Most people have never heard of it.**

It's called the yield curve — and right now, it's doing something worth paying attention to.

Here's the basics: when you lend money to the US government, you earn interest. Lend for 2 years, earn one rate. Lend for 10 years, earn a higher rate (more time = more risk = more reward). Plot all those rates on a chart and you get the yield curve — normally a gentle upward slope.

Sometimes that slope flips.

When short-term rates climb *above* long-term rates, the curve "inverts." This sounds technical. What it actually means: bond investors are so worried about the future that they're piling into long-term bonds for safety, pushing long yields down — while short-term rates stay elevated because the Fed hasn't moved yet. It's the bond market collectively saying: *things are going to get worse before they get better.*

The 2Y-10Y spread — the gap between 2-year and 10-year Treasury yields — inverted in 2022. It stayed inverted for the longest stretch in recorded history.

Here's the part people get backwards: the recession doesn't usually arrive *during* the inversion. It tends to arrive *after* the curve un-inverts — when the Fed starts cutting rates to respond to a slowdown that's already underway. The un-inversion is the signal, not the inversion.

The curve has largely un-inverted over the past several months.

That doesn't mean recession is certain. It means the chart that has the best historical track record is currently in the part of its pattern that precedes downturns. Worth knowing.

There is a chart that has preceded every US recession since the 1970s. It doesn't make headlines the way jobs numbers or CPI does. Most people have never heard the term. But professional investors watch it more closely than almost anything else.

It's called the yield curve.

Here's what it is: when you lend money to the US government by buying a Treasury bond, you earn interest. The rate you earn depends on how long you're lending for. A 2-year Treasury pays one rate. A 10-year Treasury pays a different rate. A 30-year Treasury pays another. Plot all those rates on a chart — maturity on the X axis, yield on the Y axis — and the line you draw is the yield curve.

Under normal conditions, it slopes gently upward. Longer loans carry more uncertainty, so lenders demand more compensation. A 10-year yield should be higher than a 2-year yield. That's just logic.

Sometimes the curve flips.

When short-term rates climb above long-term rates, economists say the curve has "inverted." The 2-year yield is higher than the 10-year yield. On a chart, the line tilts downward instead of up. It looks like a small technical anomaly. What it actually represents is a significant signal from the bond market.

Here's the mechanism: when investors become worried about the economic outlook, they buy long-term bonds. A lot of them. Buying bonds pushes their prices up and their yields down — that's just how bond math works. At the same time, short-term rates stay elevated because the Federal Reserve hasn't cut yet. The gap between short and long yields narrows. If the pessimism is strong enough, the gap turns negative. The curve inverts.

In other words: an inverted yield curve is the bond market collectively pricing in economic trouble ahead — before the data confirms it.

The 2-year/10-year spread, the most watched version of this, inverted in mid-2022 and stayed inverted for the longest stretch in recorded history. For over two years, the bond market was persistently signaling caution.

**Here's the part people get backwards:** the recession doesn't typically arrive *during* the inversion. It tends to arrive *after* the curve un-inverts — when short rates fall back below long rates because the Fed has started cutting in response to a slowdown that's already underway. The un-inversion is actually the warning signal, not the inversion itself.

The 2Y-10Y spread has largely returned to positive territory over the past several months.

That doesn't mean a recession is inevitable. Economies are complex, the Fed has more tools than it did in prior cycles, and yield curve signals aren't a stopwatch — the lag between signal and event can vary widely. But it does mean that the chart with the most consistent historical track record is currently in the phase of its pattern that has historically preceded downturns.

Worth having in your vocabulary the next time you see a headline about Treasury yields.
