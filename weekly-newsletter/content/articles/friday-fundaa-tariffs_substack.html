Friday Fundaa | Who actually pays a tariff? (Spoiler: you already know)

A short weekly moment of "huh, didn't know that" from the world of markets and money.

"Liberation Day" was a year ago. The tariffs are still here. The promise was that other countries would pay. Your grocery receipt has thoughts on that.

Here's what's actually going on — and why the way tariffs are talked about in public is almost perfectly backwards.

First, a fact most people get wrong: a tariff is not a bill sent to a foreign government. It's an import tax collected at the US border — paid by the American company importing the goods, not the factory in Guangzhou or the farm in Mexico. The foreign exporter gets their agreed price. The US government collects the tax. The importer makes up the difference the only way a business can: by raising prices.

The math is straightforward. A $25 pair of sneakers plus a 46% tariff equals a $36 pair of sneakers. The factory in Vietnam still got their $25. The US government collected $11. You paid $36. Nobody in Hanoi wrote a cheque. You did.

"But can't companies just absorb it? Take the hit on their margins?"

Sometimes, for a while, on some goods. A large retailer might eat a small tariff increase on a high-volume item to stay competitive. But margins in retail, electronics, and apparel are already thin — we're talking 3–8% net profit margins for most consumer goods companies. A 25–46% tariff is not a rounding error they can quietly absorb. It's a structural cost that has to go somewhere. And it goes to you.

Economists call this "tariff pass-through" — the percentage of the import tax that ends up in the final retail price. Studies on the 2018–2019 tariff rounds found pass-through of roughly 80–100% for consumer goods. When Levi's explicitly said "tariffs" in their earnings call to explain a $10 price increase, they were telling you the pass-through happened. Bless their honesty.

Scale it up and you get your 2026 shopping list:

  • Avocados: $0.87 → $1.45/lb (90% of US avocados come from Mexico, which got a 25% tariff)
  • Bananas: 50¢ → 54¢/lb
  • Levi's jeans: +$10 a pair
  • Nike shoes: +$5–10 per pair
  • PlayStation 5: $499 → $649 after two price hikes in one year

Yale Budget Lab estimated the total across all the active tariffs: roughly $2,400 more per household per year. Not a line item. Background noise on everything.

"But what about the other country? Don't they suffer too?"

A bit — but in a completely different way, and this distinction matters. If prices get high enough, US buyers switch suppliers. That hurts the original exporter's revenue. But losing a customer is not the same as paying a tax. The tariff money doesn't flow to China. It flows to the US Treasury. Via your wallet. China's loss is in sales volume, not in dollars handed over to Washington.

Then they retaliate. China responded to Liberation Day with 125% tariffs on US exports. American soybean farmers, pork producers, and manufacturers who'd spent years building relationships in China suddenly couldn't sell into their biggest overseas market. The bill circled back — hitting producers in farm states this time, not just consumers at Target.

This is what headlines almost always miss: a tariff isn't a single economic event. It's a sequence.

Step one: US places tariff → US consumers pay more for imports. Step two: trading partner retaliates → US exporters lose revenue. Step three: affected domestic industries lobby for relief → exemptions, subsidies, or bailouts get funded from somewhere. Step four: supply chains slowly reroute — some manufacturing shifts to non-tariffed countries, but this takes years and raises costs in the interim. What begins as "making other countries pay" tends to unfold as a tax on domestic consumption, a hit to domestic exporters, a government subsidy bill, and a multi-year supply chain restructuring — all at once.

There's one group that does benefit, in the short run: domestic producers who compete with the tariffed imports. If Chinese steel gets taxed, US steel mills get a pricing advantage. This is why tariffs have political supporters — the winners are concentrated and vocal (a few thousand steelworkers), the losers are diffuse and quiet (every manufacturer who buys steel). The economics are messy but the politics make sense.

Here's the part that actually matters for you: the higher prices you're seeing right now on groceries, electronics, and clothing are not random inflation. They're a policy outcome — one that was presented as costing other countries money, but which works through a mechanism that routes most of the cost through your wallet first. The foreign country watches. Sometimes loses some export revenue. And sometimes charges back.

The tariff is a tax on your shopping cart. That's not a political opinion. It's how the plumbing works.

Now you know.
