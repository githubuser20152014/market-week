The Brief

Night eleven of U.S.-Iran strikes on the Strait of Hormuz and Wall Street rallied anyway, because nothing says risk-on like oil and gold climbing in the same breath as stocks. That's not a clean signal, that's two asset classes quietly hedging a war nobody's calling a war yet. Futures are pulling back this morning, Nasdaq off 0.6%, because even the optimists want to hear what Alphabet says before doubling down.

Yesterday's session closed green across the board: the S&P 500 added 0.9% to 7,509, the Nasdaq climbed 1.3% to 25,837, the Russell 2000 led the pack at 1.5% to 2,987, and the Dow tacked on 0.7% to 52,224, small-cap outperformance being the closest thing to a confidence vote this market can muster. WTI crude surged 2.6% to $87.08 and gold added 1.3% to $4,122.50, both cheering the same war that stocks apparently decided to ignore. The 10-year yield ticked up 3 basis points to 4.63% and the USD Index shrugged at 101.17, because somebody has to stay boring while everything else catches fire.

Two things drove the tape. Semiconductors led the bounce as traders positioned ahead of Alphabet's Q2 report after the close, Nvidia and Micron clawing back last week's beating like nothing happened. The bigger story is the Strait of Hormuz escalation, night eleven of U.S.-Iran strikes that have rerouted more than 30 commercial vessels and pushed Brent above $92, WTI to $87. Secretary Rubio called Iran "not serious" about peace talks, which is diplomatic-speak for buckle up. The strait carries 20% of the world's oil through what is functionally a one-lane highway, so even a partial blockage sends insurance premiums and spot prices sprinting ahead of any actual supply loss. Gold doesn't rally with stocks in a normal risk-on session, and it did yesterday anyway, so this isn't optimism. It's a hedge wearing a rally's clothes.

What it means for you

For ETF investors, the energy trade has actual plumbing behind it, not just vibes. XLE and XOP benefit directly as long as WTI holds above $85. Gold at $4,122 is not a bubble call, it is a Hormuz hedge, and GLD stays a core position while diplomacy remains theoretical. Jamie Dimon's warning that markets are underpricing risk is worth remembering right about now: at S&P 7,509, 10-year yields at 4.63%, and an actual shooting war disrupting energy supply, there isn't much room left for Alphabet to disappoint tonight. Soft capex guidance or wobbly AI commentary and QQQ gives back its week fast. Defense names (ITA, XAR) are the quiet, boring way to play the same war without touching a barrel of oil.

Going into today, S&P futures are off 0.2% to 7,531 and Nasdaq futures are down 0.6% to 29,152, tech getting cold feet before its own earnings. The Hang Seng dropped 1.0% overnight on China's Q2 GDP miss at 4.3% against a 4.5% consensus, a demand-side headwind for a commodity rally that's currently running on supply fear alone. The ECB decides rates Thursday, expected to hold at 2.25%, but Lagarde's September signaling is the real event for European bonds. None of that matters as much as Alphabet's earnings after the close: capex trajectory and AI monetization commentary either validate this whole semiconductor bounce or torch it by tomorrow's open.

The One Trade
GLD — Long
Gold rising 1.3% in a session where equities also rallied is the clearest signal of a genuine geopolitical bid: when both move up together, gold is not a hedge, it is a primary trade driven by the Hormuz escalation.
Confirms: GLD holds above $382 through the first hour of trading and does not fade as tech opens lower. A sustained bid in gold while Nasdaq futures stay negative confirms the Hormuz premium is driving the asset independently.
Kill switch: Secretary Rubio or the State Department announces a framework for Iran ceasefire talks before Thursday's UN Security Council session, removing the geopolitical floor and sending GLD back below $378.
