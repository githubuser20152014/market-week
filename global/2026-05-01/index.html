Risk Appetite Returns as Dollar Loses Ground

The week's dominant story was a broad risk-on rotation driven by a weakening US dollar, down 1.13% to 98.21, which unlocked buying across equities, commodities, and select currencies simultaneously. The greenback's slide was not accompanied by falling yields. The 10-year Treasury rose 7 basis points to 4.38% and the 30-year held near 4.97%, meaning the dollar sold off on factors beyond rate differentials alone. That combination, falling dollar with rising yields, points to a credibility or confidence dynamic rather than a simple macro softening.

WTI crude oil surged 6.63% to close at $101.94, breaching the psychologically significant $100 level and registering a weekly range from $94.59 to $110.93. Natural gas added 10.76%. Energy is now leading commodities with force. Gold, however, fell 1.65%, a notable divergence from the typical dollar-weakness playbook. When oil surges and gold retreats in the same week, the market is pricing real-asset inflation, not safe-haven fear.

Equity Markets

US equities posted the clearest gains of the week. Nasdaq rose 1.27% to 25,114, outpacing the S&P 500's 1.08% gain and the Dow's 0.79% advance. The Russell 2000 added 0.87%, which is constructive but trails large-cap tech. The growth factor is leading again. With the 10-year at 4.38%, that is not the rate environment that typically favors long-duration tech multiples, yet QQQ outperformed. Either earnings expectations are carrying the weight, or liquidity is flowing into the highest-volume names as a default risk-on expression.

Europe disappointed. Euro Stoxx 50 barely moved, up 0.05%, the DAX added a modest 0.31%, the FTSE 100 slipped 0.14%, and the CAC 40 fell 0.64%. The euro's marginal 0.15% gain against the dollar did little to catalyze European equity buyers. Asia-Pacific was similarly uninspiring: Nikkei added 0.18%, Hang Seng 0.17%, and the ASX 200 fell 0.65%. MSCI EM led the region with a 0.36% gain, benefiting from dollar softness, but the move was thin. The divergence between US and international equities widened this week, a signal that global capital is not yet rotating out of American large-caps.

Currency Markets

The dollar index fell 1.13% to 98.21, its sharpest weekly decline in recent memory and the clearest macro signal of the week. Every major currency gained against the greenback. The yen led with a 1.61% advance, followed by the Australian dollar up 0.81% and the Swiss franc up 0.63%. Sterling added 0.45% while the euro gained a thin 0.15%. The yen's outperformance is notable given that Japan has been a persistent laggard in this dollar-strength cycle. A yen bounce of this magnitude, especially alongside rising US yields, warrants watching as a potential signal of Bank of Japan positioning or unwinding of yen-funded carry trades.

For globally diversified ETF investors, dollar weakness at this scale is a direct return tailwind on unhedged international exposure. Every percentage point the dollar falls adds roughly equivalent return to unhedged positions in EWG, EWJ, or EEM when translated back to US dollars. If dollar softness continues, the case for unhedged international equity ETFs strengthens materially.

Commodities & Metals

WTI crude oil closed at $101.94, up 6.63% on the week, with an intraweek high of $110.93. That range is extraordinary. A $16 weekly spread in crude signals a market repricing supply risk, not just demand optimism. Natural gas compounded the energy story with a 10.76% weekly gain to $2.78. Energy commodities are now flashing an inflationary pulse that fixed income markets are already acknowledging through higher yields.

The gold move cuts against the narrative. Gold fell 1.65% to $4,629.90 in a week when the dollar weakened more than 1%. That is an unusual divergence. Gold typically rallies when the dollar falls. The selloff suggests either profit-taking after a strong prior run, or that real yields rising fast enough are compressing the gold premium. Silver held better, up 0.92% to $75.95, maintaining its industrial-metal character. Watch the gold-oil ratio: it compressed sharply this week, which historically signals a shift from financial-fear positioning toward real-economy-inflation positioning.

The market signals, especially the oil spike, dollar decline, and yield rise, suggest the week was driven by supply-side or geopolitical catalysts rather than scheduled macro data surprises. The VIX closing at 16.99, down 11.56% on the week from an intraweek high of 19.43, tells a clear story: something spooked markets mid-week and then resolved. The VIX spike to 19.43 followed by a close below 17 is a compression pattern that often precedes directional follow-through in equities. The resolution of whatever drove the volatility spike appears to have been the catalyst for the equity rally into the close.

Next Week: What to Watch

No scheduled events were provided for the coming week. Given the unresolved tensions in the data, three dynamics deserve attention. First, whether crude oil sustains above $100 or mean-reverts after the spike. A hold above $100 resets inflation expectations upward and pressures the Fed's narrative. Second, whether the dollar continues lower or bounces from 98: a break below 97 would be a structural signal for international equity exposure. Third, whether Nasdaq can hold its gains with the 10-year at 4.38% and rising. If yields push toward 4.50% next week, the growth-factor trade faces a genuine test.

  • GLD warrants a reduced position or cautious stance after its 1.65% decline in a dollar-down week. The gold-oil ratio compression argues for rotating inflation-hedge exposure from gold toward energy.
