Hormuz Blockade Rewrites the Global Energy Order

The dominant story this week was not a data release or a central bank meeting. It was the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and the UAE's formal exit from OPEC, two events that together represent the most significant structural break in global energy markets in decades. WTI Crude surged 6.6% to close at $101.94, having touched $110.93 intraday, while Brent peaked at $126/bbl mid-week before retreating as hedge funds locked in profits. The UAE's departure, effective May 1, liberates ADNOC's $150 billion capacity expansion from quota constraints and fractures the Saudi-led pricing mechanism that has governed oil markets since the 1960s.

The Federal Reserve held rates at 3.5%–3.75% in what is expected to be Jerome Powell's final meeting, while the Bank of England held at 3.75% but split 8-4, the widest dissent in years. Both decisions reflect the same impossible arithmetic: war-driven energy inflation is pushing headline CPI higher while slowing global growth pulls the other way. March PCE inflation printed at 3.5% year-over-year, almost entirely attributable to the Hormuz Premium. Kevin Warsh's pending confirmation as the next Fed Chair signals a harder line on price stability, which matters for duration and rate-sensitive equity valuations going forward.

The counterintuitive signal this week: equities shrugged. The S&P 500 closed up 1.08% and the Nasdaq gained 1.27%, even as crude hit a four-year high and inflation expectations deteriorated. The market is betting that AI-driven productivity, evidenced by strong tech earnings, can outrun stagflation. That is a high-conviction bet with a narrow margin for error. Personal savings sitting at a multi-year low of 3.6% leaves the consumer with little buffer if energy costs keep compounding.

This Week’s Economic Events

The formal economic calendar was sparse this week, but the macro data that did cross was significant. March PCE inflation printed at 3.5% year-over-year, almost entirely attributed to energy costs embedded in the Hormuz Premium, and personal savings dropped to 3.6%, a multi-year low that constrains the consumer's ability to absorb further price increases. The Fed's decision to hold at 3.5%–3.75% was widely expected, but the FOMC statement's explicit reference to oil volatility and the Iran conflict as structural obstacles to returning to 2% inflation was a meaningful shift in language. The BoE's 8-4 split hold at 3.75% is similarly revealing: four members voted to cut, signaling that the growth risk is being taken seriously even as energy inflation complicates the picture. The World Bank's warning of a potential 25% surge in average energy prices this year is not a forecast that fits neatly into current consensus rate-path models.

Next Week: What to Watch

The dominant variable next week is whether the Strait of Hormuz blockade shows any signs of resolution or escalation. Any credible diplomatic signal from the Trump administration or Iran would reprice crude sharply lower and relieve the pressure on inflation expectations that is currently suppressing bond prices and complicating the Fed's path. On the scheduled calendar, watch for any commentary from Kevin Warsh as his Senate confirmation process advances: his public positioning on rate policy will set the tone for how markets price the Fed's next move. European PMI data and any UK economic prints will test whether the BoE's four dissenters were ahead of the curve on growth deterioration. The UAE's first week operating as an independent energy producer outside OPEC quotas may produce surprise production announcements that move crude either direction.

Global Investor Positioning
  • UNG (Long): Natural gas is the cleanest expression of the Hormuz supply shock. The +10.76% weekly move has fundamental backing from blocked LNG routes and is not dependent on further escalation to sustain elevated prices.
  • USO (Long, trimmed): WTI above $100 with a fractured OPEC and a closed chokepoint justifies holding energy exposure, but the $94-to-$110 weekly range signals high volatility. Size accordingly and watch for diplomatic headlines.
  • QQQ (Hold/Trim): The AI earnings narrative drove the Nasdaq's +1.27% gain, but OpenAI revenue shortfalls flagged mid-week suggest the AI spending cycle is not universally delivering. Trim into strength rather than adding at these levels.
  • FXY (Long): The yen's +1.61% gain reflects BoJ intervention at 160, a credible line in the sand. If the dollar continues its broad slide and the energy shock forces further BoJ action, FXY offers both a safe-haven and a mean-reversion trade.
  • TLT (Avoid/Underweight): The 10-year yield rose 7 basis points to 4.38% with the 30-year at 4.97%. With PCE at 3.5%, Warsh incoming, and energy inflation unresolved, duration is not a safe place to hide. Stay short duration or flat.
