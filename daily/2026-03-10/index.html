The Brief

The dominant mood is a geopolitical relief rally with a sharp commodity repricing — oil is getting crushed while gold holds firm near record highs, a split that tells you this isn't simple risk-on. The standout cross-asset signal: WTI down nearly 7% while gold adds another 2% and the dollar continues its multi-week slide, suggesting markets are pricing a de-escalation in Iran/Middle East tensions but retaining a macro hedge. Futures are essentially flat, meaning the equity upside from yesterday's broad rally is already baked in — the open will be a tell on whether rotation into beaten-down sectors can extend.

US equities closed broadly higher yesterday — S&P 500 +0.83% to 6,795.99, Nasdaq +1.38% to 22,695.95, Russell 2000 +1.12%, and the Dow +0.50% to 47,740.80. The macro backdrop was equally active: gold surged to $5,190.80 (+1.95%), WTI crude collapsed 6.86% to $88.27, the 10Y yield barely moved (4.136%, +0.3bps), and the USD Index fell to 98.68 (-0.50%) — its weakest level in years.

The dominant driver was shifting Iran-war risk. Markets priced a meaningful reduction in Strait of Hormuz disruption risk as Trump comments on Iran diplomacy circulated — think of WTI's crash as the market unwinding the 'premium' it had stuffed into crude like an insurance payout nobody needs. The dollar's continued slide amplifies the gold trade: a weaker dollar is like a tail wind on an already moving train for gold, which is now pricing in a combination of geopolitical hedging, real-rate suppression, and de-dollarization. Bonds never really flinched — 10Y yields barely moved despite equities rallying, telling you fixed-income investors aren't convinced this is a clean all-clear.

What it means for you

For ETF investors, the energy trade is back on the table but inverted — the oil crash punishes (XLE, XOP) while the broader market relief benefits risk assets. Falling oil is essentially a tax cut for consumers, which favors discretionary (XLY) and industrials (XLI). Gold (GLD) near all-time highs with the dollar still in freefall is the cleanest momentum trade in the complex — the path of least resistance is higher until the Fed or dollar reversal intervenes. If you're in long-duration bonds (TLT), the flat yield reaction is a yellow light: equities rallied but bonds didn't follow, so don't chase duration here. International developed (EFA, EWJ) and EM (EEM) ETFs should benefit from the weak dollar and APAC's strong overnight close.

Going into today, S&P futures are essentially flat at 6,800 — the overnight gains are priced. APAC was a standout, with KOSPI surging +5.35%, Nikkei +2.88%, and Hang Seng +2.17%, suggesting global risk appetite is genuinely elevated. European indices are up 1.5–2.4% in early trade. The key swing factor today is oil price stability — if WTI finds a floor and bounces, energy shorts will cover aggressively; if it continues sliding through $87, it signals demand destruction fear more than geopolitical relief, which could spook broader markets. Watch for any Iran headline or Warsh Fed confirmation update as a potential volatility trigger.
