The Brief

The dominant mood is geopolitical risk pricing — oil is surging on Hormuz blockade fears while equities are fractured, with the Nasdaq eking out a gain as megacap tech holds but the Dow and small caps bleed. The critical cross-asset signal is crude exploding +4.5% in futures while the dollar is whipsawing: yen weakening hard (+2.5% USD/JPY) and EUR/GBP selling off sharply, suggesting coordinated dollar strength that complicates the commodity surge narrative. APAC sold off broadly (Nikkei -0.74%, Hang Seng -1.23%, KOSPI -1.21%) and US futures are red across the board with Dow futures down -1.7%, pointing to a risk-off open with energy as the lone standout.

Yesterday's US session ended in a split verdict: the Nasdaq closed up +0.35% at 22,902 while the S&P 500 slipped -0.11% to 6,816, the Dow shed -0.56% to 47,916, and the Russell 2000 fell -0.22% to 2,630 — a classic large-cap tech holding pattern while the broader market leaked. Gold closed at $4,728 and WTI crude settled at $104.36. The 10-year yield held at 4.32%, and the USD Index sat at 99.0 — still soft in structural terms but showing intraday muscle in FX crosses.

The driver is unambiguous: Strait of Hormuz risk is repricing the entire energy complex. WTI crude futures are now up over +4.5% to $104.36 as headlines confirm blockade fears and the Trump administration simultaneously threatens 50% tariffs on China while reports surface of potential US arms shipments to Iran — a geopolitical cocktail that markets are struggling to price rationally. Think of it like a supply shock layered on top of a trade war: oil goes up, but the consumer and industrial economy faces a simultaneous cost squeeze and demand destruction. That's stagflation math, and bonds never bought the rally — yields at 4.32% aren't compensating for the new risk environment.

What it means for you

For investors, this is a bifurcated setup. The energy trade is back on the table — XLE and XOP benefit directly from WTI above $100, and with crude futures at $104+, the margin expansion for E&P names is significant. But the flip side is that energy input costs will crater industrials and transports (watch XLI and IYT for downside pressure). Gold at $4,728 with futures near $4,746 remains the cleanest geopolitical hedge — GLD and PHYS hold their bid even as equities wobble. TLT is the difficult one: yields at 4.32% in a stagflationary shock don't automatically rally, so long-duration Treasuries (TLT) are a crowded and complicated safe-haven trade right now. Defensive dividend plays (XLU, VPU) and broad commodity exposure (DJP, GSG) look better positioned than bonds in this scenario.

Going into today, S&P futures are down -0.55% at 6,816 and Dow futures are off -1.7% — the open will be soft, with European markets absent due to Easter Monday providing no liquidity offset. The APAC selloff (-1.2% across Korea and Hong Kong) confirms the geopolitical premium is being applied globally. Today's swing factor is the Goldman Sachs Q1 earnings print — if GS beats, it could provide a brief floor for financials (XLF) and temper the sell-off. But if crude holds above $104 and any escalation headline drops intraday, the Dow's -500 point futures gap could widen fast. Watch the $6,800 level on S&P cash as the first technical line in the sand.
