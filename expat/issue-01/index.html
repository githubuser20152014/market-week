About This Issue
Built in Public

I've been doing this research for myself.

Not theoretically. I'm planning the move — 401k, brokerage account, and all. What I discovered is that nobody is publishing a clean financial primer for this situation.

The resources that exist are for the ultra-wealthy, the digital nomad, or someone trying to escape the system entirely. I'm none of those things. I just want to live somewhere else without getting the finances wrong.

The American Expat Investor is what I needed when I started: one country, one financial mechanism, one pre-departure checklist. Built in public. Shared as I go.

This month: Portugal — the D7 visa, the real cost of living, banking as an American, and what FBAR means for your first foreign account.

Section 1 · The Portugal File
Why Americans Are Going There

The weather. Lisbon gets 300+ days of sun a year. Atlantic coast, mild winters. More like San Diego without the price tag. Porto is cooler, more northern European in feel, better food scene.

The cultural fit. English is widely spoken — not just in tourist corridors but in professional settings, grocery stores, among younger locals everywhere. The bureaucracy is genuinely maddening, but the day-to-day social experience isn't alienating the way it can be in other countries.

Safety. Portugal consistently ranks in the top 5 safest countries globally. For Americans who've internalized a low-grade threat awareness in certain environments, that shift in baseline feels significant the first week — then just becomes normal.

EU access — the strategic angle. A D7 visa gets you to legal residency. Residency gets you to citizenship after five years. Portuguese citizenship means an EU passport — the right to live and work anywhere in the Schengen zone.

That's not just a travel convenience. It's optionality — the ability to reconsider your next move from a position of strength.

Section 1 · Visa Pathway
The D7 Passive Income Visa

The D7 was designed for retirees but works equally well for anyone with investment income, rental income, dividends, or a pension — as long as you're not planning to take a Portuguese job.

Income threshold (2025): €870/month (~$950). Advisors recommend showing €1,200–1,500/month to clear the consulate's informal comfort zone. Social Security, brokerage dividends, or rental income may qualify you on paper. The question is documentation.

Application Steps

① Gather documents — income proof, criminal background check, Portuguese address, health insurance valid in Portugal

② Apply at Portuguese consulate in your US jurisdiction (Boston, NYC, DC, SF, or Miami)

③ Enter Portugal on D7 entry visa → apply for residency permit at AIMA within four months

④ First permit: two years, renewable. After five years: permanent residency or citizenship eligible.

Timeline: 3–6 months minimum. Hire a local immigration lawyer (~€500–1,000). Worth every euro.

⚠ NHR is closed. The Non-Habitual Resident tax regime ended for new applicants Dec 31, 2023. NHR 2.0 (IFICI) targets R&D/startup workers only — D7 passive income residents do not qualify. Your income is taxed at Portugal's progressive rates: 14.5%–48%. Any article describing NHR as an active Portugal benefit is out of date.

Section 1 · Banking
Banking as an American Abroad

When you update your address with your US bank to a foreign address, many institutions will freeze your account, restrict online access, or close it entirely. This has happened to people mid-move, with rent due in Portugal and a frozen account back home.

The fix: open expat-friendly accounts before you leave.

Charles Schwab International Account

The default recommendation in every expat community for good reason. No foreign transaction fees. Unlimited ATM fee reimbursements globally. Functions as a checking account you can use anywhere in the world.

Interactive Brokers (IBKR)

Primarily a brokerage, but offers a cash management account. Widely used by expats who need continued access to US markets. More flexibility than most on EU residency.

Local Portuguese Bank

You'll need one for rent, utilities, and AIMA. NovoBanco, Millennium BCP, and Caixa Geral are common choices. Budget a few weeks and two or three in-person visits. Onboarding for foreigners is tedious — this is normal.

Section 1 · Investing
Investment Access from the EU

This is the part that surprises most American investors.

When you become an EU resident, the EU's PRIIPs regulation bars you from purchasing most US-domiciled funds. VTI, VXUS, QQQ, SPY — you cannot buy new shares in them as an EU resident. The regulation requires funds sold to EU retail investors to provide a standardized Key Information Document. US-domiciled ETFs don't provide that document.

The standard workaround: keep your US brokerage accounts open from before you leave. Holdings you already own can generally be maintained and sold. The restriction is on new purchases.

IBKR is more flexible than most — depending on account structure, some users report continued trading access. Get clarity from IBKR directly, in writing, before you move.

What EU Residents Can Buy

UCITS ETFs — EU-domiciled equivalents. Vanguard FTSE All-World (VWRA), iShares MSCI World (IWDA), and Invesco S&P 500 (SPXS) are the common substitutes. They function similarly to US equivalents but aren't the same funds.

Before you move: get your US accounts funded with the positions you want to hold long-term. Don't close anything.

Section 2 · FBAR
What Every American Abroad Must Understand

Before you open your first foreign bank account, you need to know one acronym: FBAR.

FBAR stands for Foreign Bank Account Report. The official form is FinCEN Form 114. The first thing to understand: this is not a tax form. It is not filed with the IRS. It does not trigger a payment. FBAR is a disclosure requirement administered by FinCEN — the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network — inside the US Treasury. The government wants to know the account exists. That's it.

FBAR applies to anyone who opens a bank account in another country. The checking account you'll use to pay rent in Lisbon. The account to pay AIMA fees. The European brokerage account for UCITS ETFs. All of it counts.

The rule has existed since 1970. It was barely enforced for decades. Starting around 2010, the US government began enforcing it aggressively — and has collected billions in penalties since. FBAR is not a technicality.

Section 2 · FBAR
Who This Applies To

Any US person with a financial interest in, or signature authority over, foreign financial accounts must file if the aggregate value exceeds $10,000 at any point during the calendar year.

US Person

US citizens (regardless of where you live), green card holders, and anyone meeting the IRS's substantial presence test. If you hold a US passport, you are almost certainly a US person for FBAR purposes — even if you haven't lived in the US in years.

Financial Interest or Signature Authority

Broader than ownership. You must file if you can control the account — even if the money isn't yours. Joint accounts count. If your name is on a business account in Portugal, report it. The question isn't ownership — it's control.

The Single-Day Rule
The $10,000 threshold applies to any single day, not year-end. If your accounts collectively held $12,000 on one day in July — then fell to $4,000 by December 31 — you still must file. This is where people get tripped up. They check their December 31 balance, see $4,000, and conclude they don't need to file. That's wrong.

Section 2 · FBAR
Deadlines and Requirements
Filing deadline: April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15. No action required to get the extension — it applies automatically. You still want to file by April 15 if possible, but October 15 is available without penalty.
How You File

Electronically, through the BSA E-Filing System at the FinCEN website. Not mailed. Not attached to your tax return. It is a separate filing in a separate government system. Your tax preparer can file it alongside your return as part of their workflow — but it goes to a different place than Form 1040.

What You Report

Every foreign financial account: bank accounts, savings accounts, brokerage accounts, pension accounts held in foreign institutions, and any account over which you have signature authority.

For each account: account number, institution name, country, and the maximum value during the reporting year.

Section 2 · FBAR
Penalties for Non-Compliance

Read this section twice.

Non-Willful Violations

Cases where the government believes you simply didn't know: civil penalty up to $10,000 per violation per year. "Per violation" generally means per account per year. Two foreign accounts for three years, unfiled: up to $60,000 in potential penalties before any negotiation.

Willful Violations

Cases where you knew and ignored it: penalty up to the greater of $100,000 or 50% of the account balance per violation. That is per account, per year. Criminal prosecution is also on the table.

The Enforcement Reality

Foreign banks are reporting your accounts to the US government through FATCA agreements — whether you tell FinCEN or not. The IRS has run multiple amnesty programs (OVDP and Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures) specifically because so many Americans abroad were non-compliant. The enforcement infrastructure is real.

Section 2 · FBAR
How to Stay Compliant

1. List every foreign account the moment you open it. Account number, institution name, country, date opened. Keep this in a running document. You need the maximum annual balance at year-end. Track it throughout the year and the filing takes twenty minutes.

2. Set a calendar reminder. April 15 for FBAR. October 15 as the extended deadline. Set both, set them to repeat annually, treat them as non-negotiable.

3. Work with a FBAR-aware expat CPA. Ask specifically whether they handle FBAR and FinCEN Form 114 as part of their standard expat service. If they don't file it, find someone who does.

4. If you're already behind, act now. The IRS's Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures allow non-compliant but non-willful filers to get caught up with reduced or waived penalties — but only before the IRS contacts you first.

This section is orientation only, not tax or legal advice. Requirements vary. Verify your specific situation with a qualified expat CPA before filing.

Section 3
Before You Go: The Financial Checklist

These have a sequencing logic. Open Schwab before you update your address. Get D7 income documentation in order before booking a consulate appointment. Set the FBAR reminder before you open the first foreign account, not after.

  • Notify your US bank and brokerage of your new international address
  • Open a Schwab International or IBKR account before you leave
  • Set recurring FBAR reminder: April 15 (auto-extended to Oct 15)
  • List every foreign account you'll open — track aggregate balance for FBAR
  • Confirm your 401k / IRA custodian allows an international address on file
  • Check your health insurance (ACA, employer plan, or international coverage)
  • Understand the D7 passive income requirement before applying
  • NHR is closed — original program ended 2023. NHR 2.0 (IFICI) applies to R&D/startup workers only. Most D7 applicants do not qualify.
